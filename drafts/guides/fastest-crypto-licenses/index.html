Section 2 — #1 Fastest

Georgia — 2–3 Weeks from Application to License

Georgia's National Bank of Georgia (NBG) operates a Virtual Asset Service Provider registration framework that is genuinely among the fastest in the world. From submission of a complete application package, approval typically arrives within 10–20 business days. This is not a rubber-stamp process — NBG conducts real AML/KYC review — but the scope is proportionate and the regulator is efficient.

What the Georgia VASP Registration Covers

The Georgia NBG registration authorises exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency, exchange between virtual assets, transfer of virtual assets, safekeeping and administration of virtual assets, and participation in financial services related to virtual assets. It is a real regulatory authorization, listed on the FATF-compliant framework, which matters for correspondent banking relationships.

Requirements

  • Georgian-registered legal entity (LLC or JSC) — takes 5–7 business days
  • AML/KYC policy and compliance procedures tailored to the business
  • Appointed AML Compliance Officer (can be remote for basic registrations)
  • Beneficial owner KYC packages for all UBOs with 10%+ shareholding
  • Business plan describing services, target clients, and transaction volumes
  • No minimum capital requirement (though working capital must be demonstrated)
  • Government registration fee: approximately GEL 2,000 (~€700)

Banking Options with Georgia VASP

Georgian domestic banks — TBC Bank and Bank of Georgia — are the primary banking partners for NBG-registered VASPs. Both have active crypto-friendly banking programs and understand the NBG registration framework. International banking is more difficult: European correspondent banks may accept Georgian VASPs, but the due diligence is extensive and not guaranteed.

Key Limitations

A Georgia VASP registration does not provide access to the EU single market. To serve EU-resident retail clients, you need an EU-authorized license under MiCA or national VASP frameworks. Georgia is not an EU member state and has no EU financial services passporting rights. For businesses targeting Georgian and CIS markets, or using Georgia as a starting jurisdiction before EU licensing, this is a well-priced and fast option.

Best use case: Georgia is ideal for businesses that want to start operating quickly in non-EU markets, have a clear path to EU licensing within 12–18 months, or are targeting Georgian, CIS, and Middle Eastern client bases where the NBG registration carries genuine credibility.

Section 3 — #2 Fastest

Poland KNF — 2–4 Weeks with EU Recognition

Poland's KNF (Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego) VASP registration is arguably the most valuable fast crypto license in Europe in 2026. It combines a processing time of 2–4 weeks with EU jurisdiction credibility, real banking access to European payment rails, and a clear transition path to full MiCA CASP authorization.

What's Included in the KNF VASP Registration

The Polish VASP registration covers exchange between virtual currencies and fiat currency, exchange between virtual currencies, brokering exchange of virtual currencies, and maintaining accounts enabling holders to use virtual currencies. The registered entity appears on KNF's official register, which is publicly accessible and recognized by banks and institutional counterparties across Europe.

Registration Requirements
  • Polish sp. z o.o. (LLC) — 5–10 days to incorporate
  • Polish registered address and real substance
  • AML/KYC compliance program
  • AML Officer appointed (can be non-resident)
  • Director KYC — clean criminal record required
  • UBO KYC for all beneficial owners
  • Business description and operations overview
  • Government fee: PLN 616 (~€140)
What You Get
  • Entry on KNF official VASP register
  • Legal authorization to operate in Poland
  • EU-jurisdiction entity with SEPA access
  • Accepted by most European EMIs and payment processors
  • Direct path to MiCA CASP authorization
  • Banking relationship with Polish banks (PKO BP, mBank, Santander PL)
  • Recognized by EU correspondent banking networks

EU Recognition Under MiCA Transition

Under the EU's MiCA regulation, Poland (like all EU member states) is transitioning from national VASP registrations to full MiCA CASP authorizations. Entities registered under the Polish VASP framework before the national transition deadline benefit from a grandfathering period — allowing them to continue operating while upgrading to full MiCA CASP status. This means a Polish VASP registration obtained in 2026 is not a dead-end — it is the first step on the MiCA pathway.

Banking Reality for Polish VASPs

Polish VASP-registered entities have genuine access to European banking infrastructure. PKO BP and mBank have active crypto business banking programs. European EMIs (including those in Lithuania and the UK) readily onboard Polish-registered crypto entities. SEPA EUR accounts are accessible, and some Polish banks also offer USD accounts through US correspondent relationships. This is a material advantage over offshore alternatives at similar cost.

Bottom line: For businesses that want EU jurisdiction, real banking, and a path to MiCA in the shortest possible time, Poland KNF is the clearest recommendation. The 2–4 week timeline is realistic for a well-prepared application, and the all-in cost of €8–15k is among the lowest for any EU-regulated crypto entity.
Section 4 — #3 Fastest

Slovakia — 4–6 Weeks, Zero Capital Required

Slovakia's crypto licensing framework operates through the Trade Licensing Authority (Živnostenský úrad) rather than a dedicated financial regulator. This makes it structurally simpler than most EU frameworks — the process is more similar to registering a business than applying for a financial services license — and correspondingly faster.

How the Slovak Process Works

Crypto exchange activities in Slovakia are classified as a "free trade" — meaning there is no examination, no minimum educational requirement, and no capital adequacy test at the registration stage. The applicant incorporates a Slovak s.r.o. (LLC), registers for crypto trade activity with the Trade Licensing Authority, and notifies the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) of VASP status. The AML obligations are real and enforced, but the initial registration process is genuinely fast.

Simple Process, Real Obligations

Do not mistake a simplified registration for an absence of regulation. Slovak VASPs are subject to the full scope of Slovak AML law (Act No. 297/2008) which implements the EU's 6th Anti-Money Laundering Directive. The FIU actively supervises registered VASPs and has issued administrative fines for non-compliance. You need a genuine AML program, proper customer due diligence procedures, and transaction monitoring — the registration is simple, the compliance is not optional.

  • Slovak s.r.o. incorporation — 5–10 business days
  • Trade license registration for crypto exchange activity
  • FIU notification of VASP status
  • AML/KYC compliance program (Slovak-law compliant)
  • Register of beneficial owners completed
  • Zero minimum capital requirement for trade license
  • Total government fees under €500

Limitations — No EU Passport Pre-MiCA

A Slovak trade license for crypto activities does not provide financial services passporting rights across the EU. Slovakia is an EU member state, and a Slovak entity can operate freely within Slovakia, but serving clients in Germany, France, or the Netherlands under the Slovak license alone is not straightforwardly permitted under existing national frameworks. Under full MiCA CASP authorization (which Slovakia will implement), this changes — but the trade license itself is not a passport.

For businesses primarily serving Slovak clients or using Slovakia as a low-cost EU entity while building toward full MiCA authorization, this is a practical and affordable option. For businesses that need immediate EU-wide access, Estonia or Lithuania are better choices despite the longer timelines.

Banking note: Slovak VASPs have access to Slovak domestic banks (Tatra Banka, CSOB, Slovenská Sporiteľňa) and can open EUR SEPA accounts. However, some Slovak banks remain cautious about crypto businesses. Working with a specialist who has established banking relationships in Slovakia significantly improves account opening success rates.

Section 5 — Offshore Options

Fast But Limited: SVG, Vanuatu & Seychelles

St. Vincent and the Grenadines (SVG), Vanuatu, and Seychelles consistently appear in "fast crypto license" lists. They are fast — SVG takes 2–4 weeks, Vanuatu 4–6 weeks, Seychelles 4–8 weeks. And they are cheap. But there is a reason they are cheap and fast: the regulatory depth is limited, and the consequences for banking are severe.

Why They're Fast

SVG has no crypto-specific regulatory framework — the SVG Financial Services Authority (FSA) does not regulate forex or crypto businesses, meaning what is sold as an "SVG crypto license" is largely an FSA registration for an international business company. There is no substantive regulatory review because there is no substantive regulation. Vanuatu's VFSC dealer license exists under a proper regulatory framework but is designed for a small, under-resourced regulator, which keeps processing times short. Seychelles FSA has a more developed framework but still operates at a fraction of the cost and speed of EU or UAE regulators.

Why Banks Reject Offshore Licenses
  • Correspondent banks flag high-risk jurisdictions automatically
  • FATF grey-list status (Vanuatu was grey-listed in 2022)
  • Lack of reciprocal regulatory recognition with major markets
  • Offshore entities cannot access SEPA, SWIFT easily
  • KYC on the entity itself is harder for banks to complete
  • Most EU and US correspondent banks exclude SVG VASPs entirely
When Offshore Makes Sense
  • As a secondary license while EU license is in process
  • For B2B / institutional-only operations (not retail)
  • Serving markets where offshore licenses are accepted (some APAC, LATAM)
  • When cryptocurrency-native payment rails replace traditional banking
  • For a holding structure while the operating entity gets licensed elsewhere
  • When speed to soft-launch outweighs banking limitation risks

The Banking Reality Check

The most common failure pattern we see: a startup obtains an SVG or Vanuatu license because it is fast and cheap, then spends 6–12 months failing to open bank accounts, eventually concludes they need an EU license anyway, and has wasted both time and money. The fast license was not cheap if you factor in the months of failed banking attempts and delayed revenue.

If you are considering an offshore license, budget for a plan B: either a crypto-native payment infrastructure (stablecoins, crypto payment processors), or a parallel EU licensing application from day one. Do not assume the bank account will follow the license automatically — for offshore entities, it frequently does not.

SVG-specific warning: The SVG FSA has explicitly stated that it does not regulate forex and crypto businesses. The FSA registration that is sold as an "SVG crypto license" does not confer regulatory authorization — it is a company registration. Many payment processors and banks have specifically added SVG to their exclusion lists. Proceed with full awareness of this reality.

Section 6 — The Banking Question

Fast vs Credible: What Banks Actually Think

Getting a crypto license is only half the problem. The other half is opening a bank account — and the two are not equally difficult. A license that takes 3 weeks to obtain may take 12 months to bank. A license that takes 12 weeks to obtain may have a bank account within 30 days of approval. Speed at the regulator and speed at the bank are different variables.

The Correspondent Banking Hierarchy

When a crypto business approaches a bank, that bank's correspondent banking partners are the real decision-makers. Correspondent banks provide the USD and EUR clearing that makes international payments possible. Most major correspondent banks (JPMorgan, Deutsche Bank, Citibank, BNP Paribas) have specific crypto business policies that flow down to the local banks they work with.

These policies typically accept: EU MiCA-authorized CASPs, established EU VASP registrations (Poland, Estonia, Lithuania, Bulgaria), UAE VARA-licensed entities, Singapore MAS-licensed entities. They typically reject or flag for enhanced due diligence: offshore VASPs (SVG, Seychelles, Vanuatu), entities from FATF grey-list countries, newly registered entities with no operating history.

A Real-World Banking Case

Consider two crypto businesses starting operations in early 2026. Company A chooses a Seychelles FSA license — 6 weeks, $12k all-in. Company B chooses Estonian FIU — 6 weeks, €18k all-in. Company A spends 8 months approaching banks across Europe, Asia, and the Caribbean. Four reject immediately. Two request enhanced due diligence, then decline. One opens an account with a $100k minimum balance and $50k monthly fee. Company B opens an account with LHV Bank (Estonia) within 4 weeks of license issuance. It accesses SEPA in week 6. By month 8, when Company A is still struggling with banking, Company B is processing client transactions.

The total cost difference at month 12: Company A spent more time, more advisory fees, more management attention, and significantly more money — despite the cheaper license.

Section 7 — Advanced Strategy

The 2-Step Strategy: Fast Now, Credible Later

For businesses that genuinely cannot wait 3–6 months for a premium EU license but also understand the banking limitations of pure offshore options, there is a structured approach that experienced operators use: obtain a fast license in a credible jurisdiction to start operating, while simultaneously pursuing the more comprehensive license that will support long-term growth.

This is not a workaround or a grey-area approach — it is standard practice among professional crypto operators. The key is choosing the right combination of jurisdictions and maintaining compliance throughout.

1
Months 1–2
Fast License — Poland or Georgia
Incorporate in Poland or Georgia (depending on target market — EU vs. CIS/MENA) and obtain the fast VASP registration. For Poland, this takes 2–4 weeks from complete application. Build your AML/KYC program, appoint your compliance officer, and begin banking conversations immediately after approval. Do not delay — banking takes time even with a credible license. Start generating revenue in your initial market while the EU banking infrastructure comes online.
2
Months 2–6
Begin EU License Application in Parallel
While operating under the fast license, begin the application for your target long-term license — typically Estonia FIU (for EU credibility and excellent banking), Lithuania (for EMI integration possibilities), or full MiCA CASP authorization once your national regulator begins accepting applications. The preparation work overlaps significantly: the AML program you built for Poland or Georgia forms the foundation for your EU application. This parallel approach means you arrive at EU authorization without a revenue gap.
3
Months 12–18
Transition to Full EU Authorization
By month 12–18, your comprehensive EU license is in place. You now have full MiCA CASP passporting rights (or strong national VASP status), excellent banking relationships, and 12+ months of operating history that makes future banking and partnership conversations much easier. The fast license served its purpose: it got you operational while the real infrastructure was built. Some operators maintain both licenses permanently — the fast license for specific markets, the EU license for European operations.

When the 2-Step Strategy Works

This approach works best when: you have a product ready to launch but need a license quickly, your initial target market accepts a Poland or Georgia VASP, you have the capital to maintain two licensing processes simultaneously (typically €20–40k total), and you have specialist advisors who can manage both applications efficiently without duplication of effort.

When It Does Not Work

The 2-step strategy fails when the fast license is an offshore registration (SVG, Vanuatu) with no banking, or when the operator believes the fast license is "good enough" and deprioritizes the EU application once operations begin. Regulatory inertia is real — businesses that intend to upgrade their license often do not, because the immediate commercial pressures of running the business crowd out licensing projects. Build the EU application into your funding and management plan from the start.

Capital planning: The 2-step strategy requires capital for two licensing processes running in parallel. Budget approximately €20–35k for the combination of a fast EU registration (Poland/Slovakia/Georgia) plus a parallel EU VASP application (Estonia/Lithuania). This is significantly more efficient than failing with an offshore license and starting the EU process from scratch 12 months later.
