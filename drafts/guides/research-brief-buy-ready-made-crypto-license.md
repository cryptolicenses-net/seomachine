## Core thesis (every claim must trace to a source)

A "ready-made" (a.k.a. shelf or pre-licensed) crypto company is an existing, already-incorporated and in many cases already-registered/authorised entity that is sold to a new owner so they can start operating faster than building from scratch. The legitimate benefit is **speed and a track record**. The decisive caveat: in virtually every credible jurisdiction, **transferring ownership of a licensed crypto company triggers a regulator change-of-control / qualifying-holding approval**, plus a **fit-and-proper re-assessment** of the new owners. So it is NOT an instant shortcut around vetting, and the buyer can **inherit hidden liabilities and AML history**.

## Verified facts + sources

### 1. Change of control needs prior regulator approval (UK / FCA)
- From **11 August 2022**, a person acquiring "control" of an FCA-registered cryptoasset business (directly or indirectly) must get **prior FCA approval**; failure to obtain approval when required is now a **criminal offence**.
- The change lets the FCA run a **"fit and proper" assessment** of the proposed controller and **object** to the transaction. A 50%+ investment requires a business plan.
- FCA Change in Control team has had significant backlogs/delays.
- Source: White & Case, "Change in control regime for cryptoasset businesses" — https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/change-control-regime-cryptoasset-businesses

### 2. MiCA qualifying holding / change of control (EU CASPs)
- A **qualifying holding** = **10%** or more of voting share capital/voting rights, or below 10% if it confers significant influence.
- **Non-objection** from the competent authority is required for acquisitions reaching/crossing **20%, 30% or 50%**, or that result in **control** of the CASP.
- Competent authority assesses **suitability of the proposed acquirer and financial soundness** under **MiCA Article 84**.
- **Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/414** = RTS on the detailed info needed to assess a proposed acquisition of a qualifying holding in a CASP.
- Joint ESMA/EBA Guidelines on suitability of shareholders/members with qualifying holdings (EBA/GL/2024/09).
- Sources:
  - Latham & Watkins MiCA tracker — https://www.lw.com/en/markets-in-crypto-assets-regulation-tracker/mica-requirements-casps
  - A&O Shearman, "MiCAR under the microscope Part 6: Acquisition of qualifying holdings" — https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/micar-under-the-microscope-acquisition-of-qualifying-holdings-of-casps-and-art-issuers
  - ESMA/EBA Joint GL on suitability — https://www.esma.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2024-06/Joint_GL_suitability_members_management_body_and_QH_MiCA.pdf
  - Springlex MiCA Art. 84 — https://www.springlex.eu/en/packages/mica/mica-regulation/article-84/

### 3. Licences are non-transferable; you buy the company, not a tradeable licence (Estonia)
- Estonian crypto licences are **indefinite, non-transferable**, registered office must be in Estonia.
- FIU supervised legacy VASP licences until ~**1 July 2026**, after which oversight shifts to the **FSA** under MiCA / Crypto Asset Market Act.
- Implication for "ready-made": you acquire the *entity* (and its corporate history), not a portable permit. Corporate transformations/mergers governed by national law alongside MiCA.
- Source: Estonian Ministry of Finance FAQ — https://www.fin.ee/en/faq-how-will-new-estonian-draft-legislation-affect-virtual-assets-and-crypto ; Charltons Quantum Estonia overview — https://charltonsquantum.com/estonia-virtual-assets-regulation/

### 4. FATF — beneficial owners / management must meet fit-and-proper standards
- FATF Recommendation 15 + VA/VASP guidance: VASPs must be licensed/registered and regulators apply criteria to **beneficial owners and senior management**; this re-applies on a change of beneficial ownership.
- Source: FATF Updated Guidance for a Risk-Based Approach to VAs and VASPs — https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/Fatfrecommendations/Guidance-rba-virtual-assets-2021.html

### 5. Honest framing of "buying" a license
- Industry/legal commentary confirms: you cannot simply "buy a license" as a tradeable object; you buy a company that holds a registration/authorisation, and the regulator must approve the ownership change.
- Source: cryptolicense.io "Can You Really Buy a Crypto License?" — https://cryptolicense.io/en/can-you-really-buy-a-crypto-license-heres-what-that-actually-means-3/ (treat as opinion/secondary, cross-reference only — not a figure source)

## Risks to cover (balanced)
1. **Regulator change-of-control approval still required** — no skipping vetting.
2. **Fit-and-proper re-assessment** of new UBOs/directors.
3. **Hidden liabilities** — tax, debts, prior contracts, litigation.
4. **AML/compliance history** — prior SARs, supervisory findings, sanctions exposure, "dirty" customer base.
5. **MiCA re-authorisation / passport risk** — legacy registration may not equal a full MiCA CASP authorisation; transitional periods ending mid-2026.
6. **Non-transferable licences** — entity acquisition only; some regimes can revoke on control change.
7. **Banking de-risking** — partners may drop a re-owned shell.
8. **Due diligence cost/time can erase the speed benefit.**

## Legitimate uses
- Genuine speed where a clean, well-documented entity exists and regulator pre-clears the buyer.
- Inheriting bank relationships, staff, operating history (if clean).
