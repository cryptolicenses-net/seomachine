In Short · Crypto Regulation in Asia

There is no single Asian crypto framework. Each jurisdiction licenses crypto businesses under its own statute and regulator: Singapore under MAS, Hong Kong under the SFC, Japan under the FSA, South Korea under the FSC, and Australia under ASIC and AUSTRAC. Picking the right one is a model-by-model decision, not a one-size answer.

Key Takeaways
  • Singapore (MAS) licenses digital payment token services under the Payment Services Act, with a separate DTSP regime for offshore-only providers since 30 June 2025
  • Hong Kong (SFC) runs a dual SFO and AMLO trading-platform regime that allows retail access, with more than a dozen licensed platforms by early 2026
  • Japan (FSA) registers exchanges under the Payment Services Act today but is moving most crypto regulation toward the securities-style FIEA from 2026
  • South Korea's Virtual Asset User Protection Act (in force 19 July 2024) mandates cold-storage and market-abuse controls; Australia uses a dual ASIC and AUSTRAC model
  • India, Thailand and Malaysia each take a distinct path, so jurisdiction selection should follow your business model, not the other way around
Section 1: The Landscape

The Asian Crypto Map

Asia is not a single market for crypto regulation. It is a patchwork of national regimes that range from some of the most mature licensing frameworks in the world to jurisdictions still drafting their first comprehensive laws. A business that is fully compliant in Singapore may be unauthorised next door, because each country writes its own rules, sets its own capital and custody requirements, and names its own regulator.

That fragmentation is the single most important thing for a founder to understand. There is no pan-Asian passport equivalent to the EU's MiCA. Serving multiple Asian markets usually means licensing, or carefully structuring around, each one separately. The upside is choice: the region offers credible homes for almost every model, from institutional custody to retail exchange to cross-border payment tokens. Our Asia crypto licensing hub lists the individual country pages behind this overview.

Three jurisdictions anchor the region as recognised crypto hubs: Singapore, Hong Kong and Japan. Each runs a defined, mature licensing path rather than a legal grey area, which is why most serious institutional players cluster around them. South Korea and Australia are large, sophisticated markets with their own protective regimes, and India, Thailand and Malaysia round out the picture with distinct national approaches.

Section 2: Singapore

Singapore: MAS and the Payment Services Act

Singapore is Asia's institutional crypto hub, and the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) is its single financial regulator. Digital payment token services provided to customers in Singapore are regulated under the Payment Services Act 2019, where qualifying firms hold a Major Payment Institution licence. The regime covers buying, selling and facilitating the exchange of digital payment tokens, and MAS has steadily tightened consumer-protection and reserve expectations on licensed providers.

In June 2025 MAS added a second pillar. The Digital Token Service Provider regime under Part 9 of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2022 took effect on 30 June 2025, capturing Singapore-incorporated entities that provide digital token services solely to customers outside Singapore. MAS has been explicit that it will license such offshore-only DTSPs only in extremely limited circumstances, effectively closing a route that some firms had used to base in Singapore while serving foreign markets.

The practical takeaway is that Singapore rewards firms genuinely serving the Singapore or institutional market and discourages purely offshore booking structures. Section 3: Hong Kong

Hong Kong: SFC Platforms with Retail Access

Hong Kong took a deliberately different path from Singapore by opening regulated crypto to retail investors. Since 1 June 2023, centralised virtual asset trading platforms operating in or actively marketing to Hong Kong must be licensed by the Securities and Futures Commission (SFC) under a dual regime: the Securities and Futures Ordinance (Cap. 571) and the Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorist Financing Ordinance (Cap. 615). The SFC accepts a single combined application for both licences to ease the administrative burden.

Crucially, licensed platforms may serve retail investors, subject to investor protection measures covering onboarding, governance, disclosure and token due diligence and admission. By early 2026 the SFC had licensed more than a dozen platforms, including established names such as OSL and HashKey Exchange. In April 2026 the SFC went further, permitting secondary trading of tokenised SFC-authorised investment products on licensed platforms, bringing regulated on-chain liquidity to its fund market.

For a retail-facing exchange that wants a regulated brand in Asia, Hong Kong is often the strongest fit. Our regulation hub tracks how the SFC regime sits alongside neighbouring frameworks.

Section 4: Japan

Japan: FSA Registration and the FIEA Shift

Japan was one of the first major economies to formally license crypto exchanges, and it remains a heavyweight market. Crypto-asset exchange service providers register with the Financial Services Agency (FSA) under the Payment Services Act, and members belong to the Japan Virtual and Crypto Assets Exchange Association (JVCEA), the industry self-regulatory body that vets new token listings before they can be offered.

The big development for 2026 is structural. The FSA has advanced a plan to move most crypto-asset regulation out of the Payment Services Act and under the Financial Instruments and Exchange Act (FIEA), treating crypto with rigour closer to that applied to stocks and bonds. The shift brings pre-sale disclosure duties, insider-trading prohibitions and tougher penalties for unlicensed operators. Fiat-linked stablecoins are intended to remain under the Payment Services Act, and NFTs are expected to sit outside FIEA.

Japan suits firms targeting a large, mature domestic retail base willing to work through a demanding registration and listing process. Our country page on crypto regulation in Japan covers the FSA framework in more detail.

Section 5: South Korea

South Korea: The User Protection Act

South Korea has one of Asia's most active retail crypto markets, and its first dedicated statute is squarely focused on protecting users. The Act on the Protection of Virtual Asset Users came into force on 19 July 2024, supervised by the Financial Services Commission (FSC). It is a protective rather than a market-opening law, built around three pillars: safeguarding customer deposits and assets, banning unfair trading, and giving regulators inspection and sanction powers.

The operational requirements are strict. Exchanges must keep customer money separate from their own funds and deposit it with a credible financial institution, store at least 80 percent of customers' virtual assets in cold wallets, and monitor for abnormal price and volume activity, reporting suspicious transactions immediately. Market manipulation and the use of material non-public information are explicitly prohibited.

In February 2025 the FSC signalled a gradual opening to institutional participation, a sign the regime may broaden over time. For now, South Korea is best understood as a large market with a hard protective floor that any licensed operator must meet.

From Our Practice

In our Asian licensing work, the most common founder mistake is treating the region as one market and assuming a licence in one hub unlocks the others. It does not. We regularly see firms that built around a Singapore base discover they still need separate authorisation to onboard Hong Kong retail users or to serve Japanese customers through a domestic entity. Each regulator wants its own application, its own capital and its own local compliance officer.

The second recurring issue is timing the structural shifts. Japan's move toward FIEA, Australia's transition from the DCE label to a full ASIC framework, and South Korea's institutional opening all change what a credible application looks like. Building the licensing plan against where each regime is heading, not just where it is today, is consistently what separates a clean approval from a stalled one.

Section 6: Australia

Australia: ASIC and AUSTRAC

Australia is included in any serious Asia-Pacific roundup, and it runs a dual-regulator model. AUSTRAC handles anti-money laundering and counter-terrorist financing and operates the exchange registration regime, while the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) oversees consumer protection and financial-services licensing. Registration with AUSTRAC has long been mandatory for digital currency exchange activity.

Two changes define the 2026 landscape. First, the FATF Travel Rule became effective on 31 March 2026, legislated through the AML/CTF amendment framework, so all in-scope exchanges must collect and transmit originator and beneficiary information. Second, the long-standing digital currency exchange label is giving way to the internationally recognised VASP terminology, and a forthcoming ASIC Digital Asset framework will require larger platforms to hold an Australian Financial Services Licence in addition to AUSTRAC registration, phasing in from 2027 after a transition period.

Australia therefore sits between the light-touch and the heavily regulated camps: registration is straightforward, but the AFSL layer and Travel Rule obligations raise the bar for platforms above the relevant thresholds.

Section 7: Emerging Markets

India, Thailand & Malaysia

Beyond the five anchor jurisdictions, three markets are worth a closer look. India permits crypto trading and ownership but has no comprehensive licensing law. Gains are taxed at a flat 30 percent plus a 1 percent transaction levy, and since April 2025 the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) oversees crypto tokens that behave like securities. The Finance Ministry has been consulting with SEBI and the Reserve Bank of India on a fuller framework ahead of the 2026 to 2027 budget, so the regime is best treated as evolving rather than settled.

Thailand regulates crypto through the Securities and Exchange Commission under its Digital Asset Business Decree, and a 2025 amendment extended licensing to offshore operators that serve persons in Thailand. Regulatory attention has shifted toward a baht-backed stablecoin framework designed to operate on a fully backed, one-to-one basis. Malaysia takes a securities-led approach: the trading, issuance and safekeeping of digital assets are regulated by the Securities Commission Malaysia, which licenses digital asset exchange operators and custodians within its capital markets framework.

The common thread across these three is movement. Each is tightening or building rules rather than standing still, which makes timing and structure as important as the rule book itself when planning an entry.

Section 8: Decision

How to Choose a Jurisdiction

Because there is no pan-Asian licence, jurisdiction selection is the decision that shapes everything that follows. The right answer flows from a handful of variables, and the timeline below shows how the region's major regimes have arrived at their current shape, which matters because choosing well means reading where each is heading next.

1

Singapore Payment Services Act
MAS brings digital payment token services into a formal licensing regime, anchoring Singapore as Asia's institutional crypto hub.
2

Hong Kong opens dual SFO and AMLO licensing for trading platforms and permits retail access under investor-protection safeguards.
3

The FSC-supervised Virtual Asset User Protection Act takes effect, mandating cold-storage, fund segregation and market-abuse controls.
4
June 2025
Singapore DTSP regime begins
The FSM Act Digital Token Service Provider regime captures Singapore entities serving only offshore customers, licensed in very limited cases.
5

Japan FIEA shift & Australia framework

Work through four questions before filing anywhere. What is your model: custodian, exchange, broker or payment-token issuer? Who are your customers: retail, institutional, domestic or cross-border? What capital and banking can you support? And how do retail rules and timelines in each candidate jurisdiction match your launch plan? Answer those, and the shortlist usually narrows to one or two countries. We run exactly this assessment before any application, so the licence fits the business rather than the other way around.
