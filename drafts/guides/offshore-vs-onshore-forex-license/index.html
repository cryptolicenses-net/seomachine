Offshore licenses in Mauritius, Seychelles, or Vanuatu are cheap, fast, and flexible, but carry weaker banking and a reputational discount. Answer · Offshore vs Onshore Forex License

An onshore forex license comes from a tier-one regulator such as the FCA, CySEC, or ASIC and brings credibility, banking access, and client protection at the cost of six-figure capital and leverage caps. An offshore license from Mauritius, Seychelles, or Vanuatu is cheaper, faster, and allows higher leverage, but carries weaker banking and reputational risk.

Key Takeaways
  • Onshore regulators (FCA, CySEC, ASIC) demand high capital, from EUR 125,000 to EUR 730,000 at CySEC, cap retail leverage at 30:1 on majors, and enforce client-money segregation
  • Offshore regimes (Mauritius, Seychelles, Vanuatu) need far less capital, often impose no leverage cap, and launch in three to six months
  • The real trade-off is credibility and banking access (onshore) against cost, speed, and leverage flexibility (offshore)
  • SVG is no longer a standalone solution: since January 2023 the FSA requires forex companies to hold a foreign license from where they actually operate
  • Many established brokers run a hybrid model, an onshore entity for EU clients plus an offshore entity for high-leverage markets
Section 1: The Basics

Offshore vs Onshore, Defined

In forex licensing, onshore means a license from a major, well-resourced regulator inside a large regulated market: the Financial Conduct Authority in the UK, the Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission under EU MiFID II, or the Australian Securities and Investments Commission. These regimes impose high capital, strict conduct rules, leverage caps, and client-money protections, and in return confer strong credibility and market access.

Offshore means a license from a smaller jurisdiction that has built a financial-services framework to attract international brokers: Mauritius, Seychelles, Vanuatu, the British Virgin Islands, and others. These regimes are deliberately lighter, with lower capital, faster timelines, lower tax, and far more flexibility on leverage, but with a smaller compliance burden comes a smaller trust premium. For a fuller breakdown of the routes available, see our forex licenses hub.

The choice is rarely about which regulator is objectively best. It is about which regulator fits your clients, your capital, your leverage proposition, and your appetite for the long, expensive, but reputationally powerful onshore path. Section 2: Onshore

The Onshore Route: FCA, CySEC, ASIC

The onshore proposition is straightforward: pay more, wait longer, get trusted. Cyprus is the most popular onshore entry point because a Cyprus Investment Firm authorised by CySEC operates under MiFID II and can passport across the entire European Economic Area. Minimum initial capital is EUR 125,000 for a firm dealing on a straight-through-processing basis and EUR 730,000 for a full market-maker that deals on its own account, with substance, governance, and an audited capital base maintained throughout.

The UK FCA and Australia's ASIC sit at the same tier. Both adopted the European model of retail protection: leverage on major currency pairs is capped at 30:1, and 20:1 on minors and exotics, alongside negative balance protection, restrictions on bonuses, and mandatory client-money segregation. The FCA backs retail clients with the Financial Services Compensation Scheme, while ASIC requires substantial net tangible assets from market-making licensees.

What you buy with that burden is genuine. Onshore brokers find it materially easier to open bank accounts, secure tier-one payment processing, sign institutional liquidity providers, and convert informed clients who actively screen for regulatory standing. The cost is real, but so is the moat. Our forex broker setup service manages the full onshore application end to end.

Section 3: Offshore

The Offshore Route: Mauritius, Seychelles, Vanuatu

The offshore proposition inverts the trade-off: pay less, move faster, accept a lower trust premium. Mauritius sits at the credible end of offshore. Its Investment Dealer (Full Service Dealer) license, issued by the Financial Services Commission under the Securities Act 2005, covers forex, CFDs, and securities. The statutory minimum stated capital is MUR 1,000,000, roughly USD 22,000, although advisers often plan for a higher working buffer. Mauritius adds a treaty network and a low effective tax rate, making it a frequent choice for brokers wanting offshore economics with more respectability.

Seychelles is the workhorse retail offshore license. The FSA's Securities Dealer License under the Securities Act 2007 covers forex and CFDs, and the regulator raised minimum paid-up capital from USD 50,000 to USD 100,000, with power to demand USD 250,000 or more for higher-risk models. Vanuatu's Financial Dealers License, issued by the VFSC, requires a one-time security deposit of around VT 5,000,000, roughly USD 44,000 to 50,000, plus genuine local presence and several years of relevant experience from the applicants.

The defining offshore advantage is leverage. None of these regimes imposes a statutory retail leverage cap, so brokers commonly market 100:1, 500:1, or higher, which is impossible onshore. The British Virgin Islands offers a middle path under its Securities and Investment Business Act, with more substance than the lightest havens. The cost of all this flexibility is banking: tier-one banks and major processors increasingly decline offshore brokers, pushing them toward payment institutions with higher fees and more friction. If you are selling to EU retail or institutional desks, an offshore license will cost you banking relationships and conversions you cannot afford to lose. If you are selling 500:1 leverage to markets that allow it, an onshore cap simply kills the product."

Section 4: Head to Head

Offshore vs Onshore: Side by Side

The table below distils the decision into the six dimensions that matter most when comparing a forex jurisdiction: capital, leverage, total cost, banking access, reputation, and timeline. Read it as a spectrum rather than a binary, because Mauritius and the BVI sit closer to the middle than Seychelles or Vanuatu.

Factor Onshore (FCA / CySEC / ASIC) Offshore (Mauritius / Seychelles / Vanuatu)
Minimum capital EUR 125,000 to EUR 730,000 (CySEC); high NTA at ASIC ~USD 22,000 (Mauritius) to USD 100,000 (Seychelles)
Leverage Capped: 30:1 majors, 20:1 minors No statutory cap; commonly 100:1 to 500:1+
Total cost High: six figures plus heavy legal and compliance Low: tens of thousands all-in
Banking and PSP access Strong: tier-one banks and processors Weak: reliant on payment institutions, de-risking common
Reputation High trust; client-money rules and compensation Lower trust; reputational discount
Timeline Long: 6 to 12+ months (CySEC), longer for FCA/ASIC Fast: 3 to 6 months

Capital figures are statutory minimums; real-world working capital is typically higher. Leverage offshore is market-typical, not a regulator-published cap.

Why this matters for your model: the dimension that breaks deals most often is not capital, it is banking. An offshore broker with strong volume but no stable banking partner is fragile, while an onshore broker pays heavily up front but rarely loses its rails. Section 5: A Closing Door

The SVG Shift: A Haven That Closed

St. Vincent and the Grenadines was for years the default destination for brokers wanting the cheapest, lightest forex setup. That changed in January 2023, when the SVG Financial Services Authority issued a policy notice requiring any business company engaged in forex activity to provide a certified copy of a license or approval from the jurisdiction where its business is actually conducted. Existing companies were given until March 2023 to comply, and new applicants must show the foreign license at incorporation.

Crucially, the SVG FSA does not itself license, regulate, or supervise forex trading, issue brokerage authorisations, or provide investor protection. The 2023 change did not ban registration outright, but it ended the no-questions-asked era: an SVG company alone is no longer a credible standalone forex solution, and now functions mainly as a holding or incorporation layer behind a real license elsewhere.

The lesson generalises. Offshore jurisdictions are tightening under pressure from the FATF and international banking partners, so a license that looks cheap today can become a liability if the regulator changes course or banks de-risk the jurisdiction. Choosing an offshore route means choosing a jurisdiction that is investing in its framework, not one quietly heading for the exit.

From Our Practice

In our forex licensing work across more than 60 jurisdictions, the most common mistake we see is a founder optimising for headline cost and discovering, six months after launch, that no reputable bank will hold the broker's funds. We have helped offshore-licensed clients restructure into a second, onshore entity precisely because their offshore-only setup had quietly capped their banking and institutional growth.

The pattern that works best is intentional from day one: brokers serving EU or institutional clients start onshore and budget for it, while brokers targeting high-leverage retail markets start offshore in a jurisdiction that is actively maintaining its framework, such as Mauritius, then add an onshore entity once volume justifies the cost. Sequencing the two correctly is consistently the difference between a clean scale-up and an expensive retrofit.

Section 6: The Decision

How to Choose Between the Two

Start with your clients, not the regulator. If you intend to solicit retail clients inside the EU, UK, or Australia, you generally need local authorisation there, which pushes you onshore regardless of cost. Marketing an offshore-only license into those markets is where brokers run into trouble, because the issue is not the legality of the license but where you sell. Institutional and EU-facing brokers should treat onshore as a baseline, not an upgrade.

If your target markets allow high leverage and value price over brand, and your budget is lean, offshore is the rational starting point. Within offshore, weigh credibility against cost: Mauritius and the BVI carry more substance and respectability than Seychelles or Vanuatu, but cost more and take longer. Match the jurisdiction to how much trust your distribution actually requires.

For many brokers the answer is both. A hybrid structure pairs an onshore entity, such as CySEC, for EU clients and credibility with an offshore entity, such as Vanuatu, to serve high-leverage markets, with the two cleanly separated and each compliant with its own regulator. That is the structure most large multi-market brokers eventually converge on, and it is worth designing toward from the outset rather than retrofitting later.
