Quick Answer · How to Get a Forex Broker License

To get a forex broker license, choose your model (A-book or market maker) and jurisdiction, incorporate a local company, fund the regulator's minimum capital, appoint directors and an AML and compliance team, build compliant technology with liquidity and segregated client accounts, then submit your application and pass the regulator's review before launch.

Key Takeaways
  • The first decision is your model: an A-book or STP broker passes orders to liquidity providers, a market maker is the client's counterparty and needs more capital
  • Jurisdiction drives capital: a CySEC Cyprus Investment Firm needs 150,000 euros to hold client money or 750,000 euros to deal on own account under the EU IFR and IFD framework
  • Australia requires net tangible assets of at least AUD 1 million for retail OTC derivative issuers under ASIC RG 166; the UK FCA uses class-based own-funds requirements under its IFPR
  • Offshore options such as the Mauritius FSC Investment Dealer Broker licence start near MUR 700,000 (about USD 15,500), far lower but with weaker reputation and harder banking
  • Every regulated route needs the same core: incorporation, fit and proper people, AML and KYC, technology and liquidity, segregated client accounts, and a complete application file
Overview

What Getting a Forex License Actually Involves

A forex broker license is a regulatory authorisation that lets a company offer foreign-exchange and, usually, contract-for-difference trading to clients. The process is consistent in shape across the world: pick a model and a jurisdiction, set up a properly capitalised company, staff it with fit and proper people, build compliant systems, and prove all of it to a regulator. What changes from country to country is the capital, the timeline, and the depth of review.

The single most consequential choice is jurisdiction, because it fixes your capital, your credibility, and which markets you can serve. Step 1: Strategy

Choose Your Model and Jurisdiction

Before any paperwork, settle your execution model. An A-book broker, also called STP, agency, or matched principal, routes client orders straight to external liquidity providers and earns the spread or a commission. It carries no market risk because it never takes the other side of a trade. A market maker, or B-book broker, is the counterparty to its clients and absorbs the resulting exposure. Because market makers hold principal risk, regulators demand markedly higher capital from them.

That model choice feeds directly into jurisdiction. Onshore regulators such as CySEC, the FCA, and ASIC give you reputation, banking access, and in the EU case the ability to passport across the bloc, in exchange for higher capital and a longer review. Offshore regulators such as the Mauritius FSC or Saint Vincent offer lower cost and faster setup, but weaker prestige and harder access to banks and payment processors.

Pick the pairing of model and jurisdiction that matches your target clients, budget, and risk appetite. A B-book broker targeting EU retail clients points toward a CySEC market-maker authorisation; a lean A-book startup serving less restricted markets may begin offshore and upgrade later.

Step 2: Entity

Incorporate the Local Company

Regulators license companies, not individuals, so you must first incorporate a legal entity in the chosen jurisdiction. In most regulated regimes the entity has to be a locally registered company with a registered office, and increasingly with genuine local substance: a real office, local directors or staff, and operations actually run from that jurisdiction rather than a nameplate.

At incorporation you set the share capital, register the ultimate beneficial owners, and prepare the corporate documents the regulator will scrutinise, including the memorandum and articles, the ownership chart, and the group structure. Getting the structure right at this stage matters, because the regulator assesses the company, its owners, and its controllers together when it reviews the licence application.

"Founders often pick a jurisdiction on headline capital alone, then discover the real constraint is substance and banking. A 150,000-euro CySEC firm that cannot open a client-money account or staff a local compliance team is not cheaper than a well-banked alternative, it is stuck. We model substance and banking before we file."

Step 3: Capital

Meet the Capital Requirements

Minimum capital is the requirement that varies most between jurisdictions, and it follows directly from your model. In the European Union, a Cyprus Investment Firm under CySEC is governed by the EU Investment Firms Regulation and Directive, in force since 26 June 2021. The initial capital is 75,000 euros for an advisory firm that does not hold client money, 150,000 euros for a firm that holds client money and runs an STP model, and 750,000 euros for dealing on own account as a market maker. Many secondary sources still quote the older 125,000 and 730,000 euro figures from the pre-2021 regime; the IFR and IFD figures are the current ones.

In the United Kingdom, the FCA applies the Investment Firms Prudential Regime, which uses class-based own-funds requirements: a matched-principal STP broker sits at the lower class while a firm dealing on its own account falls into the 750,000-pound class. In Australia, ASIC requires a retail OTC derivative issuer to hold net tangible assets of at least AUD 1 million, or 10 percent of average revenue if greater, with at least half held in cash. Offshore, the Mauritius FSC Investment Dealer Broker licence requires minimum stated capital of about MUR 700,000, roughly USD 15,500.

Why this matters: capital is not a one-time deposit. It is an ongoing prudential floor you must maintain at all times. ASIC firms must notify the regulator if net tangible assets fall below 110 percent of the requirement, and the Mauritius FSC treats any drop below the minimum as a breach of licence conditions, not a temporary blip.

Step 4: Governance

Appoint Directors, Compliance and AML

Regulators will not authorise a brokerage unless the people running it are fit and proper. Expect to appoint a board with enough executive and non-executive directors, a compliance officer, and a money laundering reporting officer, who in many regimes can be one person at smaller firms but must be qualified and resident enough to satisfy local substance rules. CySEC, for example, also expects a risk manager and an internal audit function as part of a credible governance structure.

Alongside the people you must build the AML and KYC framework: documented policies for customer due diligence, sanctions and PEP screening, transaction monitoring, suspicious-activity reporting, and record retention. This is not paperwork for its own sake. The regulator reads the framework as evidence that the firm can actually prevent financial crime, and a thin or generic AML manual is one of the most common reasons applications stall.

From Our Practice

In our forex and CFD licensing work across more than 60 jurisdictions, the application files that move fastest are the ones where governance and AML are built early, in parallel with incorporation, not bolted on at the end. Regulators can tell the difference between a tailored framework that maps to the firm's actual products and a template downloaded the week before filing.

We also see otherwise strong applications stall on local substance. A director who exists only on paper, or a compliance officer juggling five unrelated mandates, signals to the regulator that the firm is a nameplate. Funding genuine local presence and a properly resourced compliance team before filing is consistently the difference between a smooth review and months of follow-up questions.

Step 5: Infrastructure

Build Technology, Liquidity and Segregated Accounts

A forex brokerage is a technology business with a licence attached. You need a trading platform, commonly a white-label MetaTrader or cTrader deployment or a proprietary system, connected to one or more liquidity providers or prime brokers that supply pricing and absorb your order flow. A-book and STP brokers depend on these connections entirely; even market makers usually hedge net exposure with liquidity providers. Tight spreads, low latency, and platform stability are what make the offering competitive.

Equally important, and a hard regulatory requirement in regulated jurisdictions, is segregation of client money. Client funds must be held in segregated accounts at a regulated bank, separate from the broker's own operating money, so they cannot be used to fund the firm and are ring-fenced if it fails. Opening those accounts is often the slowest practical step, because banks apply their own due diligence on top of the regulator's, which is why banking readiness should be planned alongside the licence, not after it.

Step 6: Application

Submit the Application and Pass Review

With the company, capital, people, and infrastructure in place, you compile and file the application. A typical file includes the business plan and three-year financial projections, the corporate and ownership documents, the regulatory business plan describing services and the execution model, the compliance and AML manuals, fit-and-proper questionnaires for directors and key staff, and evidence of capital and banking arrangements. Application and annual fees are paid at this stage.

The regulator then reviews everything and almost always comes back with questions. For a full CySEC Cyprus Investment Firm, the process commonly runs nine to fourteen months from filing to approval; FCA and ASIC authorisations are comparable or longer. Offshore licences such as Mauritius or Saint Vincent are reviewed far faster, often within weeks to a few months, reflecting their lighter scrutiny. Responsiveness to regulator queries is the biggest variable you control.

Step 7: Go Live

After the License: Ongoing Obligations

Authorisation is the start of compliance, not the end. Once licensed, the firm must maintain its minimum capital continuously, file periodic prudential and financial reports, run ongoing AML monitoring and reporting, treat clients fairly under the local conduct rules, and observe any product-intervention limits, such as the leverage caps and negative-balance protection that the FCA, ASIC, and EU regulators impose on retail CFDs.
