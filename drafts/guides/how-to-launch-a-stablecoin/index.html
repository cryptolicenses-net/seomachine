Quick Answer · How to Launch a Stablecoin

To launch a stablecoin in the EU, first classify it under MiCA. A token pegged to a single official currency is an e-money token (EMT) and must be issued by a licensed electronic money institution or bank, with redemption at par. A token backed by a basket or other assets is an asset-referenced token (ART) and needs its own MiCA authorisation plus at least EUR 350,000 in own funds.

Key Takeaways
  • MiCA splits stablecoins into two categories: e-money tokens (EMT, single fiat) under Title IV and asset-referenced tokens (ART, basket or other assets) under Title III, both applicable since 30 June 2024
  • An EMT must be issued by an authorised EMI or credit institution and is redeemable at par; an ART needs its own MiCA authorisation from a national competent authority
  • ART own funds under Article 35 are the highest of EUR 350,000, 2 percent of average reserves, or a quarter of fixed overheads; EMTs build on the EUR 350,000 EMI capital floor
  • Significant tokens (over 10 million holders or EUR 5 billion in value) face up to 3 percent own funds and direct EBA supervision
  • The choice between EMT and ART is the single most important decision, and it should be made before you design the reserve or write the white paper
Section 1: The Basics

How MiCA Classifies Stablecoins

The first thing to understand about launching a stablecoin in the EU is that the word "stablecoin" does not appear in the law as a single licence. The Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation, Regulation (EU) 2023/1114, instead splits stable-value tokens into two distinct categories, each with its own title, issuer rules, and capital requirements. Getting this classification right is the foundation of the whole project. For the wider framework, our guide on MiCA explained sets out how the regulation fits together.

The first category is the e-money token, or EMT, governed by Title IV. An EMT references the value of a single official currency, so a euro-pegged or US dollar-pegged stablecoin is an EMT. It is treated as electronic money, which means it can only be issued by an entity authorised as an electronic money institution (EMI) or a credit institution under existing EU law. Our overview of the EMI licence explains that base authorisation.

The second category is the asset-referenced token, or ART, governed by Title III. An ART references any other value: a basket of several currencies, a commodity such as gold, one or more crypto-assets, or a combination. Because it does not fit the narrow e-money definition, an ART issuer needs a dedicated MiCA authorisation from a national competent authority. Both titles have applied since 30 June 2024. Our companion piece on stablecoin regulation traces how these rules sit alongside frameworks in other regions.

Section 2: EMT vs ART

E-Money Token vs Asset-Referenced Token

The choice between an EMT and an ART defines everything that follows: the licence you apply for, the capital you post, the reserve you build, and the regulator you answer to. The distinction is not a matter of marketing. It turns on a single legal test of what the token references. If the peg is to one official currency and nothing else, it is an EMT. If it references anything broader, it is an ART.

For most fiat-pegged projects, the EMT route is the more efficient one because it builds on the mature electronic money institution framework rather than a brand-new authorisation type. The trade-off is that only an EMI or credit institution can issue an EMT, so a founder without that base licence must either obtain one or partner with an institution that holds it. The ART route is broader and more flexible, but heavier: it carries its own MiCA authorisation, a higher governance burden, and a own funds calculation that scales with the reserve.

The table below sets out the practical differences a founder needs before choosing a path. It is the single most consequential decision in the project, and it should be made before any reserve or white paper work begins.

Feature EMT (E-Money Token) ART (Asset-Referenced Token)
Peg / reference A single official currency (e.g. EUR or USD) A basket: multiple currencies, commodities, or crypto-assets
Issuer licence Authorised EMI or credit institution + white paper Dedicated MiCA ART authorisation from a national authority
MiCA title Title IV (Articles 48 onward) Title III (Articles 16 to 47)
Capital / own funds EUR 350,000 EMI capital floor (plus extras if significant) Highest of EUR 350,000, 2% of reserves, or 25% of overheads
Reserve 1:1 safeguarded e-money funds Segregated reserve of assets fully covering liabilities

Section 3: Capital & Reserve

Capital, Reserve, and Redemption Rules

Capital and reserve are where the two routes diverge most sharply in cost. For an ART issuer, MiCA Article 35 sets own funds at the highest of three figures: EUR 350,000, two percent of the average amount of the reserve of assets, or a quarter of the fixed overheads of the preceding year. The two percent figure is calculated on the average reserve measured at the end of each calendar day over the preceding six months, so a large or fast-growing token quickly pushes the requirement well above the EUR 350,000 floor.

An EMT does not have its own MiCA own funds article in the same form, because the issuer is already an EMI or credit institution and carries that framework's capital. The initial capital floor for an electronic money institution under EU e-money rules is EUR 350,000, with additional prudential requirements layered on for significant EMTs. In both routes the reserve sits on top of own funds and is the real backstop for holders.

The reserve must fully cover the issuer's liabilities to token holders and be segregated from the issuer's own assets. Under the EBA technical standards for ARTs, at least twenty percent of the reserve must be capable of being sold within one day and thirty percent within five days, rising to forty and sixty percent respectively for significant tokens. Redemption is the holder's core protection: an EMT must be redeemable at par value at any time in funds under Article 49, while an ART carries a permanent redemption right that may be settled in funds or in the underlying assets.

Why this matters for your licence: the two percent reserve test means an ART issuer's capital is not a fixed number, it grows with the token. A project that models only the EUR 350,000 floor and ignores the reserve-based calculation will be undercapitalised the moment the token scales. Build the capital plan around the highest-of test from day one, and revisit it as the reserve grows. See our MiCA compliance overview for how this fits the full application.

Section 4: Significant Tokens

Significant Tokens and EBA Supervision

MiCA applies a second, heavier tier of rules to stablecoins that reach systemic scale. A token is classified as significant when it meets at least three of a set of criteria, which include more than ten million holders, an issued value, market capitalisation, or reserve over EUR 5 billion, and more than 2.5 million daily transactions with EUR 500 million in average daily transaction volume. Other criteria capture international reach and interconnection with the financial system.

Crossing into significant status changes who supervises the token and how much capital it must hold. The European Banking Authority takes over direct supervision of significant ART issuers from the national competent authority, while for significant EMTs the EBA and the national authority supervise jointly, with the EBA focused on the additional own funds and liquidity requirements. Significant tokens face higher own funds of up to three percent of average reserves, stricter liquidity management, and recovery and redemption plans.

For most projects at launch this tier is a planning horizon rather than an immediate obligation, but it should shape the design from the start. A token built without headroom for the significant-token rules will hit a hard wall of extra capital and EU-level supervision exactly when it is succeeding, which is the worst moment to be re-engineering a reserve and a governance model. Our EU regulation overview tracks how these supervisory lines are drawn.

From Our Practice

In our EMT and ART licensing work across the EU, the most expensive mistakes are made in the first month, before a single line of code is written. We routinely meet founders who have already committed to an ART structure, and an ART reserve, for a token that references nothing but the euro. Reclassifying it as an EMT then saves them a separate authorisation, a reserve-based capital calculation, and several months of regulator dialogue.

We also see applications stall because the capital plan models only the EUR 350,000 floor and ignores the two percent reserve test, or because the white paper and the reserve design are built in isolation and contradict each other under review. Settling the classification, the capital model, and the white paper as one coherent package, before approaching the regulator, is consistently the difference between a clean authorisation and a long round of queries.

Section 5: The Roadmap

How to Launch a Stablecoin: Step by Step

With the theory settled, launching a stablecoin under MiCA follows a consistent sequence. The detail varies by route and member state, but the order below is the workflow we run on every mandate. The first step, classification, governs all the others, which is why it sits at the front.

1
Classify
Decide EMT or ART
Test what the token references. A single official currency makes it an EMT under Title IV; a basket or other assets makes it an ART under Title III. This single decision sets the licence, capital, and supervisor.
2
Structure
Choose the issuing entity and home state
For an EMT, secure or partner with an EMI or credit institution. For an ART, set up the issuer for a dedicated authorisation. Pick the home member state, because the licence passports across the EU.
3
Capitalise
Model own funds and design the reserve
Build the capital plan around the highest-of test (EUR 350,000, 2% of reserves, or 25% of overheads for ARTs) and design a segregated, liquid reserve that fully covers liabilities to holders.
4
Document
Draft the MiCA white paper and policies
Prepare the crypto-asset white paper, redemption policy, reserve management policy, governance, and AML framework. For an ART the white paper is approved at authorisation; for an EMT it is notified.
5
Authorise & launch
File, answer the regulator, then issue
Submit the application, manage the competent authority's review, obtain authorisation or EMI sign-off, and issue the token with passporting and ongoing reporting, reserve attestation, and supervision in place.
Section 6: Pitfalls

Mistakes That Sink a Launch

Most failed or delayed stablecoin launches stumble on the same handful of issues, and almost all of them trace back to the planning phase. The first is misclassification: choosing the ART route for a token that is plainly an EMT, which adds a separate authorisation and a heavier capital base for no benefit. The second is undercapitalisation: building the capital plan on the EUR 350,000 floor while ignoring the two percent reserve test that bites as the token grows.

The third is a reserve that looks adequate on paper but fails the liquidity standards, where too little of it can be sold within one or five days to meet redemption demand. The fourth is treating the white paper as a marketing document rather than a regulated disclosure that must match the reserve, redemption, and governance design exactly. The fifth is ignoring the significant-token horizon, so a successful token suddenly faces three percent own funds and direct EBA supervision with no headroom to absorb them.

None of these are exotic. They are the predictable result of starting the build before the classification, capital model, and disclosure are settled as one package. The roadmap above exists to put those decisions first, where they belong.
