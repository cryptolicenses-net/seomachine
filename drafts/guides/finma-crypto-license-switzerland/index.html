FINMA Crypto Licensing in Switzerland: SRO, FinTech & DLT Routes Explained 2026

Switzerland has no single FINMA crypto license. Instead it offers a ladder of routes, from AMLA SRO membership through the FinTech licence and the DLT trading facility licence to a full banking licence. This guide, written from our Zug office in the heart of Crypto Valley, explains each route, the capital and deposit limits, FINMA's token taxonomy, and which path fits which activity in 2026.

In short · FINMA crypto license

A FINMA crypto license is not one document but a choice of route. Switzerland matches the authorisation to the activity: SRO membership under AMLA for pure financial intermediaries, the FinTech licence under Banking Act Article 1b for deposit-taking up to CHF 100 million without interest or lending, a DLT trading facility licence for trading tokenised securities, or a full banking licence for classic banking activity.

Key Takeaways
  • Switzerland has no single FINMA crypto license; you pick the route that fits what your business actually does
  • SRO membership under AMLA is the entry route for exchanges, brokers and OTC desks that act only as financial intermediaries
  • The FinTech licence (Banking Act Article 1b) allows public deposits up to CHF 100 million with no interest and no lending
  • The DLT trading facility licence under the FMIA, in force since 2021, governs multilateral trading of DLT securities
  • FINMA classifies tokens as payment, utility, or asset tokens, and only asset tokens are treated as securities
Section 1: The Framework

There Is No Single FINMA Crypto Licence

The first thing founders need to understand is that there is no single FINMA crypto license to apply for. Swiss financial market law is technology-neutral and activity-based, so the question is never "do I get the crypto licence", but "which regulated activity am I performing, and which authorisation does that trigger". Get that mapping right and the rest of the process is orderly; get it wrong and you waste months on the wrong application.

Swiss crypto regulation in 2026 rests on a stack of statutes rather than a dedicated crypto act. The core pieces are the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA), the Banking Act (which contains the FinTech licence in Article 1b), the Financial Market Infrastructure Act (FMIA, which hosts the DLT trading facility), the Collective Investment Schemes Act, and the 2021 distributed ledger technology legislative package. Our broader explainer on what a crypto license is and how it works sets the global context for these Swiss specifics.

From that stack flow four practical routes, ordered roughly by intensity: SRO membership, the FinTech licence, the DLT trading facility licence, and the full banking or securities firm licence. The sections below take each in turn. For the country-level overview, see our dedicated page on the Swiss crypto licence and the deeper regulatory picture on Swiss crypto regulation.

Section 2: Route 1

SRO Membership (AMLA)

The lightest route is membership of a FINMA-recognised self-regulatory organisation, or SRO. This is the entry point for businesses that act purely as financial intermediaries: crypto exchanges, brokers, OTC desks, payment processors, and certain wallet providers. Under the Anti-Money Laundering Act, these activities make the firm a financial intermediary subject to AML and counter-terrorist-financing obligations.

It is vital to be precise here, because a common misconception trips up new entrants. SRO membership is not a FINMA licence. It is the way a financial intermediary discharges its AML obligation: the SRO, a private body recognised by FINMA, supervises its members for AML compliance, while FINMA supervises the SROs. The route does not authorise deposit-taking or holding client assets in the banking sense, so it suits models where the firm facilitates transactions but does not become a depositary of public funds.

Practically, this is the fastest and least capital-intensive way to operate a compliant Swiss crypto business. A typical setup involves a Swiss company, a resident director, and an appointed AML officer, with the intermediary then admitted to one of the recognised SROs such as VQF, PolyReg or SO-FIT. If your model goes beyond pure intermediation, the next route up is the FinTech licence.

Section 3: Route 2

The FinTech Licence (Banking Act Art. 1b)

The FinTech licence, formally the authorisation for persons under Article 1b of the Banking Act, was created to give innovative firms a lighter alternative to a full banking licence. It permits an institution to accept public deposits of up to CHF 100 million, or crypto-based assets, on a critical condition: those funds may not be invested, may not bear interest, and may not be used for lending. In Swiss terms, this is a prohibition on active banking business and on maturity transformation.

The applicant must be a Swiss legal entity, a company limited by shares, a corporation with unlimited partners, or a limited liability company, with both its registered office and its actual business activity in Switzerland. That substance requirement is not a formality; FINMA expects genuine operations and governance in the country, not a letterbox.

There is a significant trade-off to flag clearly to clients. Because the FinTech licence sits below full banking, deposits held under it are neither privileged nor covered by deposit protection in the event of the institution's bankruptcy, and the firm must inform its clients of exactly that. The FinTech licence is the right fit for custodial wallets, certain stablecoin and payment models, and platforms that hold customer balances without running a lending book.

"The line that decides FinTech licence versus SRO membership is deceptively simple: are you holding public funds or crypto deposits, or merely facilitating transactions? The moment client balances sit on your books, you have crossed into Article 1b territory, even with no interest and no lending. We map that line before a client commits to a route, because re-papering an application halfway through is the costliest mistake in Swiss licensing."

Section 4: Route 3

The DLT Trading Facility Licence

Switzerland's 2020 distributed ledger technology legislative package created a purpose-built market infrastructure category: the DLT trading facility. The relevant amendments to the Financial Market Infrastructure Act entered into force on 1 August 2021, and in March 2025 FINMA granted the first ever DLT trading facility licence, to BX Digital AG, demonstrating that the regime is live and usable rather than theoretical.

A DLT trading facility licence authorises the multilateral trading of DLT securities, the tokenised securities introduced by the same legislative package. Crucially, and unlike a traditional stock exchange, a DLT trading facility may admit retail participants to trade directly. This makes it the correct route for a venue matching buyers and sellers of tokenised shares, bonds, or other asset tokens, rather than a spot exchange dealing only in payment tokens, which would sit under the AML or FinTech routes instead.

Capital requirements scale with the services offered. A DLT trading facility needs minimum capital of CHF 1 million where it provides no custody, clearing, or settlement, rising to CHF 5 million where it does. Small DLT trading facilities benefit from a reduced floor of CHF 500,000, or, where custody is offered, 5 percent of the DLT securities held in custody subject to that CHF 500,000 minimum.

Section 5: Route 4

The Full Banking Licence

When a crypto business genuinely needs classic banking capability, accepting interest-bearing deposits, lending, or performing maturity transformation, none of the lighter routes will do, and a full banking licence under the Banking Act becomes necessary. This is the path taken by Switzerland's crypto-native banks, and it is the most demanding of the four routes in both capital and supervisory terms.

The statutory minimum is a fully paid-in share capital of CHF 10 million under the Banking Ordinance. That figure is a floor, not a target: in our experience FINMA expects materially more for a viable application, often in the region of CHF 20 million or above for a niche institution, and it can demand higher capital still depending on the intended activities, balance-sheet projection, and risk profile. Governance, internal control, and audit requirements are correspondingly heavier.

The pay-off is the broadest licence scope in Switzerland and the strongest signal of institutional credibility. For most crypto founders, though, the full banking licence is the wrong starting point; it is reserved for models that truly require banking activity rather than custody, trading, or payments, which the lighter routes already cover. Section 6: Token Classification

FINMA's Token Taxonomy

Running parallel to the licensing question is the classification of the token itself, and FINMA's approach here is one of the most cited frameworks in global crypto regulation. Its 2018 ICO guidelines set out three economic categories, with the legal treatment flowing from each. The category is determined by the token's underlying purpose and whether it is already transferable or tradeable, and hybrid forms are expressly recognised.

Payment tokens are synonymous with cryptocurrencies: a means of payment with no further function or claim. FINMA subjects their transfer to anti-money laundering requirements but does not treat them as securities. Utility tokens provide digital access to an application or service; if that access is fully functional at issue and is the token's sole purpose, the issue does not require securities-law approval. Asset tokens represent assets such as participations, debt, or claims to dividends or interest, and FINMA treats them as securities, which brings prospectus duties and securities-trading rules into play.

This taxonomy interacts directly with the licensing routes above. An asset-token issuance or a venue trading asset tokens points toward securities law and, for a trading venue, the DLT trading facility licence. A payment-token exchange points toward the AML or FinTech routes. Misclassifying a token is one of the surest ways to end up in the wrong authorisation, which is why classification is the very first step in any serious Swiss application.

Choosing Your Route

The table below maps the four routes against the criteria that matter most: the activity each one permits, the capital involved, whether it allows deposit-taking, and its AML status. Use it as a first filter, then validate against your specific token and business model, because edge cases and hybrid activities frequently shift the answer.
