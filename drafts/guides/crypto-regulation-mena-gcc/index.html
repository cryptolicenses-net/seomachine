In Short · Crypto Regulation UAE

Crypto regulation in the UAE is split across four authorities: VARA regulates virtual assets in onshore Dubai, ADGM's FSRA covers the Abu Dhabi free zone, the DFSA covers the DIFC free zone, and the federal Securities and Commodities Authority, renamed the Capital Market Authority in January 2026, sits above them. Which one applies depends on where you operate and who your clients are.

Key Takeaways
  • The UAE has no single crypto regulator; VARA, ADGM's FSRA, the DFSA, and the federal CMA each govern a different zone and audience
  • VARA covers onshore Dubai and is retail-friendly; ADGM and DIFC are common-law free zones favoured by institutional firms
  • The federal SCA was renamed the Capital Market Authority on 1 January 2026 under Federal Decree-Laws 32 and 33 of 2025, with expanded virtual-asset powers
  • Under FDL33, only assets on the CMA's official list may be traded, so federal rules layer on top of any local or free-zone licence
  • Across the GCC, Bahrain's CBB leads with a framework since 2019, while Qatar and Saudi Arabia remain more cautious
Section 1: The Map

The UAE Multi-Regulator Map

The first thing to understand about crypto regulation in the UAE is that there is no single rulebook and no single regulator. Instead, the country runs a layered system: federal authorities set overarching policy, the Emirate of Dubai has its own dedicated crypto regulator, and two common-law financial free zones write their own frameworks. A virtual asset business can fall under more than one regime at once, depending on its activities and geographic footprint.

Four bodies matter most. VARA, the Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority, regulates virtual assets in onshore Dubai. The FSRA regulates the Abu Dhabi Global Market free zone. The DFSA regulates the Dubai International Financial Centre free zone. And the federal Securities and Commodities Authority, renamed the Capital Market Authority, or CMA, in January 2026, sits at the top alongside the Central Bank of the UAE. Choosing among them is a licensing decision before it is a compliance one, which is why our guide to choosing a crypto licence jurisdiction treats the UAE as several options rather than one.

For founders weighing the UAE, the practical question is not "is crypto legal here" but "which authority should license me." The answer turns on where you base the business and who your customers are. Our MENA crypto licensing hub sets out each route in detail, and the sections below explain how the pieces fit together in 2026.

Section 2: Dubai Onshore

VARA: The Dubai Regulator

VARA, the Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority, was established in 2022 under Dubai Law No. 4 of 2022 and is widely described as the world's first purpose-built virtual-asset regulator. It supervises all virtual asset activity carried out in or from the Emirate of Dubai, with one important exception: the DIFC free zone, which is regulated separately by the DFSA. Any provider offering issuance, custody, exchange, or broker-dealer services in onshore Dubai must hold a VARA licence.

VARA's growth has been rapid. As of February 2026 it was reported to have licensed 507 virtual asset service providers, with combined assets under management exceeding USD 25 billion, as Dubai positions itself as a leading regulated digital-asset hub. Our VARA licensing page walks through the activity categories and capital expectations in detail.

The framework is detailed and increasingly demanding on compliance. In 2026 VARA tightened its anti-money-laundering guidance, expecting firms to use data-driven risk models and to refresh risk profiles at least every three months, and it has pushed Dubai crypto businesses to track FATF blacklists closely. VARA is generally seen as the retail-friendly choice, suiting exchanges, marketplaces, and payment firms that serve the public rather than purely institutional clients.

Section 3: The Free Zones

ADGM and DFSA: Free-Zone Frameworks

Two common-law financial free zones run their own crypto frameworks, separate from both VARA and the federal regime. In Abu Dhabi, the Financial Services Regulatory Authority, the FSRA, regulates the Abu Dhabi Global Market, or ADGM. In Dubai, the Dubai Financial Services Authority, the DFSA, regulates the Dubai International Financial Centre, or DIFC. Both operate under English-law-inspired systems and tend to attract institutional and cross-border firms.

ADGM's FSRA was an early mover, operating a dedicated virtual-asset framework with an Accepted Virtual Assets list. It refreshed its guidance in June 2025 and again with amendments in 2026, adding new licence categories and addressing tokenized securities and DeFi protocols. ADGM is commonly favoured by global institutional firms, custody providers, and tokenization platforms seeking regulatory certainty. Our companion piece on VARA versus ADGM compares the two head to head.

The DFSA introduced its Crypto Token regime in 2022, allowing DIFC firms to be licensed for financial services involving recognised Crypto Tokens, and brought updated Crypto Token rules into force on 12 January 2026. The DFSA leans towards investment tokens and securities-style activity. Our DIFC licensing page sets out what the DFSA route involves in practice.

Section 4: Federal Layer

SCA, Now the CMA: The Federal Layer

Above the local and free-zone regulators sits the federal layer, and it changed significantly at the start of 2026. The Securities and Commodities Authority, long the UAE's federal securities regulator, was replaced by the Capital Market Authority, the CMA, with effect from 1 January 2026 under Federal Decree-Laws No. 32 and No. 33 of 2025. The CMA is the legal successor to the SCA, so existing references to the SCA in legislation now read CMA, and it carries an expanded statutory mandate over virtual assets.

The most consequential change for crypto is asset listing. Under Federal Decree-Law No. 33 of 2025, no virtual asset may be traded in the UAE unless it appears on the CMA's official list, is registered with the CMA, and is operated by a CMA-recognised platform. In practice this gives the federal authority a decisive say over which tokens can trade nationwide, even on platforms licensed by VARA or in the free zones. Our regulation hub tracks how these federal rules interact with local regimes.

Federal oversight, administered by the CMA together with the Central Bank of the UAE, applies to mainland UAE outside Dubai and outside the financial free zones, and reaches activities targeting UAE clients from abroad. The reforms also introduced a transitional period of roughly a year, running to around September 2026, for firms to obtain licensing, partner with a licensed entity, or stop serving UAE users. The federal Securities and Commodities Authority portal remains the reference point for this national framework.

Why this matters for licensing: a local or free-zone licence is necessary but no longer the whole story. A VARA or ADGM authorisation governs how you operate, while the federal CMA list governs which assets you may offer to UAE users. Plan for both layers from the outset rather than discovering the federal constraint after launch. See our MENA licensing hub for how the layers combine.

Section 5: Choosing

Which Regulator Applies to You

Because the UAE runs four regimes in parallel, the decision tree below describes the questions a firm works through to identify the right route. The detail of each licence varies, but the sequence of questions is consistent, and getting it right at the start saves a costly relicensing later.

Locate
Decide where you base the entity
Onshore Dubai points to VARA; the Abu Dhabi free zone points to ADGM's FSRA; the DIFC free zone points to the DFSA; mainland activity engages the federal CMA framework.
2
Audience
Identify who your clients are
Retail and public-facing models often suit VARA, while institutional, custody, and tokenization businesses tend to favour the common-law free zones at ADGM or the DIFC.
3

Exchange, custody, broker-dealer, payment, and token-issuance activities each carry their own permissions and capital expectations under the relevant regulator.

Confirm assets against the CMA list

Build the application and AML framework
Prepare the licence application with the AML, KYC, and governance documentation the chosen regulator expects, allowing for the federal transition deadline around September 2026.
From Our Practice

In our Gulf licensing work, the most common mistake we see is a founder choosing a regulator for its reputation rather than its fit. A retail exchange that applies to a free zone built for institutions can spend months meeting requirements designed for a different business, while the same firm under VARA would have moved faster. The geography and the client base, not the brand of the regulator, should drive the decision.

The second recurring issue since 2026 is the federal listing layer. We now routinely advise clients that a VARA or ADGM licence does not by itself settle which tokens they may offer to UAE users, because the CMA's official list applies on top. Building both the local licence and the federal asset position into the plan from day one is what keeps a launch on schedule rather than stalled at the last hurdle.

Section 6: The Wider GCC

Bahrain, Qatar and Saudi Arabia

The UAE is the most developed crypto market in the Gulf, but it is not alone. Across the GCC, regulators are moving from treating crypto as a fringe asset class to building regulated digital-asset infrastructure, though they are doing so at very different speeds. For firms with regional ambitions, the neighbours matter.

Bahrain was the first GCC country to formalise a comprehensive framework. The Central Bank of Bahrain, the CBB, has regulated crypto activity through its Crypto-Asset, or CRA, Module within Volume 6 of the CBB Rulebook since 2019, and it updates the module regularly. In July 2025 the CBB added a Stablecoin Issuance and Offering Module, permitting approved issuance of fiat-backed stablecoins denominated in Bahraini dinar and US dollar, an early move into regulated stablecoins for the region.

Qatar and Saudi Arabia remain more cautious. The Qatar Financial Centre has introduced a Digital Assets Framework covering tokenization and the legal recognition of smart contracts, while keeping onshore spot crypto trading restricted. Saudi Arabia stays conservative: its central bank, SAMA, runs a regulatory sandbox and has appointed a crypto lead, but a full licensing regime is still developing. For most international firms today, the UAE and Bahrain offer the clearest licensing routes in the GCC, and a MENA crypto licence is most commonly built around one of them.
