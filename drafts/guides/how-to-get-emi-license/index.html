Answer · How to Get an EMI License

To get an EMI license in Europe you incorporate a company in an EEA state, raise EUR 350,000 in initial capital, appoint fit-and-proper management, and file a full application with the national regulator covering your business plan, safeguarding, and AML framework. The Bank of Lithuania is the most popular route, with statutory review of up to three months.

Key Takeaways
  • An EMI license is granted under the second Electronic Money Directive (2009/110/EC) and the PSD2 framework, and lets you issue e-money and run payments across the EEA
  • Minimum initial capital is EUR 350,000, plus ongoing own funds of at least 2 percent of average outstanding e-money under Method D
  • You apply to one national regulator; a full EMI then passports into all 30 EEA states without a separate licence in each
  • Lithuania is the most popular hub: the Bank of Lithuania works in English with a statutory review of up to three months, though six to twelve months is realistic end to end
  • Safeguarding of client funds and fit-and-proper management are hard requirements, not optional extras
Section 1: The Basics

What an EMI License Actually Is

An EMI, or electronic money institution, is a regulated company authorised to issue electronic money and to provide payment services. Electronic money is stored monetary value: the balance in a digital wallet, a prepaid card, or an IBAN account that a customer can use to pay and be paid. An EMI license is the permission that lets a business hold that value on behalf of customers and move it, which a plain technology company cannot legally do.

The license sits on two pieces of EU law. The second Electronic Money Directive, Directive 2009/110/EC, known as EMD2, governs the issuance of e-money and sets the EUR 350,000 capital floor. The second Payment Services Directive, Directive (EU) 2015/2366, known as PSD2, governs the payment-service side, the authorisation procedure, and the passporting that lets a single licence work across the European Economic Area. An EMI is, in effect, a payment institution that can also store value.

For founders, the appeal is independence and reach. An EMI can issue its own IBANs and card programmes rather than renting them, and it can serve customers across 30 EEA states on one home-state authorisation. For crypto businesses in particular, an EMI provides the fiat rails, wallets, and account infrastructure that pair naturally with a crypto-asset authorisation. If you are weighing the EMI against a full bank, our guide on EMI vs banking license sets the two side by side.

Section 2: Requirements

EMI License Requirements

Every EMI application is judged against the same core set of requirements, whichever EEA regulator you choose. There are five that decide most applications: capital, fit-and-proper people, safeguarding, a credible business plan, and a working compliance framework. Get all five right on paper before you file, because regulators review the whole file together and a weakness in one area slows the entire application.

The capital requirement is the most quoted figure. An EMI needs EUR 350,000 in paid-up initial capital under EMD2, against EUR 125,000 for a general payment institution and EUR 50,000 for a firm offering only payment initiation services under PSD2. The higher EMI figure reflects the broader risk of holding and issuing stored value rather than simply moving funds. The table below sets out how the three permission types compare.

Beyond capital, the regulator wants suitable management and shareholders, a clear safeguarding method for client money, a business plan with three-year financial projections, and a documented risk, AML, and IT framework. Our EMI licensing overview breaks each requirement down in more detail.

Section 3: The Process

How to Apply, Step by Step

The application follows a consistent sequence across the EEA. The seven steps below describe the path from first decision to live authorisation. The detail and exact document set vary by regulator, but the order is the same, and skipping ahead, for example filing before the capital is in place or the team is hired, is the most common reason applications stall. Section 4: Capital

Capital and Own Funds

Capital comes in two layers, and confusing them is a common mistake. The first is initial capital: the EUR 350,000 paid-up minimum you need in the company before the regulator will authorise it, set by EMD2. This is a fixed entry ticket. It must be genuinely paid up and held, not a paper figure, and the regulator screens the source of the money as part of fit-and-proper review.

The second layer is ongoing own funds, which scale with the business. An EMI must hold own funds equal to at least 2 percent of its average outstanding electronic money under what the rules call Method D, and never less than the EUR 350,000 floor. So a small EMI is governed by the EUR 350,000 minimum, while a larger one with substantial e-money balances must hold more, because 2 percent of its outstanding e-money exceeds the floor.

In practice, supervisors expect a buffer above the bare minimum rather than a firm operating exactly at the line, and your financial projections need to show that capital stays comfortably above the requirement as volumes grow. Underestimating future own-funds needs is a frequent cause of regulator queries, so model the capital trajectory, not just the opening figure.

Section 5: Safeguarding

Safeguarding Client Funds

Safeguarding is the requirement that protects customer money if the EMI fails, and it is one of the points regulators scrutinise most closely. The principle is simple: funds received in exchange for electronic money belong, in substance, to the customer, so they must be kept apart from the institution's own money and shielded from its creditors. An EMI can never treat client balances as working capital.

EMD2 allows three safeguarding methods. The most common is segregation: client funds sit in a dedicated account at a credit institution, ring-fenced from the EMI's insolvency. The second is investing the funds in secure, low-risk, liquid assets such as high-quality sovereign bonds or qualifying money market instruments. The third is covering the funds with an insurance policy or a comparable guarantee from an insurer or bank that pays out if the EMI defaults. Many firms combine segregation with one of the others.

Whichever method you choose, the regulator wants to see the operational detail: which bank holds the segregated account, how and how often you reconcile client money against e-money issued, and what happens on default. For crypto-payment models that need fiat accounts, this also connects to wider banking access, a theme we cover in our guide on the crypto bank account for a VASP.

From Our Practice

In our EMI work across Lithuania, Cyprus, Ireland, and Malta, the two issues that most often stall an application are substance and safeguarding. Regulators have grown wary of letterbox structures, so a credible application now needs real local presence: resident senior managers, key control functions actually based in the jurisdiction, and an operational footprint that matches the business plan rather than a single registered address.

On safeguarding, we see firms name a method but fail to evidence how it works day to day, who reconciles the client-money account, against what records, and on what cadence. Building that operational detail into the application from the start, alongside the AML and KYC framework rather than after it, is consistently the difference between a clean review and a long series of regulator questions that push a six-month timeline towards twelve.

Section 6: Jurisdiction

Choosing a Jurisdiction

Because an EMI passports across the EEA, you only license once, so the choice of home regulator is a strategic decision rather than a question of where your customers are. The main variables are language, timeline, cost, substance expectations, and how comfortable the supervisor is with fintech and crypto. Lithuania, Cyprus, Ireland, Malta, and Estonia are the regular EMI hubs, each with a different balance of these factors.

Lithuania is the most popular by a wide margin. The Bank of Lithuania operates fully in English, publishes a clear statutory timeline, and supervises a large cluster of authorised EMIs, including the EU entity of Revolut. Its application fee is EUR 1,463 for a full EMI and EUR 1,235 for a restricted EMI, and it offers a restricted EMI route with no minimum initial capital for firms that intend to operate only in Lithuania with a capped e-money volume. That restricted licence cannot passport, so most firms with cross-border plans apply for a full EMI from the outset.

None of this means Lithuania is automatically right for every business. Ireland and Malta can suit firms wanting a particular reputational profile, and substance costs differ across all of them. We help clients weigh the trade-offs rather than default to the cheapest headline. Our Lithuania EMI guide goes deeper on the most-chosen route.

Why the jurisdiction choice matters: a full EMI in any one EEA state passports into all 30, so you are choosing a regulator and a home base, not a market. Pick on timeline, language, substance cost, and crypto-readiness, because the customers you can reach are the same wherever you license. See our EMI licensing overview for how the hubs compare.

Section 7: Timeline & Passporting

Timeline and Passporting

The statutory clock and the real-world clock are different, and founders should plan for the second. In Lithuania, the Bank of Lithuania first checks within a few business days whether the file is complete, then has a statutory review period of up to three months for a full EMI, or two months for a restricted EMI, once a complete application is accepted. That statutory window only starts running when the file is genuinely complete.

In practice, applications almost never pass first review without comments, and answering each round of regulator questions takes time. Counting preparation, incorporation, hiring, drafting, submission, and the question-and-answer cycle, a realistic end-to-end timeline runs from six to twelve months. The firms that land at the faster end are those that arrive with capital paid up, substance in place, and a complete, well-evidenced file rather than a draft that needs rebuilding under questioning.

Once authorised, passporting is the payoff. An EMI does not relicense in each country. It notifies its home regulator of the EEA states it intends to serve, under freedom of services or freedom of establishment, and gains the right to operate across all 30 EEA states on its single home-state authorisation. That single-licence reach is the central commercial reason the EMI exists, and it is why where you license matters far less than getting the application right.
