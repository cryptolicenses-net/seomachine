Definition · Crypto-Friendly Bank

A crypto-friendly bank is a licensed bank or electronic money institution that will knowingly onboard a crypto business, provide fiat IBAN rails, and support on and off-ramps to exchanges and stablecoins, rather than refusing the customer because the activity touches digital assets. It still applies full anti-money-laundering checks; what differs is its risk appetite, not its compliance standard.

Section 1: The Basics

What "Crypto-Friendly" Actually Means

Almost every regulated bank in the world runs anti-money-laundering and know-your-customer checks. What separates a crypto-friendly bank is not a weaker standard but a willingness to apply that standard to a crypto business and still say yes. A genuinely crypto-friendly institution combines fiat and digital-asset infrastructure under one roof: a usable account or IBAN, payment rails, and smooth on and off-ramps to exchanges, stablecoins, and supported wallets.

The reason this is hard is structural. For years, many banks treated any crypto exposure as reputational and compliance risk to be avoided wholesale, a posture often called de-risking. A crypto-friendly provider instead builds the controls to bank the sector deliberately. Our companion guide on opening a crypto bank account for a VASP walks through what that onboarding looks like in practice.

This guide deliberately speaks in categories and regulatory regimes rather than handing you a ranked list of named banks with invented fees. The market moves too fast for that, and the right answer depends on your model, your licence, and your jurisdiction. What stays stable is the structure of the market, which is what we map below alongside our crypto banking service overview.

Section 2: The Landscape

The Four Account Categories

Crypto business banking is easiest to understand as four distinct categories rather than a single list. Each has a different licence behind it, a different scope, and a different best-fit use case. Most established crypto firms end up using more than one in parallel, because relationships in this sector can change quickly when a provider reviews its risk appetite.

The first category is the dedicated crypto bank, a full deposit-taking bank built for digital assets, as found in Switzerland. The second is the crypto-friendly electronic money institution, or EMI, which issues IBANs and moves fiat but is not a deposit-taking bank. The third is the US trust charter, which can custody crypto without taking deposits. The fourth is the traditional bank that has chosen to serve regulated crypto clients, a group that is finally growing again.

Section 3: Swiss & Liechtenstein

Switzerland's Crypto Banks

Switzerland hosts the deepest cluster of dedicated crypto banks in Europe, and the story starts in August 2019, when FINMA granted the world's first two crypto banking licences to Sygnum and SEBA, since renamed AMINA Bank. Both are anchored around Zug, the canton known as Crypto Valley, and offer regulated custody, trading, staking, and tokenization for digital assets under a full Swiss banking licence.

The Swiss advantage is regulatory clarity. FINMA issued pragmatic frameworks early for token classification and anti-money-laundering, which gave both institutions and their clients confidence to build. Beyond Sygnum and AMINA, the wider ecosystem includes Bitcoin Suisse, Hypothekarbank Lenzburg, and InCore, and neighbouring Liechtenstein offers its own blockchain-aware banking environment. For the licensing side of operating here, see our overview of the VASP licence.

For an institutional crypto business that needs deposits, custody, and a recognised banking counterparty in one place, the Swiss model is often the gold standard. The trade-off is that onboarding is rigorous and selective, and a credible licence or licensing pathway is effectively a precondition rather than a nice-to-have.

Section 4: EU EMIs

EU EMIs & Fiat Onramp Banking

For most crypto businesses, the workhorse account is not a bank at all but a crypto-friendly electronic money institution. An EMI is authorised under European e-money rules to issue dedicated IBANs and move fiat through SEPA and SWIFT, but it does not take deposits or lend. That narrower scope is exactly why EMIs are usually faster to open and more willing to onboard Web3 firms than a traditional bank.

Lithuania is the European hub for this market, having built a large EMI sector that explicitly serves crypto and payments businesses. Source-confirmed examples in the space include Bankera, a Lithuanian EMI offering crypto-friendly business accounts with European IBANs; Monerium, an EU-authorised and MiCA-compliant EMI providing fiat on and off-ramps; and Fiat Republic, an EMI authorised by De Nederlandsche Bank that supplies virtual IBANs to crypto businesses. These providers are the backbone of fiat onramp banking.

The key is that the EMI must explicitly support crypto-related activity, because not all do. This is also where Web3 banking and payment processing meet: a payment flow that converts fiat to crypto and back relies on exactly these rails. Section 5: United States

US Trusts & the 2025 Reset

The United States is the clearest example of how fast crypto banking conditions can swing. In January 2023, the Federal Reserve, FDIC, and OCC issued a joint statement warning that issuing or holding crypto on public, decentralized networks was highly likely inconsistent with safe and sound banking. When Silvergate, Signature, and Silicon Valley Bank collapsed that year, the sector lost its main banking rails, an episode many in the industry call Operation Chokepoint 2.0.

Where US firms could still bank crypto, they leaned on trust charters, which custody digital assets without taking deposits. The main routes are a Wyoming Special Purpose Depository Institution, a New York limited purpose trust charter from NYDFS, and a national trust bank charter from the OCC. In December 2025 the OCC conditionally approved five crypto-focused firms, including Ripple, BitGo, Paxos, and Circle, for national trust charters, the first such wave since Anchorage in 2021.

Policy has since reversed sharply. In April 2025 the Federal Reserve formally withdrew its crypto-asset guidance for banks. In 2026 the OCC and FDIC finalised the removal of reputation risk from bank supervision, and the agencies reissued fifteen interagency documents stripping reputation-risk references, explicitly to stop the misuse of that concept to deny lawful businesses banking access. The OCC also confirmed national banks may hold digital assets as principal and run riskless-principal crypto transactions.

Why this matters for licensing: the US reset means the route into mainstream banking now runs through clear licensing and charter status rather than around it. A firm that holds a recognised charter or licence is far better positioned with US banks in 2026 than one operating without. See our crypto banking overview for how this fits a wider banking strategy.

From Our Practice

The single most common reason a crypto business cannot bank is not the sector itself but the absence of a clean compliance story. When clients come to us after being refused, the gap is almost always the same: no licence, an incomplete AML and KYC framework, or no documented Travel Rule arrangement. Banks and EMIs are not refusing crypto in principle, they are refusing risk they cannot evidence.

We also see firms learn the hard way that a single banking relationship is fragile. A provider quietly reviews its risk appetite, a counterparty exits, and a perfectly compliant business is suddenly offline. We build banking strategies with deliberate redundancy across a crypto bank, one or more EMIs, and where useful a trust charter, so no single decision can halt operations.

Section 6: Singapore & Asia

Singapore & the Asian Model

Singapore approaches crypto banking differently. The Monetary Authority of Singapore does not issue a dedicated crypto banking licence. Instead, it licenses Digital Payment Token service providers under the Payment Services Act 2019, usually through a Major Payment Institution licence that covers trading, custody, and exchange of digital assets. That licence is the credential a crypto business needs to access banking and payment infrastructure in the city-state.

On the banking side, Singapore runs five MAS-licensed digital banks, namely Trust Bank, GXS Bank, MariBank, ANEXT Bank, and Green Link Digital Bank, with no new licences currently being issued. In April 2026, MAS went further and proposed a more flexible prudential approach to cryptoassets issued on public blockchains, giving banks a workable path to hold such assets without facing prohibitive capital charges where risks are adequately mitigated.

The Asian model is therefore licence-led: secure the right MAS authorisation, and the banking relationships become realistic. It is a useful contrast to Europe's institution-led model and the US charter system, and it reinforces the central theme of this guide, that the licence is the key that unlocks the account.

Section 7: The Key Lever

How a Licence Unlocks Banking

Across every region above, one factor decides whether a crypto business can bank: whether it holds a licence. A VASP, CASP, or DPT licence is not just a permission to operate. It is documented proof of your AML and KYC framework, your Travel Rule controls, and your standing with a regulator, which is exactly the evidence a bank or EMI needs to clear its own de-risking checks.
