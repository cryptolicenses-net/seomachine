Quick Answer · Crypto Payment Processing

Crypto payment processing is the infrastructure that moves value between fiat and crypto for businesses: fiat-to-crypto on-ramps, crypto-to-fiat off-ramps, merchant settlement, and stablecoin rails. It splits into a fiat leg, licensed as a Payment Institution or EMI under PSD2, and a crypto leg, licensed as a MiCA CASP. Both legs sit under one AML and Travel Rule framework.

Key Takeaways
  • Crypto payment processing has two legs: a fiat leg (card acquiring, settlement, e-money) and a crypto leg (custody, exchange, transfer of crypto-assets)
  • The fiat leg needs a Payment Institution or EMI authorisation under PSD2 and EMD2; the crypto leg needs a MiCA CASP authorisation in the EU
  • PI initial capital runs from EUR 20,000 to EUR 125,000 by service; an EMI requires EUR 350,000 and can store customer value
  • From 2 March 2026, the EBA treats stablecoin (EMT) transfers by CASPs as payment services under PSD2, triggering a PI or EMI licence
  • Crypto and related sectors are often high-risk for acquirers; crypto and stablecoin settlement is used to cut rolling reserves and de-risking exposure
Section 1: The Basics

What Crypto Payment Processing Actually Is

Crypto payment processing is the plumbing that lets a business send or receive money where crypto is involved on at least one side of the transaction. A customer might pay with a card and the merchant receives stablecoins, or a customer pays in Bitcoin and the merchant is settled in euros. The processor sits between the public blockchain and the traditional banking system, converting, routing, and settling value while keeping each side whole.

The crucial thing to understand from the outset is that a crypto payment processor is rarely one regulated activity. It is usually two. There is a fiat leg, which looks like classic payments: acquiring card transactions, holding client money, issuing e-money, and settling to bank accounts. And there is a crypto leg, which involves custodying, exchanging, or transferring crypto-assets on behalf of clients. Each leg attracts a different licence family, which is why so many founders underestimate the authorisation burden.

Because this is a payments business and not just a crypto business, the rulebook is the established one for payment services in the EU, principally PSD2 and the E-Money Directive, layered with MiCA for the crypto side. Section 2: How It Works

How Crypto Payments Actually Move

At a high level, crypto payment processing comes down to three flows. The first is the on-ramp: a customer pays in fiat by card or bank transfer, and the processor converts that fiat into crypto. The second is the off-ramp: a customer pays in crypto, and the processor converts it into fiat or a stablecoin for the merchant. The third is settlement: getting the agreed value to the merchant, either in their bank account or directly to a wallet.

Stablecoin rails sit at the centre of the modern model. Because a fiat-pegged stablecoin like USDC or USDT holds a steady value, a processor can accept a card payment, convert it, and settle the merchant in stablecoins paid to their own wallet, with no acquiring bank holding the float in between. This is the mechanism behind much of the 2026 shift toward crypto settlement, which we return to in the high-risk section below.

Every one of these flows touches both regulated worlds. Holding the customer's fiat, even briefly, is a payment or e-money activity. Converting to or from crypto, and moving crypto on the customer's behalf, is a crypto-asset service. That is why mapping the flow precisely is the first step in scoping which licences a given model actually needs.

Section 3: The Licences

PI vs EMI vs MiCA CASP

Three authorisations dominate crypto payment processing in the EU, and they do different jobs. A Payment Institution (PI) under PSD2 can execute transfers, acquire card payments, and remit money, but it cannot store customer value over time. Initial capital scales with the service: around EUR 20,000 for money remittance only, EUR 50,000 for payment initiation, and EUR 125,000 for executing transfers, acquiring, and card issuing.

An Electronic Money Institution (EMI) does everything a PI does and adds the one thing a PI cannot: issuing and redeeming electronic money, and holding stored value in customer wallets or IBANs. That extra power comes with a flat EUR 350,000 initial capital requirement under the E-Money Directive. If your model holds customer balances, pre-funds wallets, or issues e-money, you are in EMI territory. Our step-by-step guide to getting an EMI licence walks through that process in full.

The third piece is the MiCA crypto-asset service provider (CASP) authorisation, which covers the crypto leg: custody of crypto-assets, exchanging crypto for funds or other crypto, and transferring crypto-assets on behalf of clients. A processor that custodies or moves crypto for customers generally needs CASP authorisation alongside its payment licence, not instead of it.

Section 4: Stablecoins

Stablecoins and the 2 March 2026 Rule

The single most important regulatory development for crypto payments in the EU is the way stablecoins now interact with payment law. Most fiat-pegged stablecoins are classified under MiCA as electronic money tokens (EMTs). The European Banking Authority issued a No-Action Letter clarifying that when a crypto-asset service provider transfers EMTs on behalf of clients, that activity should be treated as a payment service under PSD2.

The practical effect arrives with a date. Before 2 March 2026, EMT transactions by CASPs did not require a separate PSD2 authorisation. From that date, national competent authorities began treating those transfers as payment services, so a CASP moving stablecoins for clients must hold a Payment Institution or EMI authorisation in addition to its MiCA licence. The EBA deliberately steered away from full dual authorisation, encouraging regulators to reuse the information already filed for CASP licensing to streamline the payment authorisation.

For payment processors built on stablecoin rails, this is not a fringe technicality, it is the licensing model. If your settlement layer moves USDC or USDT for merchants, you are very likely in scope, and the choice between a PI and an EMI will hinge on whether you also store that value. Founders who built around the assumption that a CASP licence alone covered stablecoin payments have had to revisit their structure in 2026.

Why this matters for licensing: a stablecoin settlement model that looked like a pure MiCA CASP business in 2025 may need a PI or EMI authorisation in 2026. Build the payment authorisation into the plan from day one rather than retrofitting it after the CASP licence is granted. Section 5: High-Risk Merchants

High-Risk Merchants, Chargebacks & Rolling Reserves

Card networks and acquiring banks sort merchants by risk, and crypto-adjacent sectors frequently land in the high-risk bucket. The classification turns on chargeback history, fraud exposure, the merchant's industry and geography, and how predictable the revenue is. Once a business is high-risk, the economics change sharply and not in the merchant's favour.

High-risk merchants typically face transaction fees that industry sources put at roughly 4 to 8 percent, against around 2.9 percent for mainstream merchants, along with rolling reserves of about 5 to 15 percent withheld for 6 to 12 months and chargeback fees of USD 25 to 100 per dispute. A rolling reserve is essentially insurance the processor holds against future chargebacks: the processor keeps the float and the merchant loses the cash flow. Worse, if an acquiring bank decides to exit an entire merchant category, the business can be cut off regardless of its own clean record.

This is where crypto and stablecoin settlement has gained ground in 2026. Crypto-settlement gateways let merchants accept Visa and Mastercard and receive settlement directly in USDC, USDT, or Bitcoin, paid to their own wallet, bypassing the traditional acquiring-bank reserve model. Legitimate merchants with clean histories can reduce or avoid rolling reserves, though the card network rules and underwriting still apply on the acceptance side. Section 6: AML & Travel Rule

The AML and Travel Rule Overlay

Whatever licence stack a crypto payment processor holds, an AML and counter-terrorist-financing programme sits on top of all of it. For the crypto leg, the FATF Travel Rule requires that when the processor transfers crypto-assets on behalf of clients, it collects and shares originator and beneficiary data with the counterparty provider. The EU Transfer of Funds Regulation applies this with no de-minimis threshold, while the US FinCEN recordkeeping rule under 31 CFR 1010.410 applies at USD 3,000.

In the United States the AML obligation is bound up with the licence itself. A business that accepts and transmits convertible virtual currency, or buys and sells it, is generally a money transmitter and therefore a money services business. It must register with FinCEN by filing Form 107, operate a written AML programme, and meet recordkeeping and reporting duties including suspicious activity and currency transaction reports. State money transmitter licences usually apply on top of the federal registration.

For a crypto payment processor, AML is not a separate workstream bolted on at the end. The same customer identification that underpins onboarding feeds the Travel Rule data, the sanctions screening, and the transaction monitoring.
