Crypto Accounting: IFRS vs US GAAP for Digital Assets Explained in 2026

IFRS and US GAAP now treat digital assets very differently. IFRS routes crypto holdings through IAS 38 intangible assets or IAS 2 inventory, while FASB ASU 2023-08 moved US GAAP to fair value through net income. This guide explains both frameworks, the divergence, and what it means for crypto firms reporting in 2026.

In Brief · Crypto Accounting IFRS vs US GAAP

IFRS has no crypto-specific standard: a digital asset holding is an intangible asset under IAS 38, measured at cost or the revaluation model, or inventory under IAS 2 for traders. US GAAP, after FASB ASU 2023-08, measures in-scope crypto at fair value with gains and losses in net income. The result is a clear divergence between the two frameworks.

Key Takeaways
  • IFRS has no standard written for crypto; under the IFRIC June 2019 agenda decision, holdings are intangible assets under IAS 38, or inventory under IAS 2 if held for sale by traders
  • Under IAS 38 the entity picks the cost model or the revaluation model; revaluation gains go to other comprehensive income, not net income, and require an active market
  • FASB ASU 2023-08 moved US GAAP to fair value through net income for in-scope crypto, replacing the old cost-less-impairment model
  • ASU 2023-08 is effective for fiscal years beginning after 15 December 2024, with early adoption permitted
  • The divergence means the same Bitcoin holding can show very different net income and equity under IFRS versus US GAAP
Section 1: The Basics

Crypto Accounting Basics

Crypto accounting is the discipline of recognising, measuring, presenting, and disclosing digital assets in financial statements. The central problem is that neither IFRS nor US GAAP was designed with cryptocurrencies in mind. Standard setters have had to fit Bitcoin, Ether, and similar tokens into rules written for other asset classes, and the two frameworks have reached different answers.

The first question in any digital asset accounting analysis is classification. A crypto holding is generally not cash, because it is not legal tender backed by a central bank, and not a financial asset, because it is not a contractual right to receive cash or another financial instrument. That conclusion, reached for IFRS by the IFRS Interpretations Committee in June 2019, pushes crypto into the intangible asset rules by default. Our crypto accounting hub sets out the full lifecycle from recognition through audit.

Once classified, measurement is where IFRS and US GAAP part company. IFRS keeps most treasury crypto at cost under the intangible asset model. US GAAP, since FASB ASU 2023-08, marks qualifying crypto to fair value each reporting date. The sections below take each framework in turn, then set them side by side and explain why the gap matters for any business that holds, trades, or reports digital assets.

Section 2: IFRS

Crypto Accounting Under IFRS

There is no IFRS standard written specifically for crypto. The authoritative guidance is the IFRS Interpretations Committee agenda decision of June 2019, "Holdings of Cryptocurrencies." The Committee concluded that a cryptocurrency holding is neither cash under IAS 7 nor a financial asset under IAS 32, because it does not give the holder a contractual right to receive a fixed or determinable number of currency units. That leaves two homes for crypto under IFRS, depending on why the entity holds it.

IAS 38 intangible assets (the default)

For most holders, a digital asset is an intangible asset under IAS 38, because it is identifiable, non-monetary, and separable. IAS 38 offers two measurement choices. The cost model carries crypto at cost less any impairment; an indefinite-life asset like Bitcoin is not amortised but is impairment-tested, with losses recognised in profit or loss and limited reversal allowed if value recovers. The revaluation model is available only where an active market exists, and crucially, upward revaluation gains are credited to other comprehensive income as a revaluation surplus rather than to net income.

IAS 2 inventory (for traders)

If an entity holds crypto for sale in the ordinary course of business, the holding is inventory under IAS 2 rather than IAS 38. Commodity broker-traders that acquire crypto principally to sell in the near term and generate a profit from price fluctuations may measure it at fair value less costs to sell, with changes recognised in profit or loss. This is the one IFRS route that brings unrealised crypto gains into the income statement. For a deeper treatment, see our dedicated page on crypto accounting under IFRS.

Section 3: US GAAP

Crypto Accounting Under US GAAP

US GAAP underwent a far bigger change. Before December 2023, crypto was treated as an indefinite-lived intangible asset under ASC 350, measured at cost less impairment. That model was widely criticised because it was asymmetric: holders had to write the asset down whenever its market price dropped below carrying value, but could never write it back up when the price recovered, no matter how far. Balance sheets carried Bitcoin at the lowest price it had touched, badly understating economic value during bull markets.

FASB ASU 2023-08, "Intangibles, Goodwill and Other, Crypto Assets (Subtopic 350-60)," issued on 13 December 2023, replaced that approach. In-scope crypto is now measured at fair value under ASC 820, with changes in fair value recognised in net income each reporting period, and presented separately from other intangible assets on the balance sheet. Unrealised gains and losses flow straight through earnings, so reported income now moves with crypto prices in both directions.

The standard is effective for fiscal years beginning after 15 December 2024, including interim periods within those years, with early adoption permitted. Entities transition using a modified retrospective method, recording a cumulative-effect adjustment to the opening balance of retained earnings in the year of adoption. Our page on crypto accounting under US GAAP walks through the disclosures the standard requires.

"The single biggest shift is psychological. Under the old US GAAP, finance teams managed crypto as a one-way impairment risk. Under ASU 2023-08, fair value runs through net income in both directions, so earnings now swing with the market. IFRS reporters do not get that symmetry by default, which is why dual-framework groups need to model both before they pick a reporting basis."

Section 5: The Divergence

Why the Divergence Matters

For most of the last decade, IFRS and US GAAP reached broadly similar outcomes for crypto: both treated it as a cost-based intangible asset, and both ignored unrealised gains. ASU 2023-08 ended that alignment for US GAAP. From fiscal years beginning after 15 December 2024, a US GAAP filer marks Bitcoin to fair value through net income, while an IFRS reporter holding the identical position keeps it at cost or routes revaluation gains to other comprehensive income.

The practical effect is that the same balance sheet item can produce materially different reported figures. In a rising market, a US GAAP filer books unrealised gains that lift net income and equity, whereas an IFRS reporter under the cost model shows neither until disposal. In a falling market, the US GAAP filer recognises the loss in earnings immediately, while the IFRS reporter records an impairment that behaves differently and can later reverse. For groups that report under both frameworks, or that are comparing peers across the Atlantic, this divergence has to be modelled rather than assumed away.

It also feeds into tax and capital. Book values flow into tax computations and into regulatory capital calculations, so a framework that recognises unrealised gains can change taxable profit and own funds even before any crypto is sold, subject to local rules on realisation. Section 6: ASU 2023-08 Scope

What Falls Inside ASU 2023-08

Fair value treatment under US GAAP is not automatic for everything labelled crypto. ASU 2023-08 applies only to assets that meet all of its scope conditions, and the boundaries matter because anything outside the scope stays on the old cost-less-impairment model under ASC 350. To qualify, an asset must meet the definition of an intangible asset, give the holder no enforceable rights or claims to underlying goods, services, or other assets, reside on a distributed ledger using blockchain or similar technology, be secured through cryptography, be fungible, and not be created or issued by the reporting entity or its related parties.

That definition cleanly captures Bitcoin and Ether, the assets most often cited as in scope. It just as cleanly excludes several categories. Non-fungible tokens fail the fungibility test. Wrapped tokens and any token that confers enforceable rights to other assets fail the no-enforceable-rights test. Tokens issued by the reporting entity or a related party are excluded outright, which keeps issuers' own coins out of fair value treatment. Assets that fall outside ASU 2023-08 continue under the legacy intangible model, so a single crypto business can end up applying two measurement bases at once.

Asset In ASU 2023-08 Scope? Why
Bitcoin, Ether Yes Fungible, cryptographic, no enforceable rights, third-party issued
NFTs No Not fungible
Wrapped tokens No Confer enforceable rights to other assets

Section 7: For Crypto Firms

What This Means for Crypto Firms

Whatever framework applies, the accounting policy is now part of the licensing and banking conversation, not a year-end chore. The five steps below describe the core sequence a crypto firm should run when setting its digital asset accounting. The detail varies by framework and holding, but the order is consistent and works for IFRS and US GAAP alike. Our crypto accounting service delivers each step as part of a licensing engagement.

1
Framework
Confirm your reporting basis
Determine whether you report under IFRS or US GAAP, since the measurement of digital assets differs fundamentally between the two from fiscal years beginning after 15 December 2024.
2
Classify
Classify each holding
Decide whether each asset is treasury (IAS 38 intangible) or trading inventory (IAS 2), and under US GAAP whether it meets all six ASU 2023-08 scope conditions.
3
Measure
Pick the measurement model
Apply fair value through net income under ASU 2023-08, or choose the IAS 38 cost or revaluation model, or IAS 2 fair value less costs to sell for broker-traders.
4
Disclose
Present and disclose
Present crypto separately on the balance sheet where required, and disclose policy, fair value inputs, impairment, and significant holdings in line with the standard.
5
Audit & license
Make it auditable and licence-ready
Document the policy, retain pricing and valuation evidence, and align it with capital, tax, and safeguarding rules so it withstands audit and supports your VASP or CASP application.
