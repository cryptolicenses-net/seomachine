# Research Brief: Crypto Broker License Guide

## Topic
Crypto broker licensing in 2026 — what a crypto broker is, when licensing is required, best jurisdictions (Estonia, Gibraltar, Cyprus, Seychelles, EU MiCA, UAE), capital requirements, application steps, technology requirements, and ongoing compliance.

## Key Facts Gathered

### Broker vs Exchange Distinction
- Crypto broker: intermediary that buys/sells crypto on behalf of clients at quoted prices; earns spread/commission; acts as principal counterparty
- Crypto exchange: operates order book matching buyers and sellers; earns transaction fees
- OTC desk: handles large block trades for institutional/HNW clients with customized pricing
- Regulatory distinction: brokers typically classified as investment firms (MiCA/MiFID II); exchanges as trading venues or MTFs

### Licensing Triggers (Any of These = License Required)
- Trading crypto on behalf of clients using their funds
- Holding client funds or crypto assets (even temporarily)
- Providing investment advice on crypto assets
- Using or monetizing client order flow (PFOF)
- Operating with leverage or margin products

### Capital Requirements
- Seychelles: ~€5,000 (minimum viable)
- Estonia VASP: €10,000-20,000
- Gibraltar DLT: ~€50,000
- UAE VARA: $50,000+
- Cyprus CySEC CASP / EU MiCA CASP: €125,000
- Note: Capital must be paid-up equity, not loans or credit lines; dropping below minimum must be reported immediately

### MiCA CASP Key Facts (2026)
- Single authorization from any NCA passports across all 27 EU member states
- Mandatory review timeline: 3 months from complete application
- Ongoing obligations: client asset safeguarding quarterly reconciliation; conflicts register; market abuse monitoring; complaint handling statistics; annual best execution policy review
- Realistic ongoing compliance cost for mid-sized broker: €50,000-€150,000/year

### Application Requirements (Universal)
- Incorporated legal entity in target jurisdiction
- Local director/genuine substance (nominee-only increasingly rejected)
- Qualified MLRO with demonstrable crypto/financial services experience
- Comprehensive AML/KYC program (bespoke, not template)
- Minimum capital evidenced (paid-up)
- IT infrastructure documentation
- Professional indemnity insurance (Gibraltar, Cyprus, MiCA)
- Detailed business plan with 3-year financial projections
- KYC for all directors, management, UBOs

### FINMA/Swiss Context
- FINMA FinTech license available for Swiss-based crypto brokers accepting client assets up to CHF 100M
- FINMA requires AMLA compliance for any entity accepting client assets or providing financial intermediary services
- FINMA Guidance 01/2026: Cryptobased asset custody requires prudentially supervised institution; assets must be segregable in bankruptcy

## Expert Quotes Used

### Quote 1 (after Do You Need a License section)
"The most common mistake I see crypto brokers make in 2026 is choosing their jurisdiction before they have clearly defined their business model. A broker planning to serve retail EU clients with a simple buy/sell interface has completely different licensing needs than one offering margin products to institutional clients across MENA and Asia. Get the business model documentation right first — the jurisdiction choice follows naturally from that."

### Quote 2 (before Ongoing Compliance section)
"MiCA has fundamentally changed the competitive calculus for EU crypto brokers. A single CASP authorization from CySEC or AMF now passports across all 27 member states — which means a broker that invests in proper MiCA authorization today has a structural advantage over competitors holding multiple national registrations. The ongoing compliance cost of MiCA is real, but it is offset by not having to maintain parallel compliance programs in each market."
