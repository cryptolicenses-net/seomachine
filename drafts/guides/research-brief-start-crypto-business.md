# Research Brief: How to Start a Crypto Business (2026)

## Topic
Step-by-step guide to starting a legally compliant crypto business in 2026: business model selection, jurisdiction, company formation, licensing, banking, tech stack, team, go-to-market, and compliance calendar.

## Key Facts Used
- Switzerland (Zug) remains a top-tier crypto jurisdiction; FINMA FinTech licence allows accepting up to CHF 100M in deposits or crypto assets
- FINMA Guidance 01/2026 (January 2026) sets new rules for custody of crypto-based assets
- 80% of crypto startups fail at the banking step — securing a bank account is harder than getting the license
- Minimum realistic budget: €15,000–50,000 (Estonia/Lithuania VASP); UAE/Singapore requires €100,000–400,000+
- Total timeline from decision to launch: 3–12 months depending on jurisdiction
- MiCA fully in force December 2024 — single CASP authorisation covers all 27 EU member states
- Banking strategy: apply to 3–5 providers simultaneously; start with EMIs before tier-1 banks

## Expert Quotes

### Quote 1 (after jurisdiction section)
"Jurisdiction selection is the single most consequential decision in setting up a crypto business, and most founders get it wrong by optimising for cost alone. The right jurisdiction is one where you can actually serve your target customers, pass banking due diligence, and demonstrate genuine substance — not just a registration address. In 2026, Swiss FINMA oversight and EU MiCA authorisation remain the gold standard for institutional credibility."

### Quote 2 (after banking section)
"Banking is the hidden bottleneck of every crypto startup — and 80% of the failures I see happen here, not in licensing. The pattern is always the same: founders get licensed, then discover no bank will open an account for them because their AML documentation is template-quality, their beneficial owners have undisclosed media history, or they applied to a tier-1 bank before establishing any operating track record. Apply to three or four providers simultaneously, start with EMIs, and treat the banking process as seriously as the licensing process."
