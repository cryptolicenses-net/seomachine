Answer · Offshore vs Onshore Crypto License

An offshore crypto license (Cayman, BVI, Seychelles) is cheaper, faster, and zero-tax, but comes with harder banking access and heavier FATF scrutiny. An onshore license (EU MiCA, Switzerland) costs more in capital and fees but delivers banking relationships, EU passporting, and credibility. The right answer turns on which markets you serve and who will bank you.

Key Takeaways
  • Offshore licenses win on speed, cost, and 0% tax; BVI totals roughly USD 15,000 to 40,000 and Cayman VASP registration runs 2 to 4 months
  • Onshore licenses win on banking access, EU passporting across 30 EEA jurisdictions, and counterparty credibility, with MiCA own funds of EUR 50,000 to 150,000
  • The decisive variable is banking: a March 2026 FATF report flagged offshore VASPs as a heightened illicit-finance risk, hardening bank due diligence
  • Economic substance and the OECD Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework have narrowed the old offshore opacity advantage
  • Many serious operators hold both: an EU MiCA entity for Europe and an offshore VASP for non-EU markets
Section 1: The Basics

Offshore vs Onshore, Defined

An offshore crypto license is issued by a low-tax, light-footprint jurisdiction whose chief appeal is speed, cost, and a zero corporate tax rate. The flagship offshore regimes for virtual assets are the Cayman Islands, the British Virgin Islands, Seychelles, and St Vincent and the Grenadines. An onshore crypto license, by contrast, comes from a large regulated market, most importantly the European Union under MiCA and Switzerland under FINMA, where the cost and capital are higher but the licence carries weight with banks, counterparties, and institutional clients.

The labels are not about geography so much as regulatory posture. An offshore jurisdiction optimises for ease of entry; an onshore jurisdiction optimises for market integrity and access. Both are legitimate. The mistake is treating the decision as a pure cost comparison. The figure that matters most rarely appears on a government fee schedule: it is whether a bank will open and keep an account for your licensed entity. For a broader jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction view, see our guide on the best crypto license jurisdiction.

This guide is deliberately balanced. Offshore is not a loophole and onshore is not always worth the premium. The right choice depends on which customers you serve, whether you need fiat banking and EU passporting, and how much regulatory credibility your business model demands. We map both sides honestly below, starting with the genuine strengths of going offshore.

Section 2: The Offshore Case

Why Founders Choose Offshore

The appeal of offshore licensing is concrete and real. Government fees are lower, timelines are shorter, and the headline tax rate is zero on corporate income, capital gains, and withholding. For an early-stage exchange or a business serving non-EU markets, this can be the difference between launching this year and not launching at all. Our overview of the offshore crypto license options sets out the leading regimes side by side.

The Cayman Islands regulate virtual assets through CIMA under a two-tier model: a VASP registration for lower-risk activity and a full VASP licence for higher-risk services such as exchanges and custody. Registration takes roughly two to four months and the full licence six to ten months, with minimum paid-up share capital of USD 100,000, a CIMA-approved director, and a local AML officer. The detail is in our Cayman Islands crypto license page.

The British Virgin Islands register VASPs under the VASP Act 2022, in force since February 2023, across three categories: Custody Provider, Exchange, and General VASP. The BVI sets no fixed statutory minimum capital, instead expecting evidence of six to twelve months of operating reserves, with total costs commonly between USD 15,000 and 40,000 and approval in three to six months. Seychelles rounds out the picture: previously FATF grey-listed, it was confirmed off the list at the February 2026 plenary, which has eased correspondent banking friction for properly structured firms. If cost is the dominant constraint, our cheapest crypto licenses guide ranks the lowest-entry routes.

The honest caveat: offshore strengths come with offshore costs that do not appear on a fee schedule. Banking is harder, economic substance is mandatory rather than optional, and a March 2026 FATF report singled out offshore VASPs as a heightened illicit-finance risk. Cheap to obtain is not the same as cheap to operate.

Section 3: The Onshore Case

Why Serious Operators Go Onshore

Onshore licensing costs more and asks more, and that is precisely the point. The premium buys access and credibility that an offshore licence cannot replicate. The clearest example is the European Union, where a single authorisation under MiCA, Regulation (EU) 2023/1114, lets a crypto-asset service provider passport across all 27 member states plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway, covering 30 EEA jurisdictions, without separate national licences. No offshore regime offers anything comparable. Our European crypto license hub covers the MiCA routes country by country.

MiCA sets minimum own funds of EUR 50,000 for advisory and order-transmission services, EUR 125,000 for exchanges and trading platforms, and EUR 150,000 for custody, with own funds also covering at least a quarter of the prior year's fixed overheads. These thresholds are identical across the EEA, and the MiCA transition that consolidates legacy national registrations is reaching its conclusion in 2026. The capital is real money, but it is the price of the single largest regulated crypto market on earth.

Switzerland offers a different flavour of onshore credibility. Under FINMA and the DLT Act, and anchored in the Crypto Valley cluster around Zug, a Swiss authorisation carries exceptional reputational weight and unlocks relationships with crypto-friendly Swiss banks. It is not cheap and not fast, but for institutional-facing businesses it can be decisive. Onshore, in short, is the route when banking, passporting, and trust matter more than speed and cost.

"Clients arrive fixated on the cheapest licence, and we have to reframe the question. The licence fee is a rounding error next to the cost of not being able to bank. A BVI VASP that no bank will touch is more expensive than a Cayman or EU entity that opens accounts in week one. We price the whole operating reality, not the application."

Section 5: The Banking Reality

The Banking Reality Nobody Quotes Upfront

Every comparison of crypto licenses eventually collides with the same wall: fiat banking. A licence authorises you to operate, but it does not oblige any bank to serve you, and this is where offshore and onshore diverge most sharply. Offshore VASPs face correspondent de-risking, where banks withdraw from entire categories of jurisdiction to avoid compliance exposure, and the friction has intensified since FATF turned its attention to offshore providers.

On 11 March 2026, FATF published a dedicated report on offshore VASPs, warning that oversight gaps enable fraud, money laundering, and terrorist financing through regulatory arbitrage and nested relationships, where unlicensed offshore providers access services from a licensed VASP by posing as private customers. The report found that under half of surveyed jurisdictions apply activity-based supervision and urged banks and VASPs to refrain from relationships with unlicensed or unregistered offshore providers. That guidance lands directly on the compliance teams who decide whether to bank your entity.

Reputation also flows through the FATF grey list, formally the list of jurisdictions under increased monitoring, which held 23 countries at the February 2026 plenary. Licensing in a grey-listed jurisdiction triggers enhanced due diligence, higher fees, and outright de-risking. The opacity that once made offshore attractive has eroded further under the OECD Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework, which drives automatic cross-border exchange of crypto account information. Onshore firms, by contrast, generally bank without these handicaps, which is why credibility-dependent and institution-facing businesses so often pay the onshore premium.

From Our Practice

Across our offshore and onshore licensing work in more than 60 jurisdictions, the pattern is consistent: founders underweight banking until it blocks them. We have seen perfectly valid BVI and Seychelles VASPs sit idle for months because no bank would onboard them, while a comparable EU CASP was transacting within weeks. The licence was never the bottleneck; the account was.

The structure we recommend most often is not offshore or onshore but both. A substance-backed EU MiCA entity handles European market access and banking, while an offshore VASP serves non-EU regions and isolates regulatory exposure. The arrangement only works if it is transparent and genuinely staffed, because regulators now scrutinise nested and shell structures closely, exactly the risk FATF flagged in its 2026 offshore report. We build that substance in from day one rather than bolting it on after a bank says no.

Section 6: How to Choose

How to Choose Between Them

The decision reduces to a short sequence of questions about markets, banking, and credibility. Work through the five steps below in order, because each one can settle the matter before you reach the next. The aim is to match the licence to the business, not the business to a fee schedule.

1
Markets
Define who you will serve
If you intend to serve EU residents on a regulated basis, MiCA authorisation is effectively mandatory and offshore will not grant lawful market access. If your customers are genuinely non-EU, offshore stays on the table.
2
Banking
Map your fiat banking need
If your model depends on smooth fiat rails, card processing, and institutional accounts, weight onshore heavily. If you operate crypto-to-crypto with minimal fiat touchpoints, offshore friction is more manageable.
3
Credibility
Gauge the trust your clients demand
Institutional clients, fundraising, and counterparty due diligence reward an onshore licence. Retail-light or regionally focused models can tolerate the lighter reputational footprint of an offshore VASP.
4
Budget & speed
Test cost and timeline against runway
If capital is constrained and time-to-market is critical, offshore speed and zero tax matter. If you can fund EUR 50,000 to 150,000 in own funds and wait out a deeper review, onshore is viable.
5
Structure
Consider a dual-entity model
Many groups pair an EU MiCA entity for Europe with an offshore VASP for the rest of the world. Build genuine substance into both so the structure survives the nested-relationship scrutiny FATF now applies.
