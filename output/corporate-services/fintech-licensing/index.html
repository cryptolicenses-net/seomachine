Overview

Why Niche FinTech Licensing Matters

The term "fintech licence" covers a broad spectrum of regulatory authorisations. A crypto exchange licence, a forex dealer licence, and an EMI licence are all fintech licences — but so are gambling licences for crypto casinos, crowdfunding platform authorisations, robo-advisor registrations, and neobank charters. Each has different capital requirements, regulatory bodies, application processes, and ongoing compliance obligations.

Choosing the wrong licence type — or operating without the correct licence — is one of the most common mistakes fintech founders make. A payment aggregator that inadvertently takes on characteristics of a lending product may need a separate lending licence. A crypto platform that offers staking or yield products may trigger securities regulation. Understanding exactly which licence(s) apply to your business model is the first step.

We work with fintech operators at all stages — from pre-launch planning through to multi-licence portfolio management. Beyond crypto exchange and forex dealer licences, fintech operators can obtain: crypto gambling licences (Malta MGA, Curaçao, Anjouan), P2P lending licences (EU ECSPR, UK FCA, US SEC), crowdfunding platform licences, neobank charters or EMI licences, robo-advisor registrations, BNPL provider authorisations, insurance distribution licences (insurtech), and DLT/blockchain-specific licences in Switzerland, Malta, and Liechtenstein.
Timelines vary by licence type and jurisdiction. Crypto gambling licences in Curaçao or Anjouan typically take 2–4 months. EU crowdfunding (ECSPR) authorisation takes 3–6 months. A US SEC-registered investment adviser (for robo-advisors) takes 4–6 months. UK FCA authorisation for P2P lending platforms takes 12–24 months. Singapore MAS MPI licence takes 6–12 months.
Yes, and many successful fintech platforms hold multiple licences across different jurisdictions and service types. Holding company structures are commonly used to separate regulated entities. For example, a crypto platform might hold a VASP licence in Lithuania, a gambling licence in Curaçao, and a payment institution licence in the UK — all under one group holding company.
A neobank licence refers to a digital banking authorisation. In practice, most neobanks use an EMI (Electronic Money Institution) licence, which allows current accounts, debit cards, and payments but not lending or deposit-taking. Full banking licences are required for credit products and are significantly more expensive to obtain. Lithuania, UK, and Malta are the most popular EU EMI jurisdictions.
A banking licence authorises deposit-taking, credit extension, and a full range of financial services — requiring substantial capital (typically €5M–€30M+) and full prudential regulation. Fintech licences are narrower authorisations for specific services: an EMI licence for e-money and payments, a VASP licence for crypto, a P2P lending licence for marketplace lending. Fintech licences have lower capital requirements and lighter regulatory burden but more restricted service scope.
Initial licensing costs range from CHF 50,000 to CHF 300,000 depending on the licence type and complexity of your operations. The Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA) charges application fees based on risk assessment, while legal and consulting fees typically add CHF 30,000 to CHF 100,000. Annual regulatory fees for maintaining your licence are generally between CHF 10,000 and CHF 50,000.

Swiss fintech licences do not have fixed renewal dates but are subject to annual supervision and compliance reviews. You must file annual regulatory reports demonstrating ongoing compliance with AML/KYC requirements, capital adequacy, and operational standards. FINMA may conduct on-site inspections every 2-3 years, and failure to maintain compliance standards can result in licence suspension or revocation.
Non-compliance can result in substantial fines up to CHF 5 million, licence suspension, criminal prosecution of management, and reputational damage that impacts customer trust. Fintech companies in Zug benefit from competitive corporate tax rates around 11-12% at the cantonal level, plus federal tax. Switzerland's FINMA licensing is generally faster (6-12 months) compared to EU frameworks that often take 12-18 months through national regulators and ECB approval. However, Swiss fintech licences only provide access to the Swiss market, while EU licences under the PSD2 or MiCA frameworks enable passporting to all 27 EU member states. Capital requirements are similar across jurisdictions, but Switzerland offers lower cantonal taxes and a more stable regulatory environment, making it preferable for long-term operations.
