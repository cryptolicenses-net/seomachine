Overview

Crypto Casino vs Traditional Online Casino

A crypto casino is an online gambling platform that primarily or exclusively accepts cryptocurrency as its payment method. Unlike traditional online casinos that rely on credit card processors, bank transfers, and e-wallets, crypto casinos use blockchain wallets for deposits and withdrawals — enabling near-instant settlement, lower fees, and borderless access for players.

The key technical distinction is in the payment layer. A crypto casino integrates a cryptocurrency payment processor or wallet system (such as CoinsPaid, BitPay, or a custom wallet solution) rather than traditional payment gateways. Some crypto casinos also feature "provably fair" games — casino games whose randomness can be independently verified by players using blockchain cryptography — which has become a marketing differentiator in the crypto gambling space.

From a regulatory standpoint, a crypto casino requires the same gambling licence as any other online casino. The gambling licence covers game integrity, player protection, responsible gambling, and AML compliance. The fact that payments are made in cryptocurrency does not change the regulatory framework — it adds crypto-specific AML requirements on top of the standard gambling AML obligations.

The online casino market accessible via crypto is vast. Many players in emerging markets with limited banking access use crypto as their primary way to deposit at online casinos. This creates both an opportunity (global player access without banking friction) and a compliance challenge (difficulty of traditional KYC for pseudonymous blockchain users).

KYC for Crypto Casinos

KYC Requirements for Crypto Casino Operators

KYC (Know Your Customer) is mandatory for all licensed crypto casinos. The pseudonymous nature of cryptocurrency does not exempt operators from verifying player identities — in fact, most gambling regulators require stricter AML controls for crypto-accepting casinos than for fiat-only platforms due to the heightened money laundering risk.

Standard KYC triggers include: first withdrawal, deposits accumulating above a threshold (typically €1,000–€2,000), suspicious activity indicators, or whenever player activity is inconsistent with their stated profile. For large deposits or withdrawals, source of funds documentation (bank statements, payslips, tax returns) is required.

Blockchain analytics tools are the crypto-specific layer of KYC. When a player deposits cryptocurrency, the casino's AML system must screen the sending wallet address against risk databases. Deposits from high-risk wallets (those associated with darknet markets, hacks, scams, or sanctions) must be flagged and investigated — or rejected. Major blockchain analytics providers include Chainalysis, Elliptic, and TRM Labs.

FATF Travel Rule: The FATF Travel Rule applies to transfers between VASPs. When a regulated crypto exchange (VASP) sends cryptocurrency to a crypto casino (which may also be a VASP or a regulated gambling operator), Travel Rule information may need to be transmitted. Licensed casinos must understand their VASP status and Travel Rule obligations in their licensing jurisdiction.

Licensing costs in Switzerland typically range from CHF 50,000 to CHF 150,000 for the initial application and approval process, depending on the canton and complexity of your operation. Additional costs include annual compliance audits (CHF 10,000-30,000), banking fees (CHF 5,000-15,000 annually), and technical infrastructure maintenance. You should budget for professional legal and consulting fees of CHF 20,000-50,000 to navigate the application process with cantonal authorities.
Most traditional Swiss banks remain reluctant to service crypto-related businesses due to regulatory uncertainty, requiring you to work with specialized banking partners or crypto-friendly institutions. You will need to provide comprehensive AML/CFT documentation, detailed business plans, and proof of regulatory compliance before any account approval. Some operators use fintech banking solutions or multi-currency accounts to mitigate banking access issues, though these often come with higher fees and stricter transaction limits.
Required documents typically include: detailed business plan, beneficial ownership declaration, financial projections (3-5 years), AML/CFT compliance procedures, responsible gambling policies, technical security specifications, and proof of banking relationships. You will also need to submit evidence of adequate capitalization (minimum CHF 250,000-500,000 depending on the canton), director background checks, and certified copies of your company articles of association. Each canton may have additional specific requirements, so consulting with a local regulatory advisor is essential.
Crypto casino operators in Switzerland are subject to corporate income tax on gaming revenue at rates varying between 11.5%-21.6% depending on the canton and municipality where you are licensed. Winnings paid out to players are not subject to withholding tax, but you must maintain detailed records of all transactions for tax authority review. Some cantons like Zug offer preferential tax rates for licensed gaming operators, potentially reducing your effective tax burden to around 12-15%.
Most crypto casino licenses in Switzerland require annual renewal or compliance certification, typically due within 30-60 days before expiration. The renewal process involves submitting updated financial statements, compliance reports, and confirmation of ongoing AML/CFT procedures to your licensing canton authority. Failure to renew on time can result in license suspension or revocation, so you should implement internal tracking systems and begin the renewal process at least 90 days before expiration.
The main regulatory risks include changing cryptocurrency classification (currently not legal tender in Switzerland), potential future restrictions on crypto gaming from FINMA, and stricter international sanctions compliance requirements. You also face operational risks such as technical security breaches, player disputes, and unexpected banking relationship terminations, which could impact your license status. Insurance coverage for cyber liability and gaming operations is strongly recommended and typically costs CHF 15,000-40,000 annually to mitigate these risks.
Switzerland offers stricter regulatory oversight but higher credibility and lower fraud risk, making it ideal for premium operators, while Malta and Gibraltar provide faster approval timelines (3-6 months) and lower costs (EUR 20,000-50,000). Swiss licenses are more restrictive regarding marketing and payment methods but provide better long-term stability and access to European banking relationships. Choose Switzerland if you prioritize reputation and compliance over speed; choose Malta or Gibraltar if you need faster market entry and lower operational costs.
