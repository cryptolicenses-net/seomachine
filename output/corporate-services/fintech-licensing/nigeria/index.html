Regulatory Overview

Nigeria's Fintech Regulatory Landscape

Nigeria has a dual-regulator fintech framework. The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) regulates payment services, banking, e-money, and mobile money. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC Nigeria) regulates securities, investment products, and — since 2022 — virtual asset service providers (VASPs) and digital asset exchanges.

Nigeria's fintech regulatory history has been turbulent, particularly in the crypto space. The CBN's 2021 banking ban on crypto services was reversed in December 2023 following industry pressure and the recognition that crypto was being driven underground. The current framework allows banks to service SEC-registered VASPs under CBN AML guidelines — a significant improvement that enables formal banking for crypto exchanges.

The FCCPC (Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Commission) has also issued guidelines for digital lending apps (2022), addressing the predatory lending practices that had made Nigeria's digital lending sector internationally notorious. Any fintech offering consumer lending via mobile app must comply with FCCPC digital lending guidelines.

Crypto / VASP

SEC Nigeria VASP Framework

SEC Nigeria's Virtual Assets Service Providers (VASP) framework (2022, updated 2024) establishes registration requirements for digital asset exchanges, custodians, and VASPs serving Nigerian clients. SEC classifies most crypto tokens as securities and requires VASPs to register with SEC as a condition for operating legally in Nigeria.

Key VASP registration requirements include: Nigerian incorporated company, minimum paid-up capital of NGN 500 million (approximately USD 350,000 at current exchange rates, subject to FX movement), comprehensive AML/KYC programme, technology infrastructure audit, board and management background checks, and SEC approval of the business model. Foreign companies must establish a Nigerian subsidiary.

Following the CBN's December 2023 reversal of the banking ban, SEC-registered VASPs can now open corporate bank accounts with Nigerian commercial banks. The CBN's guidelines require banks to conduct enhanced due diligence on VASP customers, verify SEC registration status, and implement ongoing transaction monitoring.

Binance Nigeria Case (2024): In 2024, Nigerian authorities arrested Binance executives and demanded billions in back taxes and fines. Binance was required to delist NGN trading pairs. This case highlighted the risks of operating in Nigeria without proper engagement with CBN and FCCPC on FX controls. SEC-registered VASPs are better protected but must proactively engage regulators.

Key challenges: FX volatility — the naira has depreciated 60%+ since 2023, complicating capital requirements and revenue repatriation; CBN unpredictability — policy has changed multiple times on crypto; mandatory local establishment with Nigerian directors; difficulty obtaining banking relationships (banks are cautious); and infrastructure reliability outside Lagos and Abuja. Due diligence engagement with regulators before market entry is strongly recommended.
Nigeria is Africa's largest fintech market: 220M+ population, USD 20B+ annual diaspora remittances, Africa's highest crypto adoption rate (by volume), 60% of population under 25, Africa's leading tech talent pool, and a vibrant startup ecosystem in Lagos (Africa's leading tech hub). Despite regulatory complexity, market size and growth trajectory attract significant international fintech investment — Nigeria received 52% of all African fintech investment in 2022.
License application fees vary by category, ranging from NGN 500,000 to NGN 5 million depending on whether you're applying for a Payment Service Provider, Money Services Business, or Remittance Service Provider license through the CBN. Additional costs include legal consultation (typically NGN 2-5 million), compliance infrastructure setup, and annual regulatory fees which average 0.5% to 2% of transaction volume. Foreign applicants should budget for incorporation, capital requirements (minimum NGN 25 million for most categories), and ongoing compliance audits.
The CBN typically processes Payment Service Provider applications within 90-180 days from submission of a complete application package, though this can extend to 6-9 months if additional documentation or clarifications are required. The SEC VASP registration process is generally faster at 60-90 days. Processing timelines in 2026 depend heavily on application completeness, background check thoroughness, and current regulatory workload, so submitting through an experienced licensing advisor can significantly reduce delays.
Capital requirements in 2026 range from NGN 25 million to NGN 100 million depending on your service category and whether you're a domestic or foreign entity. Payment Service Providers typically need minimum paid-up capital of NGN 50 million, while Money Services Businesses require NGN 25-50 million. Foreign companies must maintain these capital requirements in a designated Nigerian bank account and may face additional requirements to establish a local subsidiary rather than operating as a branch.
Required documents include: certified incorporation documents, directors' curriculum vitae and international police clearance certificates, detailed business plan with 3-year projections, proof of capital adequacy, IT security audit reports, anti-money laundering and Know Your Customer policies, organizational structure charts, audited financial statements (if existing company), and compliance officer appointment letter. The CBN also requires site inspection reports for physical offices and evidence of banking relationships with Nigerian institutions.
Licensed fintech companies are subject to Corporate Income Tax at 30%, Value Added Tax on applicable services (7.5%), and withholding taxes on certain transactions. The CBN may provide exemptions on certain fintech revenues under the National Technology Innovation Programme depending on your classification. Additionally, you must register with the Federal Inland Revenue Service and state tax authorities, file annual tax returns, and maintain detailed transaction records for audit purposes to demonstrate compliance with economic substance requirements.
Payment Service Providers holding CBN licenses can operate escrow accounts and maintain customer funds, but only through partnerships with licensed deposit-money banks or through specially designated settlement accounts held in trust. Direct banking operations require a full banking license from the CBN, which is a separate and substantially more complex process. As of 2026, fintech companies must maintain segregated customer accounts and undergo quarterly audits to verify fund protection and proper reconciliation.
FinTech licenses must be renewed annually and compliance reports submitted within 90 days of fiscal year-end; failure to renew results in license suspension and operational restrictions. Non-compliance with regulatory requirements can result in penalties ranging from NGN 100,000 to NGN 10 million, mandatory corrective action plans, license downgrade, or revocation in severe cases. The CBN conducts surprise regulatory examinations and requires immediate notification of material changes in operations, ownership structure, or technology infrastructure.
