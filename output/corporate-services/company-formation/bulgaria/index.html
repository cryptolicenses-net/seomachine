Why Bulgaria

Why Bulgaria for Crypto Companies?

Bulgaria stands out as the most cost-efficient EU jurisdiction for crypto company formation in 2025. The combination of 10% flat corporate income tax (the EU's lowest), minimal capital requirements, fast registration, and available VASP registration with the National Revenue Agency creates an unmatched value proposition for early-stage and growing crypto businesses that need EU presence without the cost of operating in Western Europe.

As an EU member state, a Bulgarian company benefits from EU regulatory recognition, freedom of establishment across the single market, and the credibility of an EU-based entity with banking partners, payment processors, and institutional counterparties. While Bulgaria does not have a dedicated crypto regulator with its own licensing framework (like Estonia's FIU or Lithuania's Bank), its VASP registration regime under the Bulgarian AMLA is straightforward and fast.

Bulgaria is also increasingly attractive for businesses that want to later obtain an investment firm licence (FSC) or apply for a MiCA authorisation under the EU crypto regulation. The FSC (Financial Supervision Commission) regulates investment services and its fees and capital requirements are among the most accessible in the EU.

VASP Registration

Bulgarian VASP Registration (NRA)

Under the Bulgarian Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA), all VASPs operating in or from Bulgaria must register with the National Revenue Agency (NRA — Национална агенция за приходите). This is a registration process, not a licensing process — the NRA does not evaluate the commercial viability of your business, only its AML/KYC compliance framework.

The VASP registration is mandatory for: exchange between virtual and fiat currencies, exchange between virtual currencies, transfer of virtual currencies, custody/administration of virtual currencies, and financial services related to token issuance and sale.

The NRA registration application must include: company incorporation documents, description of virtual asset activities, AML policy and internal procedures, KYC procedures, risk assessment, information on AML officer, and UBO declarations for all beneficial owners.

MiCA and Bulgarian VASPs: Under EU MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation), which applies from December 2024, VASPs in EU member states must transition to MiCA-compliant CASP (Crypto-Asset Service Provider) authorisation. Bulgarian VASPs currently registered under the NRA regime will need to apply for MiCA authorisation — either through the Bulgarian FSC or by passporting from another EU member state. Virtual Office & Banking

Registered Address & Banking Solutions

A virtual registered address in Sofia or another Bulgarian city satisfies the commercial register requirement and is accepted by the NRA for VASP registration purposes. Physical office space is not required for NRA VASP registration, though a professional address (not a private residence) is strongly recommended.

Bulgarian banking for crypto companies is challenging but not impossible. Fibank (First Investment Bank), TBI Bank, and some branches of international banks with Bulgarian presence have experience onboarding fintech/crypto clients. The key to successful Bulgarian bank account opening is: a registered NRA VASP, a clean AML programme, detailed source of funds documentation for UBOs, and a realistic business plan.

For companies that cannot access Bulgarian banking initially, Lithuanian EMI providers (Paysera, ConnectPay, Kevin.eu) are the most popular alternative — they provide IBANs, SEPA payments, and basic business account services for EU-registered companies including Bulgarian entities.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Bulgaria offers the EU's lowest flat corporate tax rate at 10%, the lowest minimum share capital for an LLC (BGN 2, approximately €1), fast incorporation (1–2 weeks), and VASP registration available through the National Revenue Agency (NRA). Operating costs including accountancy, registered address, and staff are also among the lowest in the EU.
An EOOD is a single-member limited liability company — equivalent to a sole-owner LLC. An OOD is a multi-member LLC with 2 or more shareholders. Both have identical tax treatment and the same BGN 2 minimum capital requirement. The EOOD is the most common structure for foreign founders setting up a Bulgarian crypto entity.
VASPs in Bulgaria must register with the National Revenue Agency (NRA) under the Bulgarian Anti-Money Laundering Act. The registration requires: company incorporation, AML/KYC programme documentation, internal controls description, UBO declarations, and fit-and-proper information for directors. Registration typically takes 2–6 weeks after company formation.
A Bulgarian EOOD/OOD is an EU company and benefits from EU freedoms. For regulated investment services, an investment firm authorised by the FSC in Bulgaria can passport into other EU member states under MiFID II. For pure VASP activities, MiCA provides a passporting framework from 2024 — Bulgarian VASPs will need MiCA-compliant authorisation to passport.
Formation costs typically range from €800–€1,500 including registration fee (approximately €50), notary fees (€100–€300), and professional services. The timeline is 5–10 business days for standard LLC registration through the Registry Agency, though expedited services can reduce this to 2–3 days for an additional fee of €200–€400. Total setup costs remain among Europe's lowest due to minimal share capital requirements and flat registration fees.
Bulgarian companies must file annual financial statements with the Registry Agency by March 31 following the reporting year, maintain accounting records for 5 years, and file corporate tax returns by March 31 (10% flat rate on profits). VAT returns are typically filed quarterly, and VASPs must file AML/KYC reports to the National Revenue Agency on a transaction-monitoring basis. Micro-enterprises (turnover under €300,000) qualify for simplified accounting under Bulgarian law.
Bulgaria does not restrict crypto companies by activity type at formation, but VASP classification determines regulatory oversight. Exchanges, custodians, and payment service providers must register as VASPs with the National Revenue Agency; traders, miners, and blockchain developers face lighter requirements. However, operating an unregistered exchange or custody service is illegal and carries penalties up to BGN 50,000 (approximately €25,000) plus potential criminal liability.
An EOOD (single-member LLC) has minimal governance requirements—no board meetings, shareholder approvals, or internal procedures mandated by law. An OOD (multi-member LLC) requires a general assembly of members for major decisions (profit distribution, amendments, dissolution), documented in meeting minutes, and annual accounting approval. Both must maintain a registered office in Bulgaria and appoint a representative for regulatory correspondence, though management can be conducted remotely.
Bulgaria taxes crypto company profits at a flat 10% corporate rate—lower than most EU peers (Germany 30%, France 25%, Austria 24%). Capital gains from cryptocurrency trading are treated as corporate income and taxed at 10%, while employee salaries face 10% corporate tax plus 12% personal income tax. Bulgaria has no specific wealth tax on crypto holdings, and cryptocurrency-to-cryptocurrency trades are currently not considered taxable events at the transaction level, though this may change as EU regulations evolve.
Bulgarian banks require a complete AML/KYC program, detailed business plan, proof of regulatory registration (VASP certificate from the NRA), beneficial ownership documentation, and transaction volume projections. Most banks request quarterly compliance reports and transaction monitoring summaries; some impose transaction limits (€100,000–€500,000 monthly) or require cryptocurrency exchange partnerships to be regulated. Building relationships with blockchain-friendly banks (such as fintech-focused private banks) typically succeeds within 4–8 weeks.
Key risks include Bulgaria's low international AML reputation (it ranks lower on FATF mutual evaluation), making correspondent banking relationships difficult and triggering enhanced due diligence from EU partners. Political instability and occasional government transitions can lead to regulatory shifts; the 2023–2024 banking crisis also created temporary liquidity constraints. Additionally, Bulgaria's judicial system is perceived as slow for disputes, and the country faces ongoing EU scrutiny on corruption—factors that may affect investment confidence and partner relations despite the jurisdiction's favorable tax and cost structure.
Bulgarian banks are cautious about crypto companies but not uniformly refusing. The key is to present a clean corporate structure, a detailed AML programme, and a registered VASP status with the NRA. For companies that cannot obtain a Bulgarian bank account initially, Lithuanian EMI solutions (Paysera, ConnectPay) are a common alternative.
