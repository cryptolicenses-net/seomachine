Frequently Asked Questions — Crypto Licensing

Yes. In virtually every major jurisdiction — EU (MiCA CASP), UK (FCA), USA (MSB + state MTL), Singapore (MAS MPI), UAE (VARA) — operating a crypto exchange without the appropriate licence or registration is illegal and carries significant penalties. The specific licence type depends on your business model, target markets, and the services you offer.
The fastest and most accessible crypto licences in 2026 are typically: Estonia VASP registration (1–3 months, EUR 3,300 state fee), Lithuania VASP registration (2–4 months), Georgia Virtual Zone company (1–2 months), and Seychelles FSA licence (2–3 months). However, "easiest" does not mean most commercially useful — banking access and client acceptance vary significantly by jurisdiction.
A VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider) registration is typically a lighter-touch regulatory requirement focused on AML/CFT compliance — common in Estonia and Lithuania. A full crypto licence (like MiCA CASP, Singapore MAS, or UAE VARA) involves prudential requirements: minimum capital, qualified management, technology audits, custody segregation, and ongoing reporting. Full licences take longer and cost more, but provide greater commercial credibility.
Total first-year costs range from approximately EUR 15,000–30,000 for a simple VASP registration (Estonia, Lithuania) to EUR 200,000–500,000+ for a full MiCA CASP authorisation or Singapore MAS licence including legal fees, compliance infrastructure, minimum capital deposit, and staffing. Our Crypto Licence Cost guide (publishing 29 March 2026) provides a full breakdown by jurisdiction.
Not with a single licence in most cases. Within the EU, a MiCA CASP authorisation passports across all 27 member states. Outside the EU, most jurisdictions require their own registration or licence for businesses actively serving local customers. Many crypto businesses operate with 2–3 licences covering key markets: an EU MiCA licence for Europe, a Singapore or Hong Kong licence for Asia, and a US MSB registration for North America.
You'll typically need articles of association, proof of funding (usually CHF 250,000 minimum), detailed AML/KYC policies, organizational charts, CVs of board members and beneficial owners, compliance officer appointment documentation, and a business plan covering your crypto services. FINMA and cantonal authorities may request additional documentation based on your specific service model, so preparation timelines should allow 4-8 weeks for document compilation.
Standard processing timelines range from 6 to 12 months depending on the licence type and completeness of your application. FINMA handles more complex applications (6-12 months), while cantonal authorities may process simpler registrations in 3-4 months. Incomplete submissions can extend timelines by 3-6 months, making thorough preparation critical.
You must implement continuous AML/KYC monitoring, file suspicious activity reports (SARs) within 5 business days, maintain capital adequacy ratios set by your regulator, conduct annual internal audits, and report significant operational changes to authorities within 30 days. Additionally, you must maintain detailed transaction records for 5-10 years and undergo regular compliance audits by FINMA or your cantonal supervisor.
Crypto licence holders in Zug and other cantons benefit from competitive corporate tax rates (typically 11-14% combined federal and cantonal rates), though these vary by location. However, you must report all crypto-related income and establish proper transfer pricing for related-party transactions; SECO guidelines require documentation of your crypto valuation methodology. Licence costs themselves are generally tax-deductible as business expenses.
Yes, holding a valid crypto licence significantly improves banking relationships; major Swiss banks like SIX, UBS, and Postfinance increasingly work with licensed crypto firms. However, banks will conduct enhanced due diligence on your business model, customer profiles, and compliance infrastructure—expect 2-4 months and provide detailed documentation of your AML procedures. Some banks may still decline depending on your specific services or customer demographics.
Licence suspension typically allows 30-90 days to remedy compliance violations before potential revocation. If revoked, you must cease all regulated activities within 14 days, transfer customer assets to licensed entities, and maintain records for regulatory review—violations carry fines up to CHF 5 million and potential criminal liability. You cannot reapply for 12-24 months after revocation in most cases.
Licence renewals in Switzerland occur annually or biennially depending on your cantonal regulator, with renewal applications typically submitted 90 days before expiration. Annual maintenance costs range from CHF 10,000 to CHF 50,000 depending on licence type, including regulatory fees, compliance officer salaries, and audit costs; you must also demonstrate continued capital adequacy and updated compliance documentation at each renewal cycle.
