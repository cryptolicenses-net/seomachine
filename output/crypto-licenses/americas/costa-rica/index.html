Overview

Costa Rica: Latin America's Crypto-Friendly Hub

Costa Rica operates a territorial tax system, meaning income earned outside Costa Rica is not subject to Costa Rican corporate tax. For crypto businesses primarily serving international clients, this creates an extremely favorable tax environment. The country is politically stable, has a strong rule of law tradition, and possesses a well-educated tech workforce.

The SUGEF registration requirement stems from Costa Rica's obligations under FATF Recommendations. VASPs performing exchange, transfer, custody, or related services must register with SUGEF as an "Actividad No Financiera Designada" (ANFD) and implement a full AML/CFT compliance program.

Costa Rica's banking sector has historically been cautious with crypto businesses, making banking access a key operational challenge. However, several local banks have developed crypto-friendly policies for properly registered VASPs, and international accounts can supplement domestic banking needs.

Note: Costa Rica is drafting dedicated virtual asset legislation as of 2025. New laws may introduce formal licensing requirements. Businesses registering now under the AML framework will have a compliance head start for any new regime.

Develop a SUGEF-compliant AML/CFT program including: customer identification procedures, transaction monitoring, risk assessment, suspicious transaction reporting procedures, staff training records, and internal audit mechanisms. Appoint a designated AML compliance officer.

File the VASP registration with SUGEF as a Designated Non-Financial Business or Profession (DNFBP/ANFD). Submit the corporate documents, AML program documentation, officer information, and business description. SUGEF reviews for completeness and compliance.

Launch & Ongoing Compliance
Commence operations with SUGEF registration in place. File annual AML reports, submit STRs as required, maintain 5-year record keeping, and update SUGEF of material changes to ownership or business model. Prepare for periodic SUGEF inspections.

FAQ

Costa Rica Crypto License FAQ

Costa Rica does not have a bespoke crypto licensing law, but VASPs must register with SUGEF for AML/CFT compliance under Law 7786. Operating without SUGEF registration exposes businesses to sanctions and banking access issues.
SUGEF is the Costa Rican financial supervisor. It oversees banks, financial entities, and — under AML Law 7786 — VASPs that must register for AML/CFT compliance. SUGEF conducts inspections and can impose sanctions for non-compliance.
There is no specific minimum capital requirement for VASP registration. Companies must demonstrate financial soundness and have adequate operational resources. Practical working capital of $25,000–$50,000 is typically sufficient for initial operations.
Costa Rica applies a territorial tax system — income from foreign-source activities is not taxed. The standard corporate tax rate is 30% on Costa Rican-source income. Crypto businesses primarily serving foreign customers can structure to minimize Costa Rican tax exposure significantly.

Panama has a similar territorial tax system and the Crypto Law 2023 providing a formal licensing path. Both offer relatively low-cost operations and Latin American market access.
The licensing process through SUGEF typically takes 4-6 months from initial application submission to final approval, assuming all documentation is complete and no additional information requests are issued. Complex applications involving multiple service lines may extend to 8-9 months. Processing times can vary based on SUGEF's current workload and the completeness of your submission.
Initial licensing costs typically range from USD 15,000-35,000, including application fees, legal documentation, and compliance setup. Annual maintenance fees and regulatory reporting costs generally fall between USD 5,000-12,000 per year. Additional expenses may include ongoing compliance training, audit fees, and technology infrastructure to meet AML/KYC requirements.
SUGEF requires articles of incorporation, shareholder documentation, detailed business plans, organizational charts with CVs of key personnel, AML/KYC policies, IT security assessments, financial projections, and proof of minimum capital. You must also provide a comprehensive list of crypto services offered and technical documentation describing your platform's security architecture and compliance mechanisms.
Opening a bank account as a licensed crypto business in Costa Rica has become increasingly challenging due to banking sector risk aversion and de-risking trends. However, SUGEF-regulated entities have better prospects than unlicensed operators. We recommend engaging specialized banking relationships or considering regional banks with crypto-friendly policies, though this may require multiple banking partners for full operational coverage.
Key compliance risks include strict AML/KYC enforcement from SUGEF, potential changes to tax treatment of cryptocurrency holdings, and evolving international standards like FATF recommendations. Costa Rica has strengthened enforcement against non-compliant operators, and penalties include license revocation, substantial fines, and criminal liability for senior management in cases of willful non-compliance.
Costa Rica crypto licenses require annual renewal with SUGEF, typically due within 30 days of your license anniversary date. Renewal requires updated compliance certifications, financial statements, and confirmation that your business operations remain unchanged or documented changes are disclosed. Failure to renew by the deadline can result in operational suspension and regulatory penalties.
Costa Rica offers faster licensing timelines and lower initial costs than Malta, but Malta provides stronger regulatory clarity and EU passporting benefits. Liechtenstein's framework is more stringent with higher capital requirements but offers superior banking relationships and crypto asset custody legitimacy. Costa Rica is best suited for regional Latin American operations, while Malta and Liechtenstein serve global markets more effectively.
