OTC Crypto License: Broker & OTC Desk Licensing 2026

Over-the-counter (OTC) crypto trading desks and brokers occupy a distinct regulatory space. Unlike spot exchanges, OTC desks negotiate prices directly with counterparties for large block trades. Depending on jurisdiction and how the OTC desk handles client funds, licensing may range from a basic VASP registration to a full broker-dealer authorization.

Overview

OTC vs Exchange: Regulatory Differences That Matter

The regulatory treatment of OTC crypto desks differs from spot exchanges in several important ways, even though both activities typically fall under the same VASP/CASP framework. Understanding these differences is essential for selecting the right licensing structure.

Order book vs. bilateral negotiation. Spot exchanges operate centralized or decentralized order books open to all users. OTC desks negotiate prices bilaterally with individual counterparties — typically for block trades above $50,000–$1,000,000. This bilateral nature means OTC desks serve a narrower client base (institutional, high-net-worth) but with higher transaction values and different risk profiles.

Client fund handling. Some OTC desks operate on a delivery-versus-payment (DvP) basis with no client funds held in custody — the desk simply connects buyers and sellers with simultaneous exchange of assets. Other OTC desks hold client funds temporarily during settlement, creating custody obligations. The former model (pure agency) carries lighter capital requirements in most jurisdictions; the latter (principal or custodial agency) triggers the same requirements as an exchange or custodian.

Counterparty due diligence. OTC desks typically serve institutional clients and high-net-worth individuals. KYB (Know Your Business) procedures are as important as KYC, and enhanced due diligence is expected for all counterparties regardless of transaction size. Source of funds verification for large block trades is a standard regulatory expectation in all jurisdictions where OTC licensing exists.

Travel Rule thresholds. OTC trades by their nature tend to exceed Travel Rule thresholds. OTC desks must implement Travel Rule compliance solutions for transfers above $1,000/$1,000 equivalent — in practice, this means Travel Rule applies to virtually every OTC transaction. Pre-trade counterparty verification (name, wallet address, and beneficial ownership) is standard practice for compliant OTC desks.

AML & Compliance

AML and Travel Rule for OTC Desks

AML/CFT compliance for OTC desks has several features that distinguish it from retail exchange compliance. The higher transaction values and institutional counterparties create both greater risk and greater scrutiny from regulators.

Enhanced due diligence (EDD) thresholds. Most regulators require EDD for all transactions above a defined threshold — typically $15,000–$50,000 for OTC trades. EDD involves deeper investigation into the source of funds and wealth, beneficial ownership verification beyond standard KYB, and enhanced ongoing monitoring of the counterparty relationship. Given that most OTC trades exceed these thresholds, EDD is effectively the standard procedure for OTC operations rather than an exception.

Travel Rule implementation. FATF Recommendation 16 requires that originating VASPs transmit originator and beneficiary information to receiving VASPs for transfers above threshold. For OTC desks, this means verifying counterparty VASP identity (using tools like OpenVASP, Notabene, or TRM Labs), confirming wallet ownership, and maintaining records of all Travel Rule data exchanged. Many jurisdictions now require pre-trade Travel Rule checks before executing OTC transactions.

Institutional KYB. OTC clients are typically legal entities — funds, family offices, crypto companies, high-net-worth individuals via corporate structures. KYB procedures must verify the legal entity, its ultimate beneficial owners (UBOs, typically anyone with ≥25% ownership or control), source of business funds, and the commercial rationale for using OTC services. PEP and sanctions screening of all UBOs is mandatory.

  • Full KYB/KYC on all OTC counterparties before first trade
  • UBO verification with ≥25% ownership threshold
  • Source of funds and wealth verification for trades above EDD threshold
  • Travel Rule compliance for all transfers above $1,000 threshold
  • PEP and sanctions screening (OFAC, EU, UN) on all parties
  • Ongoing transaction monitoring with risk-based thresholds
  • Suspicious activity reporting to national FIU as required

Application Process

How to Apply for an OTC Crypto License

The OTC crypto licensing process follows the same general structure as a VASP license application, but with additional emphasis on institutional client documentation, Travel Rule compliance infrastructure, and counterparty risk management. Allow 6–10 weeks for preparation.

1
Define Business Model & Choose Jurisdiction
Determine whether you are operating as a principal or agency OTC desk. Identify your primary client base and target markets. Select jurisdiction based on regulatory credibility required by your institutional clients, capital availability, and tax efficiency. For EU institutional clients, Lithuania or Switzerland are preferred. For MENA clients, UAE VARA offers the best fit.
Week 1–2
2
Incorporate & Capitalize
Register the OTC entity in the chosen jurisdiction with the appropriate legal structure. Open a corporate bank account — this can be challenging for OTC desks and may require specialist banking relationships. Ensure minimum capital is fully paid up. Appoint a qualified AML compliance officer who will be subject to regulatory fit-and-proper assessment.
Weeks 2–6
3
Build AML Framework & Travel Rule Solution
Draft the full AML/CFT policy covering OTC-specific procedures: institutional KYB, EDD thresholds, Travel Rule data collection and transmission, large transaction monitoring, and SARs filing procedures. Select and implement a Travel Rule compliance solution (e.g., Notabene, TRM Labs, or equivalent). Draft Terms and Conditions and risk disclosure documents for institutional clients.
Weeks 4–10
4
Submit Application
File the complete VASP or broker license application with all supporting documentation. For Lithuania FCIS and Estonia FIU, expect a 2–4 month processing time from a clean submission. For UAE VARA or Switzerland FINMA, allow 6–12 months. Respond promptly and completely to all regulatory RFIs (requests for information) to avoid delays.
Month 2 onward
5
Obtain License & Launch OTC Operations
Upon authorization, implement ongoing compliance obligations: transaction monitoring, periodic AML risk assessments, Travel Rule records retention (typically 5 years), annual compliance reports, and regulatory notifications for material changes to business. Onboard institutional clients through your KYB process before executing any trades. Maintain bank and correspondent relationships for fiat settlement.

OTC Crypto License Frequently Asked Questions

Yes, in virtually all regulated jurisdictions. An OTC crypto desk that exchanges crypto for fiat or other digital assets on behalf of clients constitutes a virtual asset service provider (VASP) activity under FATF Recommendation 15. Whether operating as a principal (taking positions) or agent (matching buyers and sellers), the desk is required to register or obtain a VASP, CASP, or equivalent license. The only exception is peer-to-peer trading where neither party acts professionally or on behalf of others — a status that very few commercial OTC desks can credibly claim.
A principal OTC desk buys and sells crypto using its own funds and takes counterparty risk. The desk makes money on the bid-ask spread and may hold inventory positions. This model requires higher capital (to fund inventory) and typically triggers more stringent licensing requirements. An agency OTC desk matches buyers and sellers without taking positions, earning a commission on matched volume. Agency desks do not hold client funds or crypto — they simply connect counterparties. Agency models generally face lighter capital requirements, though AML/KYC obligations are identical to principal desks in all regulated jurisdictions.

The threshold is typically $1,000 or €1,000 in most jurisdictions. For OTC transactions above this threshold, the originating VASP must collect and transmit originator and beneficiary information to the receiving VASP. For large OTC block trades — typically $100,000+ — enhanced due diligence on the counterparty (KYB/KYC of the institution or high-net-worth individual) is expected by regulators.
For OTC desks targeting institutional clients in the Middle East or Asia, UAE VARA or UAE ADGM offers the best combination of regulatory credibility and 0% corporate tax. For European institutional clients, Switzerland FINMA provides the strongest reputational standing. For cost-effective licensing with global client reach, Lithuania (FCIS) or Seychelles (FSA) offer the fastest and cheapest paths to a legitimate VASP license. For desks targeting high-volume institutional flow, Gibraltar's DLT Provider regime offers a well-regarded framework with a 10% corporate tax rate.

The process includes initial document submission, regulatory review, background checks on beneficial owners and senior management, and potential requests for additional information that can extend timelines. Expedited reviews are sometimes possible if all documentation is submitted flawlessly, potentially reducing the timeline to 6-8 weeks.
Swiss OTC operators typically need at least one bank account for client funds (segregated) and a separate operational account, with many firms maintaining relationships with 2-3 banks for redundancy. As of 2026, crypto banking relationships remain challenging due to ongoing de-risking by traditional banks; most OTC desks partner with specialist banks like Sygnum, Crypto Finance, or foreign banks with crypto exposure such as SIX-regulated institutions. Expect 2-4 months to establish banking relationships and potential requirements for proof of AML/KYC infrastructure before account approval.
Essential documents include detailed Know Your Customer (KYC) files for all clients with verification of identity and source of funds, transaction records showing counterparty details and settlement information, and internal policies covering AML/CFT procedures, client onboarding, and risk management. You must also maintain records of your compliance officer's qualifications, regular audit reports, beneficial ownership documentation for your company, and evidence of staff training on Swiss anti-money laundering regulations. All documents must be retained for a minimum of 5 years and made available to FINMA or cantonal regulators upon request.
As an OTC desk operator, your business profits from crypto trading are subject to standard corporate income tax (ranging from 12-22% at cantonal level in Zug), with no special crypto exemptions as of 2026. Your clients face capital gains tax on profits, which is generally not a direct tax at federal level but may be taxed at cantonal levels in some cases; this varies significantly by canton and residency status. You should maintain detailed records of all client transactions for tax reporting purposes and recommend clients consult with tax advisors, as OTC transactions may have different treatment than exchange-based trading under certain tax frameworks.
Critical risks to document include market manipulation and insider trading risks (especially relevant given OTC's less transparent nature), operational risk from system failures or human error in trade settlement, and counterparty credit risk if you extend margin or hold client assets. Regulatory and compliance risks must address the evolving nature of crypto regulations and FINMA's 2026 expectations around stablecoin handling and DeFi interactions. Your risk framework should also cover cybersecurity threats, liquidity risk management, and record-keeping failures, with mitigation strategies for each.
Swiss OTC licenses typically require annual renewal with submission of updated beneficial ownership information, compliance officer certification, and financial statements demonstrating operational solvency and adequate capital reserves. FINMA and cantonal regulators conduct ongoing supervision with periodic audits; renewal applications must include evidence of continuing AML/KYC compliance, staff training documentation, and any material changes to operations. Renewal fees are substantially lower than initial licensing (typically CHF 2,000-5,000) but may increase if regulatory violations are identified during the supervision period.
