Overview

What Is a White Label Crypto Exchange?

A white label crypto exchange is a pre-built trading platform developed by a third-party technology provider and licensed to operators who deploy it under their own brand. Rather than spending 12–18 months and $500K–$2M building exchange infrastructure from scratch, operators pay a setup fee plus monthly licensing to access a ready-made system covering order book matching, crypto/fiat payment rails, KYC flows, admin dashboards, and mobile apps.

The white label model separates technology from regulatory status. Your WL technology provider — B2Broker, AlphaPoint, Openware, Modulus, or similar — has built and maintains the software. But when you deploy it under your brand and accept user funds, you become the regulated entity. Your users have a relationship with your brand, not with the technology vendor.

This distinction is legally critical. No reputable WL technology provider's license covers your exchange operations. Their licenses — where they hold them — apply only to services they themselves provide as a named entity. The moment you operate a branded platform that accepts user deposits, executes trades, and holds or transmits funds, you are a VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider) under the laws of every FATF-member country where your users reside.

Obtaining your own exchange license is therefore not optional — it is a prerequisite for lawful operation. This guide covers everything you need to know: which jurisdictions to consider, what the licensing process involves, how costs stack up, and how to structure your WL launch for compliance from day one.

IMPORTANT — A white label technology license does NOT cover your exchange operations. You must obtain your own VASP/exchange license in your target jurisdiction. Operating without a license on a white label platform carries identical regulatory risk to any unlicensed exchange.

Regulators worldwide have made it explicit: the use of white label technology does not create a regulatory exemption. The UK FCA, EU national competent authorities, and the US FinCEN have all taken enforcement action against WL exchange operators who assumed their technology vendor's compliance status transferred to their operation. Fines range from hundreds of thousands to multi-million dollar penalties, with criminal liability for principals in the most egregious cases.

Launch Process

How to Launch a Licensed White Label Exchange

Launching a compliant white label exchange involves five sequential workstreams. Licensing and technology setup can proceed in parallel, but the exchange should not go live until the license is issued and all compliance infrastructure is in place.

1
Choose Jurisdiction & Obtain Your License
Select your licensing jurisdiction based on target market, budget, and timeline. Engage a crypto licensing advisor to prepare the corporate structure, AML/KYC policies, business plan, and all required documentation. Submit the application to the relevant regulator and manage the review process. This is the critical path — begin immediately.
Timeline: 2 weeks – 6 months depending on jurisdiction
2
Select & Contract Your White Label Provider
Evaluate WL providers on matching engine performance, liquidity connectivity, KYC/AML module quality, travel rule support, API documentation, SLA terms, and pricing structure. Negotiate the license agreement carefully — pay close attention to IP ownership, data rights, exclusivity clauses, and exit terms. Shortlist: B2Broker, AlphaPoint, Openware, Modulus, ChainUP.
Timeline: 2–6 weeks
3
Customize, Brand & Configure
Apply your brand identity (logo, color scheme, domain), configure trading pairs and fee structures, set up jurisdiction-specific KYC tier requirements, enable or disable features per your regulatory conditions, and complete user acceptance testing. Integrate your compliance officer's transaction monitoring rules and geo-blocking for restricted jurisdictions.
Timeline: 4–8 weeks
4
Integrate Payments & Banking
Set up fiat on/off ramp integrations (SEPA, SWIFT, Faster Payments, or local equivalents), integrate a crypto payment processor, establish corporate banking relationships — ideally with a bank or EMI familiar with crypto businesses. This step is often underestimated: banking for crypto exchanges can take 2–4 months and requires your license to be in place.
Timeline: 6–16 weeks (often the second critical path)
5
Launch & Ongoing Compliance
Go live with your licensed exchange. Implement ongoing obligations: quarterly AML reporting, annual independent AML audit, travel rule compliance for transfers over the threshold, suspicious activity reporting (SAR), ongoing transaction monitoring, and license renewal procedures. Appoint a qualified compliance officer (many jurisdictions require regulatory approval of this role).
Timeline: Ongoing post-launch

White Label Exchange Licensing FAQ

No. A white label technology provider's license covers only that company's own regulated operations — not yours. When you deploy a white label platform under your brand, you become the operator of record and must hold your own independent VASP or exchange license in every jurisdiction where you offer services. Operating under someone else's license is illegal and constitutes unlicensed financial services activity, exposing you to criminal liability in most jurisdictions.
SVG (St. Vincent & the Grenadines) has no mandatory crypto licensing requirement and no state fees, making it the cheapest entry point for offshore white label exchange operators. Seychelles FSA requires only $50,000 minimum capital with 2–4 month processing. For EU-compliant operations, Lithuania FCIS offers the lowest EU licensing cost at approximately €125,000 all-in with 2–4 month timelines. Note that offshore registrations provide no access to EU, US, or major APAC markets.
A white label exchange can go live technically in 4–16 weeks once the platform is configured, branded, and payment integrations are complete. However, total time-to-market including licensing depends on your chosen jurisdiction: SVG or Seychelles can be operational in 6–12 weeks end-to-end; Lithuania or Estonia takes 4–6 months; UAE VARA takes 9–15 months. Licensing is usually the critical path — always start licensing before or alongside technology procurement.
Generally, one license is sufficient if you implement geo-blocking for unlicensed markets and serve users based on their residency rules. An EU MiCA CASP authorization (via Lithuania or Estonia) provides passporting rights across all 27 EU member states with one license. Outside the EU, you typically need separate authorizations for major markets like the UK, UAE, Singapore, Australia, or Canada if you actively market to residents there. Most WL operators start with one anchor jurisdiction and geo-block the rest.
Most leading white label exchange providers — including B2Broker, AlphaPoint, Openware/RubyDEX, and Modulus — are technically compatible with EU VASP licensing requirements. The key requirement is that the WL platform supports full AML/KYC integration, travel rule compliance (e.g., Notabene or Sygna), and transaction monitoring. Your legal and compliance setup sits on top of the technology regardless of which WL provider you choose. Confirm your specific regulator's technology requirements with your licensing advisor.
White label setup costs typically range from USD 50,000 to USD 250,000 depending on jurisdiction and customization level, with Zug and Liechtenstein being mid-range options. Additional ongoing compliance costs run USD 15,000 to USD 40,000 annually for licensing maintenance, KYC/AML systems, and regulatory reporting. Banking integration fees and transaction processing can add USD 5,000 to USD 20,000 monthly depending on trading volume. Total first-year investment should be budgeted between USD 150,000 and USD 400,000 across all categories.
Most white label operators require at least one primary custody bank account and a settlement/fiat on-ramp banking partner, which typically take 6-12 weeks to establish in 2026. Swiss banks like Sygnum, Crypto Finance, and Seba are common choices for crypto-friendly banking in Zug, though they require comprehensive compliance documentation and business plans. You may also need a secondary bank in another jurisdiction for liquidity purposes, particularly if targeting multiple regions. Banking relationships are often the longest bottleneck in the launch timeline and should be initiated before finalizing your license application.
White label providers must comply with each jurisdiction's reporting requirements; for EU customers, this includes MiCA reporting to financial authorities as of 2024, while US customers require FBAR and FATCA reporting. Many white label platforms offer built-in tax reporting tools that generate documents compatible with national tax authorities, but you remain liable for accuracy. Switzerland requires reporting to FINMA for significant transactions, and you should budget USD 10,000-USD 25,000 annually for tax compliance consulting across multiple jurisdictions. It's critical to consult with a tax advisor in each target market before launch to avoid regulatory penalties.
Customer funds should be held in segregated accounts under your legal entity's name, not your white label provider's, which protects customers if the provider exits the market. However, if you fail to maintain proper segregation and the white label provider becomes insolvent, customers may face significant delays in fund recovery. You must verify your provider's insurance coverage and backup liquidity arrangements; reputable providers maintain USD 10 million+ in insurance bonds. Regulatory authorities in Switzerland and the EU now require documented proof of fund segregation and disaster recovery plans as part of license conditions in 2026.
FINMA requires business plans outlining your exchange model, white label provider contracts showing technology and liability arrangements, detailed AML/KYC procedures, organizational charts, and IT security audits certified by independent firms. You must also provide banking confirmation letters, shareholder/beneficial owner documentation, compliance officer CVs with relevant experience, and annual financial projections for three years. For white label specific requests, FINMA typically asks for proof that your provider maintains separate systems and that you retain operational control over compliance decisions. Typical documentation packages total 150-250 pages and take 4-8 weeks to compile professionally.
Swiss licenses issued by FINMA for crypto exchanges require renewal every 5 years, with renewal applications typically submitted 6 months before expiration to allow processing time. Renewal requires updated audited financial statements, proof of ongoing compliance with AML/KYC procedures, documentation of any material changes to your white label provider relationship, and evidence of maintained banking relationships. EU MiCA-regulated exchanges face more frequent reviews with supervisory assessments every 1-2 years depending on your member state. Renewal costs in 2026 typically run USD 20,000-USD 50,000 in legal and compliance fees, plus any licensing fee increases.
White label exchanges depend heavily on your provider's uptime and security; if their platform is hacked or experiences outages, your business suffers directly, though you maintain some liability protection through contractual indemnification clauses. You have limited control over platform updates and feature releases, which can impact competitive positioning as the market evolves in 2026. White label providers typically support 50+ exchanges simultaneously, which increases the risk that FINMA or EU regulators scrutinize the shared platform for compliance gaps affecting all clients. Building proprietary technology costs USD 500,000-USD 2 million upfront but eliminates provider dependency and regulatory concentration risk over a 3-5 year horizon.
