Overview

What Is the Anjouan Offshore License?

Anjouan is one of three major islands in the Union of Comoros, an archipelago nation in the Indian Ocean off the east coast of Africa. As an autonomous island, Anjouan operates its own financial services regulatory framework through the Anjouan Offshore Finance Authority (AOFA), independent of the national regulatory structure that governs domestic businesses on the islands.

The AOFA issues an Offshore Financial Services License that is notable for its breadth: a single license instrument covers virtual asset service providers (VASPs), forex brokers, money services businesses, and even gaming operations, depending on the activities declared at application. This makes it one of the few jurisdictions where an operator can simultaneously hold authority for crypto exchange, payment facilitation, and forex intermediation under one filing.

The license has attracted attention in the crypto industry primarily because of its combination of extremely low cost, fast processing times, and zero tax environment. For startups that need a legal entity and license to operate internationally before they can afford the more rigorous — and expensive — licensing regimes in Malta, Liechtenstein, or the UAE, Anjouan represents an accessible entry point.

It is important to understand the license within its correct context. Anjouan is not a member of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) and the jurisdiction does not feature on major whitelists consulted by institutional counterparties and tier-1 banks. Operators using this license will face friction when attempting to open accounts at large correspondent banks or onboard institutional clients who require FATF-whitelisted jurisdictions in their counterparty due diligence.

Legal Framework

Regulatory Authority & Legal Basis

The Anjouan Offshore Finance Authority (AOFA) was established under Anjouan's autonomy provisions within the Union of Comoros constitutional framework. The AOFA operates as the sole licensing and supervisory authority for offshore financial services entities registered in Anjouan, distinct from the central government of the Union of Comoros which operates through Moroni.

The governing legislation for offshore entities is the Anjouan Offshore Services Act, which provides the statutory basis for registration, licensing, ongoing compliance, and dissolution of offshore financial services companies. The act was designed specifically to create an attractive low-cost regime for international operators, with deliberately light-touch substance requirements and streamlined administrative procedures.

For virtual asset service providers specifically, the AOFA treats crypto businesses under the umbrella "Financial Services License" category rather than issuing a dedicated crypto-specific license. This means applicants do not need to navigate a separate VASP-specific rulebook — activities are declared in the application, and the license reflects those permitted activities. The legal framework does not yet incorporate MiCA-equivalent standards or FATF Recommendation 15 virtual asset provisions in a formal codified sense.

Annual reporting obligations exist but are lighter than equivalent requirements in EU-aligned jurisdictions. Licensees are expected to submit annual accounts and maintain a registered agent in Anjouan, but full substance requirements (local staff, local directors, local office) are not mandated for offshore entities serving non-Comoran customers.

License Structure

Offshore Financial Services License — What It Covers

The Anjouan Offshore Financial Services License is a combined license instrument. Unlike many jurisdictions that issue separate licenses per activity type, AOFA allows a single license to cover multiple financial services categories simultaneously. This is one of its distinguishing commercial advantages.

The all-in cost of obtaining an Anjouan offshore license — including company incorporation, government licensing fees, and registered agent fees for the first year — typically falls in the $5,000–8,000 USD range. This is significantly below comparable licenses in Seychelles (~$15,000–20,000), Mauritius (~$25,000+), or EU jurisdictions ($50,000–150,000+).

Annual renewal costs run $3,000–5,000 and cover the license renewal fee and registered agent retainer. There is no corporate tax, no dividend withholding tax, and no personal income tax for offshore entities with no Comoran-source income. The tax-neutral environment is a significant ongoing cost advantage for profitable operations.

The 4–6 week timeline is genuinely one of the fastest in the world for a formally licensed (as opposed to simply registered) financial services operation. Seychelles FSA licenses typically take 3–4 months; Malta MFSA licenses 12–18 months; UAE VARA licenses 6–12 months. For operators needing a licensed entity quickly, Anjouan is operationally relevant.

Market Context

Who Uses the Anjouan License & Why

The Anjouan license occupies a specific niche in the international crypto licensing landscape. It is most commonly used by three categories of operator: early-stage startups that need a licensed entity while awaiting approval in a more prestigious jurisdiction; offshore P2P and peer-to-peer platform operators serving emerging markets where regulatory recognition requirements are lower; and companies running combined crypto-forex-payment operations who benefit from the single-license coverage.

A common structural approach is to use an Anjouan entity as a holding or operational subsidiary within a multi-jurisdiction structure, paired with a stronger onshore entity (such as an EU-licensed entity or a UAE VARA-licensed entity) that handles institutional relationships and regulated markets. The Anjouan entity handles lower-risk, offshore, or emerging-market flows.

Anjouan competes most directly with Saint Vincent and the Grenadines (SVG) — which does not formally license crypto but has a registration regime — and with the Seychelles FSA. Compared to SVG, Anjouan offers a more formal license instrument. Compared to Seychelles, it is faster and cheaper but carries lower credibility. The choice between these jurisdictions often comes down to specific banking and client requirements.

The jurisdiction has also attracted operators in the sports betting and iGaming sectors, and many Anjouan license holders operate across crypto and gaming, using the combined license for both product lines.

Frequently Asked Questions

It is a legally constituted license instrument, not a mere registration or certificate. However, "legally valid" and "internationally recognized" are different questions. The license is valid under Anjouan and Comoros law, but many jurisdictions, banks, and institutional counterparties do not treat it with the same weight as licenses from FATF-member regulators such as the Seychelles FSA, Malta MFSA, or UAE VARA.
Banking is the most significant operational challenge for Anjouan licensees. Major banks in Europe, the US, and most of Asia will not open accounts for entities licensed in non-FATF jurisdictions operating crypto businesses. Viable options typically include EMI accounts (electronic money institution accounts), smaller fintech banking platforms that are crypto-friendly, and payment service providers that do not require tier-1 banking relationships. Some operators combine the Anjouan entity with a separate entity in a more recognized jurisdiction to handle banking, routing transactions through the better-banked entity.

You are required to maintain a registered agent in Anjouan (an AOFA-approved agent firm), but this is a administrative requirement fulfilled by your service provider, not a substance requirement. This is a deliberate feature of the offshore regime — it is designed for companies that are operated from other locations and merely need an offshore licensed entity for their international operations.
There is no statutory minimum paid-up capital requirement for the Anjouan offshore license. This contrasts sharply with most regulated jurisdictions: Malta requires €730,000 minimum capital for Class 3 VFA licenses; UAE VARA requires AED 200,000 minimum. While AOFA does not mandate a minimum, it is advisable to capitalize the company with sufficient operational capital — typically $50,000 or more — to demonstrate to counterparties and banking partners that the entity has genuine financial substance. An undercapitalized entity raises due diligence concerns regardless of license status.

Serving customers in the EU requires compliance with MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation) effective from 2024–2025, which requires authorization by an EU member state regulator. Serving UK customers requires FCA authorization. An Anjouan offshore license does not satisfy either requirement, and operating EU or UK customer-facing services under an Anjouan license would constitute unlicensed financial services provision under EU and UK law respectively. The Anjouan license is appropriate for offshore operations serving customers in jurisdictions that do not require inbound crypto platforms to hold local licenses, or where the regulatory framework is either absent or does not restrict offshore providers.
