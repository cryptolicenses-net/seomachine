Overview

Spain's Dual-Regulator Crypto Framework — MiCA Transition

Spain implemented VASP registration under Royal Decree-Law 7/2021 (transposing the EU 5AMLD), requiring all crypto service providers to register with the Banco de España for AML/CFT purposes. Under MiCA (fully applicable from December 2024), the CNMV (Comisión Nacional del Mercado de Valores) becomes the primary competent authority for CASP authorizations in Spain.

Spain has one of Europe's largest crypto user bases — approximately 5 million users — and an active regulatory dialogue between industry and the CNMV. The CNMV has been actively engaged with crypto regulation since 2021, issuing guidelines on advertising, investor protection, and classification of crypto assets.

Spanish is the world's second most spoken language and the dominant language across Latin America. A Spanish MiCA CASP license, combined with the cultural and linguistic connection, positions Spanish-licensed entities advantageously when approaching regulatory discussions in LatAm markets — even though the MiCA license itself only covers the EU.

Dual registration note: Under the Spanish framework, CNMV CASP authorization and Banco de España VASP registry registration are separate — all crypto companies operating in Spain need both. The Banco de España registration is primarily for AML/CFT oversight, while the CNMV authorization covers investment and market activities. Both must be maintained in good standing.

Process

How to Get a Spain CASP License — Step by Step

1
Incorporate Spanish Entity or Prepare EU Passport Notification

Incorporate a Spanish Sociedad Limitada (S.L.) — the most common structure for fintech companies — or prepare an EU passport notification if you already hold a MiCA CASP from another EU state. S.L. incorporation in Spain takes 2–4 weeks via notary. For an S.L., a minimum share capital of €3,006 is required (lower than MiCA capital requirements).

2–4 weeks
2
Register with Banco de España VASP Registry

File for registration on the Banco de España VASP registry. This is an AML/CFT registration — it requires evidence of the company's AML/CFT framework, compliance officer details, and description of crypto services. The Banco de España VASP registration can be pursued concurrently with the CNMV CASP application and typically takes 4–8 weeks.

4–8 weeks
3
Prepare CNMV CASP Application Package

Develop the complete CNMV application: business plan with 3-year financials, compliance documentation (AML/CFT policies, IT security, governance), organizational structure with fit & proper declarations for directors and significant shareholders, capital evidence, and description of all planned activities under MiCA Article 60. Documentation should be in Spanish.

6–10 weeks
4
Submit CNMV Application — Review Period

File the CNMV CASP authorization application along with the application fee. The CNMV reviews the complete application, including background checks on key persons and assessment of the business plan and compliance framework. CNMV review of complete applications typically takes 2–4 months. CNMV may request additional information — respond promptly in Spanish.

2–4 months
5
CNMV Authorization Issued — Maintain BoE Registration

The CNMV issues the CASP authorization. Commence operations. Maintain the Banco de España VASP registration (annual update required). Ongoing obligations include annual CNMV reports, audited financial statements, AML/CFT annual review, Travel Rule compliance, and CNMV advertising notifications for mass marketing campaigns. EU passporting to other member states can begin immediately.

Authorization and ongoing

Spain Crypto License — Common Questions

Yes. Any entity offering crypto asset services to Spanish residents must either hold a Spanish CNMV CASP authorization or passport an EU MiCA license from another EU member state. Additionally, all VASPs must register with the Banco de España for AML/CFT purposes. Operating without both authorizations is a regulatory violation subject to significant fines under Spanish law.
The CNMV (Comisión Nacional del Mercado de Valores) is Spain's securities and financial markets regulator — the equivalent of the SEC (US) or FCA (UK) for Spain. Under MiCA, it is the competent authority for authorizing and supervising Crypto Asset Service Providers (CASPs) in Spain. The CNMV has been actively engaged with crypto regulation since 2021 and issues guidance on crypto advertising, investor protection, and asset classification.
A Spanish MiCA CASP license covers EU market access only — it does not automatically authorize operations in Latin American countries, which have separate regulatory frameworks (Brazil's BCB/CVM, Mexico's CNBV, Colombia's SFC, etc.). However, a Spanish entity provides linguistic and cultural alignment, and some LatAm regulators look favorably on EU-regulated firms during their own licensing processes. A Spanish entity is often the best EU home for businesses with LatAm strategic interests.
Cyprus has lower corporate tax (12.5% vs Spain's 25%), and CySEC is often considered more streamlined than CNMV for CASP applications. Both offer comparable EU passporting rights under MiCA. Spain is preferred for companies with genuine business presence in Spain, Spanish-language market focus, or significant operations in the Spanish economy. For pure regulatory efficiency, Cyprus is typically faster and cheaper.
The CNMV issued Circular 1/2022 requiring registration of crypto advertising campaigns targeting more than 100,000 people in Spain. All mass crypto advertising must include mandatory risk warnings in a prescribed format. The CNMV must be notified 10 days before a large-scale campaign. This applies to all media — TV, online, social media, influencer marketing, and email campaigns. Non-compliant advertising can result in significant CNMV fines and reputational damage.
License application fees to the CNMV typically range from EUR 3,000 to EUR 10,000 depending on the service type, with additional costs for legal counsel, compliance infrastructure, and initial capital requirements varying by activity. Capital requirements for crypto exchanges or custodians generally start at EUR 125,000 minimum, while payment service providers may require EUR 50,000. Total first-year costs including compliance setup usually range from EUR 50,000 to EUR 200,000, with ongoing annual compliance costs of EUR 15,000 to EUR 50,000.
The CNMV licensing process generally takes 3 to 6 months from submission of a complete application, though complex cases involving novel business models may extend to 9 months. The timeline begins after you submit all required documentation, and the CNMV conducts regulatory assessment, background checks on beneficial owners, and compliance framework review. Incomplete applications result in requests for additional information, which can add 1 to 3 months to the process.
Spanish CNMV-licensed crypto firms can access banking services from Spanish and EU-regulated banks, though many traditional banks remain cautious about crypto sector relationships. Most licensed operators work with specialized crypto-friendly banks in Spain or establish banking relationships through EU jurisdictions like Malta or Portugal. Bank account opening typically requires proof of CNMV authorization, compliance policies, AML/KYC procedures, and ongoing regulatory reporting, with approval timelines of 4 to 12 weeks depending on the bank's crypto exposure policies.
CNMV penalties for compliance violations range from EUR 10,000 to EUR 3,000,000 depending on severity, with additional personal liability for directors and significant reputational damage. Common violation areas include inadequate AML/KYC procedures, unauthorized marketing, failure to segregate customer assets, and insufficient capital buffers. Serious breaches can result in license suspension or revocation, civil claims from customers, and criminal prosecution for money laundering or fraud.
Spanish crypto businesses are subject to corporate income tax (Impuesto sobre Sociedades) at 25% on profits, plus VAT treatment that became clearer in 2026 with certain exemptions for crypto services under specific conditions. Employees' cryptocurrency compensation may be taxed as income, and businesses must declare all virtual asset holdings and transactions to Spanish tax authorities (Agencia Tributaria). Spain does not currently allow special crypto tax regimes like some jurisdictions, so standard corporate taxation applies alongside detailed regulatory reporting requirements.
The CNMV requires certified copies of identification documents, proof of residence, beneficial ownership declarations for all stakeholders owning more than 10%, and detailed source-of-funds documentation for initial capital. For corporate applicants, you must provide articles of association, shareholder registers, organizational charts, and background checks for all directors and key management personnel. Documentation must demonstrate compliance with Spanish anti-money laundering requirements and include certified translations into Spanish if original documents are in other languages.
Spain's CNMV licensing process is more stringent and time-intensive than Malta's (3-6 months vs. 6-12 weeks), with higher capital requirements but offering stronger institutional credibility in continental Europe. Portugal offers faster timelines and lower costs but less established regulatory precedent for crypto activities compared to Spain's developed framework. Spanish licensing provides broader EU recognition and access to Spanish banking but requires more detailed compliance infrastructure upfront, while Malta and Portugal may appeal to businesses seeking faster market entry with lower initial investment.
