Overview

Armenia as a Regional Crypto Hub

Armenia has undergone a rapid transformation into a regional technology and fintech hub over the past several years. The acceleration began with the 2022 Russian exodus — following the Ukraine invasion and subsequent sanctions, tens of thousands of Russian IT professionals and companies relocated to Yerevan, drawn by visa-free access, USD-denominated banking, low costs, and a welcoming business environment.

The Central Bank of Armenia (CBA) regulates payment system operators and financial service providers including those operating in the crypto space, principally under the Law on Payment Systems and Payment Organizations. While Armenia does not yet have a standalone crypto-specific law on the scale of MiCA or dedicated legislation like Montenegro's Digital Assets Act, the CBA-supervised payment systems framework provides a workable pathway for VASP registration and operation.

A key practical advantage of Armenia is its banking environment. Unlike many post-Soviet jurisdictions, Armenia's banks — including Ameriabank, Ardshinbank, ACBA Bank, and Converse Bank — operate with USD accounts, SWIFT transfers, and relatively open correspondent banking relationships. This makes Armenia significantly more practical for international crypto business than jurisdictions where USD banking is restricted or unavailable.

Tech hub growth: Yerevan's Dalma Garden tech hub and the broader IT sector have experienced exceptional growth since 2022. Armenia's software export revenue grew significantly, and several major crypto exchanges and fintech companies established Armenian entities. The CBA has responded by developing clearer guidance for payment system operators including those handling digital assets.

Process

How to Get an Armenia Crypto License — Step by Step

1
Register Armenian LLC and Inject Capital

Register an Armenian LLC (ՍՊԸ) with the State Register of Legal Entities. The process requires a registered address in Armenia, charter documents, and identification of all shareholders. Inject the required minimum capital as paid-in share capital. Open an Armenian bank account (Ameriabank, Ardshinbank, or Converse Bank recommended).

1–2 weeks
2
Develop Compliance Framework and Appoint MLRO

Prepare a comprehensive AML/CFT program in compliance with Armenia's Law on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorism Financing. Appoint a qualified MLRO and register them with the Financial Monitoring Centre (FMC). Develop KYC/CDD procedures, transaction monitoring policies, risk assessment documentation, and suspicious activity reporting workflows.

2–4 weeks
3
Prepare and Submit CBA Application

Compile the CBA license application package: entity documents, ownership structure and beneficial ownership declarations, management CVs and fitness documentation, business plan with financial projections, AML/CFT framework, IT security policies, and capital evidence. Submit to the CBA Licensing Department with the applicable fee.

2–3 weeks
4
CBA Review and Due Diligence

The CBA conducts a thorough review including background checks on management and shareholders, assessment of the AML framework, and evaluation of the business plan. The CBA may conduct an on-site inspection or request additional documentation. The CBA is generally accessible and responsive to well-structured applications.

6–10 weeks
5
License Issued — Commence Operations

CBA issues the payment system operator / VASP license. Register with the Financial Monitoring Centre. Commence regulated operations. Maintain ongoing CBA and FMC reporting, annual audited financial statements, and compliance with Armenia's AML/CFT obligations.

Armenia Crypto License — Common Questions

Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine and the subsequent sanctions environment, a large number of Russian IT professionals, fintech companies, and crypto businesses relocated to Armenia — attracted by visa-free access for Russian citizens, USD-friendly banking, low taxes, and an educated tech workforce in Yerevan. This influx significantly accelerated Armenia's development as a regional crypto and fintech hub.
The Central Bank of Armenia (CBA) is the primary regulatory authority for crypto-related activities, overseeing them under the broader payment systems and financial services licensing framework. The CBA regulates entities providing payment services, including those involving virtual assets, under the Law on Payment Systems and Payment Organizations.
Armenia levies an 18% corporate income tax rate. While not as low as Montenegro or Bosnia, Armenia's operational costs are significantly lower than most EU jurisdictions, and the overall cost-of-doing-business is very competitive for crypto businesses targeting CIS and regional markets.
Yes. Armenian law permits 100% foreign ownership of Armenian limited liability companies (LLC). There is no requirement for Armenian citizen directors or shareholders, though having at least one Armenian-resident director or compliance officer is practically beneficial for regulatory relationships and day-to-day operations.
Armenia has a robust banking sector with USD-denominated accounts widely available. Key banks include Ameriabank, Ardshinbank, and ACBA Bank. The USD-friendly environment makes Armenia particularly practical for international crypto businesses compared to many post-Soviet jurisdictions with non-convertible local currencies.
The initial application fee is approximately 500,000 AMD (roughly USD 1,300) plus legal and accounting fees ranging from USD 3,000-8,000 depending on your service provider. Annual renewal fees are 300,000 AMD (approximately USD 780), and you should budget for ongoing compliance costs including AML/KYC software subscriptions and audit requirements. Total first-year costs typically range between USD 5,000-12,000.
The licensing process typically takes 8-12 weeks from application submission to approval by the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) and State Revenue Committee (SRC). This timeline assumes complete and accurate documentation; incomplete submissions can extend the process by 4-6 weeks. Some applicants have reported faster processing (6-8 weeks) with expedited document preparation and established banking relationships.
Required documents include articles of incorporation, proof of registered office address in Armenia, detailed business plan, AML/KYC policies, beneficial ownership declarations, passport copies of all directors, proof of capital adequacy (minimum 100,000 AMD or equivalent), and bank statements showing financial stability. You must also provide CVs demonstrating relevant experience in financial services or blockchain for key personnel, and proof of insurance coverage.
License holders must submit quarterly financial reports to the SRC, conduct annual independent audits, maintain transaction records for five years, and file annual AML/KYC compliance reports with the FIU. You are also required to implement real-time transaction monitoring, maintain customer due diligence records, and report any suspicious activities within 48 hours. Failure to meet these obligations can result in fines up to 10 million AMD or license revocation.
Armenia offers significantly lower compliance costs and faster licensing timelines than Switzerland, making it attractive for startups, but Switzerland provides stronger global reputation and access to traditional banking through FINMA regulation. Armenia's regulatory framework is lighter-touch, while Switzerland requires more extensive capital requirements and ongoing FINMA oversight; however, Swiss licenses carry greater international credibility for institutional clients. Armenia is ideal for bootstrap operations, while Switzerland suits businesses needing institutional partnerships.
If renewal is not completed 30 days before expiration, your license enters a grace period of 60 days during which operations must cease and no new transactions can be processed. Continuing operations after the grace period expires results in administrative penalties of 500,000-2,000,000 AMD (USD 1,300-5,200) and potential criminal liability for unlicensed financial activity. Reinstatement after license expiration requires a full reapplication and additional documentation proving remediation of compliance gaps.
The primary risks include potential FATF (Financial Action Task Force) monitoring of Armenian crypto sector, limited international recognition compared to EU/Swiss licenses, and evolving regulatory interpretation by Armenian authorities that may require operational adjustments. Banking relationships can be unstable, as some international banks remain hesitant about Armenian-licensed crypto entities; this is improving in 2026 but remains a concern. Additionally, Armenia's geopolitical situation creates occasional business continuity risks, and reliance on the relatively new regulatory framework means precedent-setting decisions are still being established.
