Overview

Czech Crypto Licensing under AMLFT Act & MiCA

The Czech Republic regulates virtual asset service providers (VASPs) under the Act on Certain Measures Against the Legalisation of Proceeds from Crime and Terrorist Financing (AMLFT Act), which was amended to incorporate the EU's 5th Anti-Money Laundering Directive. The Czech National Bank (Česká národní banka, CNB) serves as the competent authority for both national VASP registration and MiCA CASP authorization.

Two regulatory tracks exist: the legacy Czech national VASP registration (pre-MiCA) and the full MiCA CASP authorization providing EU passporting rights. The legacy track covers basic AML registration for entities offering custody, exchange, and transfer of virtual assets. MiCA CASP authorization — now the primary route for new applicants — requires higher capital, more comprehensive governance documentation, and formal CNB review, but unlocks the ability to passport services into all 27 EU member states.

Prague has emerged as a significant Central and Eastern European fintech hub. The Czech Republic is home to globally recognized crypto companies including SatoshiLabs (the manufacturer of Trezor hardware wallets), Slush Pool (one of the world's oldest Bitcoin mining pools), and Alza, a major e-commerce retailer that accepts crypto payments. This ecosystem provides a strong talent pool and infrastructure for new entrants.

MiCA transition note: As of December 2024, new applications for Czech crypto authorization are processed under MiCA CASP rules with CNB as the competent authority. Existing VASP-registered entities benefit from a transitional grandfathering period. CNB has published guidance on the transition process.

How to Obtain a Czech Crypto License — Step by Step

1
Incorporate a Czech s.r.o. and Establish Substance

Register a Czech společnost s ručením omezeným (s.r.o.) at the Commercial Register (Obchodní rejstřík). Minimum share capital is CZK 1 but capitalize adequately per MiCA requirements (EUR 50,000–150,000). Establish a physical registered office in the Czech Republic and appoint qualified management resident in or regularly present in the country.

2–4 weeks
2
Prepare Application Package in Czech

Develop the complete CNB application dossier in Czech language: corporate governance framework, AML/CFT policies and procedures per the AMLFT Act, KYC/CDD program, Travel Rule compliance plan, IT security policy, business plan with financial projections, and fit & proper documentation for all directors and UBOs exceeding 10% shareholding.

4–8 weeks
3
Submit CNB Application and Capital Proof

File the complete application with the Czech National Bank. The CNB confirms completeness of the submission. Provide bank confirmation of paid-up capital meeting MiCA requirements. Incomplete applications are returned, resetting the timeline, so thorough pre-submission review is essential.

Week 8–10
4
CNB Review, Queries, and Background Checks

CNB reviews the application and conducts background checks on directors and UBOs. CNB may issue formal queries (dotazy) requiring written responses in Czech. Engage a local Czech legal counsel to manage CNB correspondence efficiently. Typical review period for well-prepared applications is 2–4 months.

2–4 months
5
Authorization Issued — Commence Operations and EU Passporting

CNB issues the CASP authorization. For MiCA passporting into other EU member states, file passporting notifications through CNB to ESMA and the host state competent authorities. Establish ongoing reporting and compliance obligations including annual AML reporting, periodic regulatory filings, and transaction monitoring.

Authorization issued

Czech Republic Crypto License — Common Questions

Czech Republic VASP registration is a mandatory authorization issued by CNB (Česká národní banka) under the AMLFT Act for companies providing virtual asset services in the Czech Republic. It covers custody, exchange, and transfer of virtual assets. Under MiCA, this transitions to full CASP authorization with EU passporting rights.
The legacy Czech VASP registration does not provide EU passporting on its own. However, under MiCA, upgrading to a full CASP authorization issued by CNB provides full EU passporting rights to operate across all 27 EU member states without additional national licenses.
CNB VASP registration typically takes 3–6 months. The timeline depends on the completeness of the application, the complexity of the business model, and CNB's current workload. Well-prepared applications with complete AML documentation tend to be processed faster.
Czech Republic offers a balanced combination of EU membership, relatively low fees (CZK 10,000–50,000), and a growing fintech ecosystem in Prague. It's home to major crypto companies including SatoshiLabs (Trezor) and Slush Pool. The 21% corporate tax is moderate by EU standards. For full EU passporting, MiCA CASP authorization is the recommended path.
Under the legacy VASP regime, there is no minimum capital requirement set at the national level beyond general s.r.o. requirements (CZK 1 minimum share capital). Under MiCA CASP authorization, minimum capital requirements range from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on the activities authorized. CNB applies MiCA standards for new CASP applications.
The CNB registration fee is approximately 50,000 CZK (roughly 2,000 EUR), plus legal and compliance setup costs typically ranging from 15,000-40,000 EUR depending on your business model complexity. Annual maintenance fees and ongoing compliance costs should be budgeted at 10,000-25,000 EUR yearly. These figures assume you work with local advisors and do not include banking relationship establishment costs, which vary by institution.
Czech VASP licensees can open business accounts with major Czech banks like Česká spořitelna, Raiffeisenbank, or specialist fintechs, though crypto-friendly banking relationships remain selective. EU banking access is facilitated through Czech banking corridors, but international cryptocurrency banking with USD/EUR liquidity providers often requires relationships outside the Czech Republic. We recommend establishing banking relationships before or concurrent with CNB registration to avoid operational delays.
Czech VASPs must implement AML/CFT procedures compliant with EU 5th and 6th Anti-Money Laundering Directives, maintain detailed transaction records for 5 years, conduct regular risk assessments, and file annual compliance reports with the CNB. You must also appoint a compliance officer, implement customer due diligence protocols, and report suspicious activities to the Financial Analytical Office within specific timeframes. Failure to maintain compliance can result in fines up to 10 million CZK or license revocation.
Czech VASP operators are subject to corporate income tax at 19%, VAT regulations depending on service type, and must report all cryptocurrency transactions for tax purposes. Crypto-to-crypto trades may qualify for VAT exemption under certain conditions, while conversion to fiat typically incurs VAT. We strongly recommend engaging a Czech tax advisor specializing in crypto, as tax treatment of staking, lending, and other services remains clarified through ongoing CNB guidance.
Required documents include articles of association, shareholder identification and beneficial ownership registers, proof of minimum capital (500,000 CZK), detailed business plan, AML/CFT policies, organizational structure with compliance officer designation, and criminal background checks for all senior management. You must also provide evidence of registered office in Czech Republic, insurance coverage, and IT security audit reports. All documents must be in Czech or officially translated, typically adding 2-4 weeks to preparation timelines.
Czech licensing is faster (8-12 weeks vs 16-24 weeks in Malta) and has lower setup costs, but offers less established regulatory clarity and smaller crypto-native business infrastructure. Malta provides broader passporting through its MFSA framework and stronger international crypto reputation, while Lithuania offers fast processing and competitive costs but smaller market access. Czech Republic excels for EU-focused operations requiring strong banking relationships and cost efficiency, but consider Malta or Lithuania if you need faster international expansion or stronger brand recognition in crypto markets.
If the CNB rejects your application, they must provide written justification citing specific regulatory non-compliance or fitness concerns within 30 days of decision. You have the right to submit an amended application addressing deficiencies, typically within 90 days, though CNB can set a different timeline. If you believe the decision is unlawful, you can appeal through Czech administrative courts, a process lasting 3-6 months. Most rejections result from incomplete AML policies or capital insufficiency, which are addressable through resubmission.
