Overview

Why Choose the Bahrain CBB Crypto License?

The Central Bank of Bahrain (CBB) was among the first global regulators to implement dedicated crypto rules in 2019. The CBB Crypto-Asset Module (CA Module) is part of the CBB Rulebook and covers exchange, brokerage, and custody services. Bahrain's approach is notably pragmatic — the CBB engages proactively with applicants and has a reputation for responsive, commercial regulation.

Two main license categories exist: Category 1 (lower-risk activities including advisory and portfolio management) and Category 2 (exchange, custodian — higher capital requirements for firms handling client funds). Bahrain has no capital gains tax, no corporate income tax, and no withholding tax, making it one of the world's most tax-efficient financial services jurisdictions.

English is widely used in business and law across Bahrain. GCC Monetary Council coordination means a Bahrain license can assist expansion across Gulf Cooperation Council markets. Bahrain Financial Harbour provides world-class infrastructure for financial services firms.

GCC market access note: A Bahrain CBB license positions your business within the Gulf's financial regulatory ecosystem. While it does not automatically provide passporting rights to UAE or Saudi Arabia, Bahrain-regulated entities enjoy credibility with GCC institutional clients and banking partners.

Process

How to Get a Bahrain CBB License — Step by Step

1
Pre-Application Meeting with CBB

An optional but strongly recommended pre-application meeting with the CBB allows you to present your business model, receive initial regulatory guidance, and confirm the applicable license category. The CBB is known for engaging positively with prospective applicants at this stage.

2–4 weeks
2
Application Filing

File the complete application with the CBB: regulatory forms, business plan, financial projections, AML/CFT policies, governance structure, fit & proper declarations for all directors and UBOs, source of funds documentation, and technology description. Application fee paid at this stage.

Weeks 4–8
3
CBB Review and Due Diligence

The CBB conducts a thorough review of the application, including background checks on all key persons, assessment of the business plan, and evaluation of the AML/CFT framework. The CBB may request additional information or clarifications during this period.

8–12 weeks
4
In-Principle Approval (IPA) — Incorporate Entity

Upon receiving In-Principle Approval, incorporate the Bahraini entity (W.L.L. or B.S.C.) at the Ministry of Industry and Commerce. Open a corporate bank account with a Bahraini bank and inject the minimum capital. Establish the physical office in Bahrain.

Weeks 12–16
5
Final Approval and License Issuance

Submit evidence of entity incorporation, capital injection, office establishment, and appointment of compliance personnel. The CBB issues the full Crypto-Asset Module license. Ongoing obligations include quarterly CBB returns, annual audits, and immediate notification of material changes.

2–4 weeks

Bahrain CBB Crypto License — Common Questions

The CBB (Central Bank of Bahrain) issues crypto-asset service provider licenses under its Crypto-Asset Module (CA Module) of the CBB Rulebook. It covers crypto exchange, OTC brokerage, custody, and advisory services in Bahrain. The CA Module was first introduced in 2019, making Bahrain one of the earliest jurisdictions globally to create a dedicated crypto regulatory framework.

Note: a VAT of 10% was introduced in 2022 on certain goods and services, though financial services are generally exempt.
Bahrain is simpler and cheaper than UAE's VARA or ADGM routes, with lower capital requirements and a single regulator (CBB). UAE (VARA) offers a larger market and more global recognition, but Bahrain's 0% tax and streamlined process make it attractive for leaner operations and GCC-focused businesses. Bahrain's CBB also has a reputation for more direct engagement with applicants during the licensing process.
Yes. The CBB requires a physical office in Bahrain and evidence of genuine local presence. Virtual offices are not accepted. Office space in Manama is available at competitive rates compared to Dubai. The Bahrain Financial Harbour offers premium office space specifically designed for financial services firms, with direct CBB access.
The applicant must be a Bahraini-incorporated entity. Foreign companies must set up a Bahrain subsidiary (W.L.L. or B.S.C.). 100% foreign ownership is permitted in licensed financial services entities through the Bahrain Financial Harbour and BIBF frameworks. The process of incorporating a Bahraini entity is straightforward and typically takes 2–4 weeks.
The Central Bank of Bahrain charges an initial application fee of approximately 5,000 BHD (around 13,300 USD), with annual licensing fees ranging from 10,000 to 50,000 BHD depending on your business activities and asset size. Additional costs include legal consultation, compliance infrastructure setup, and audit fees, which typically add 20,000-40,000 USD to your first-year expenses. Total first-year costs generally range between 40,000-70,000 USD when including all professional services.
The standard application review period is 6-9 months from submission to final approval, though this can extend to 12 months if the CBB requests additional documentation or clarifications. Initial assessment and completeness checks typically take 4-6 weeks, followed by the substantive review phase. Expedited timelines are rare unless you qualify for specific fintech sandbox programs.
Licensed entities must maintain minimum capital requirements starting at 500,000 BHD, implement comprehensive AML/KYC procedures, submit quarterly financial reports to the CBB, and maintain cybersecurity standards compliant with CBB's digital security framework. You must also conduct annual independent audits, have a compliance officer on staff, and maintain detailed transaction records for minimum 5-year retention periods. Failure to meet these requirements can result in fines up to 500,000 BHD or license revocation.
The CBB requires licensed crypto businesses to segregate customer assets and maintain them in regulated bank accounts, typically with CBB-approved Bahraini banks or internationally recognized institutions. Most operators use a combination of traditional fiat accounts for customer deposits and cold storage solutions for cryptocurrency holdings, both subject to CBB audit and insurance requirements. Direct banking partnerships can take 2-3 months to establish, so this should be factored into your timeline.
The CBB has taken an increasingly stringent stance on AML compliance and has issued multiple warnings against unlicensed operators, creating competitive pressure on licensed entities. Regulatory changes can occur with limited notice, and the CBB has shown willingness to impose restrictions on certain asset classes or trading pairs it deems high-risk. Additionally, Bahrain's position as a smaller jurisdiction means less international recognition compared to major crypto hubs, which may limit institutional partnerships and customer acquisition.
The CBB crypto license is renewed annually, with renewal applications typically due 90 days before your license expiration date. Renewal requires submission of updated financial statements, compliance reports, and a renewal fee of 10,000-50,000 BHD depending on your license category and business volume. The renewal process is generally faster than initial approval, usually completed within 2-3 months if no compliance issues have been raised.
Singapore's MAS license carries significantly higher compliance costs (typically 150,000+ USD annually) and stricter capital requirements but offers greater international credibility and institutional client access. Bahrain's CBB license is more cost-effective and faster to obtain, making it attractive for regional Middle Eastern operations, but provides less global market recognition for B2C expansion. Singapore is generally preferred for global institutional crypto businesses, while Bahrain is stronger for regional Middle Eastern markets and cost-conscious operators.
