Philippines Crypto License: BSP VASP Registration 2026

The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP), the Philippine central bank, regulates Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) through Circular 1108 (2021) and subsequent guidelines. The Philippines has one of Asia's most active crypto adoption rates, with millions of Filipinos using crypto for remittances and payments. BSP VASP registration covers exchanges, custodians, and OTC desks — with a tiered approach based on transaction volumes. The Philippines offers access to 115 million people and a major global remittance corridor.

Philippines Crypto License — Common Questions

The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) requires all Virtual Asset Service Providers operating in the Philippines to register under BSP Circular 1108 (2021). VASPs include crypto exchanges, custodians, and OTC desks. Registration involves BSP review of AML compliance, governance, and technical systems.
Class A VASPs handle lower transaction volumes (under PHP 100 million per day) and have lower capital requirements (PHP 10 million). Class B VASPs handle higher volumes and require PHP 50 million+ capital plus stricter operational requirements. Most new entrants start as Class A.
BSP VASP registration typically takes 1–6 months depending on completeness of application and business model complexity. Well-prepared applications with clear AML programmes are processed faster.
Yes, through establishing a Philippine corporation. Foreign ownership is regulated — some sectors restrict foreign equity to 40%. Financial services including crypto exchanges may have restrictions; legal counsel should confirm equity structure requirements.
Philippines has very high crypto adoption (top globally for crypto usage), a major remittance market where crypto is widely used, 115 million population, and growing fintech infrastructure. BSP is proactive in developing clear crypto regulation.
BSP VASP registration fees vary by class: Class A entities typically require PHP 500,000 to PHP 1,000,000 in initial registration and compliance costs, while Class B entities face lower fees around PHP 250,000 to PHP 500,000. Additional ongoing costs include annual compliance audits (PHP 100,000-300,000), anti-money laundering training (PHP 50,000+), and potential legal/consulting fees. Total first-year costs generally range from PHP 800,000 to PHP 2,000,000 depending on your business structure and existing infrastructure.
The BSP requires certified copies of your articles of incorporation, financial statements for the past 3 years, detailed organizational charts, AML/CFT policies, IT infrastructure specifications, beneficial ownership documentation, and proof of capitalization meeting regulatory minimums. You must also submit board resolutions authorizing VASP activities, compliance officer appointment letters, and detailed business plans outlining transaction volumes, customer types, and geographic markets. All documents must be notarized and submitted in English.
The Philippines BSP VASP license is significantly faster (3-6 months vs 6-12 months in Malta or Singapore) and less expensive (PHP 800,000-2,000,000 vs EUR 100,000-300,000 in Malta). However, Singapore's MAS framework offers stronger global recognition and easier expansion into Asia-Pacific markets, while Malta provides clearer EU nexus. The Philippines is ideal for startups prioritizing speed and cost, while Singapore and Malta suit established firms seeking premium jurisdiction status.
BSP VASP licensees must renew annually by submitting updated financial statements, compliance audit reports, and proof of continuing capitalization requirements by March 31st each year. You are required to conduct quarterly AML/CFT risk assessments, maintain transaction monitoring systems, file suspicious activity reports within 10 business days of detection, and conduct annual staff training on BSP regulations. Non-compliance can result in fines up to PHP 1,000,000 and potential license suspension or revocation.
BSP VASP entities are subject to standard Philippine corporate income tax (30% corporate tax rate in 2026), plus value-added tax (12% VAT) on services rendered, and withholding taxes on remittances. Cryptocurrency transactions themselves are not subject to capital gains tax if treated as business income, but gains from trading activities fall under regular business income taxation. It is advisable to engage a Philippine tax advisor early, as BSP licensing does not provide tax exemptions, and improper structuring can trigger additional assessments from the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR).
Yes, but Philippine banks remain cautious due to global regulatory uncertainty around crypto; many major banks require additional documentation, higher minimum balances (PHP 5,000,000+), and conduct enhanced due diligence. BSP VASP licensees typically have better access than unlicensed entities, but some banks still decline crypto-related accounts due to correspondent banking restrictions and FATF Recommendation pressures. It is strongly recommended to establish banking relationships before formally launching VASP operations, as account freezes or closures can occur mid-operation if banks receive negative compliance signals.
Major violations include failure to implement adequate AML/CFT controls (penalties up to PHP 5,000,000 and potential license revocation), inadequate capital maintenance (PHP 500,000-2,000,000 fines), and late filing of suspicious activity reports (PHP 100,000 per violation). The BSP can impose administrative penalties, freeze assets, revoke licenses, and recommend criminal prosecution for willful violations or involvement in money laundering schemes. In 2026, regulators are intensifying enforcement on stablecoin reserves and custody practices, making operational transparency and documented compliance essential to avoid severe consequences.
