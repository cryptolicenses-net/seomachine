Overview

Japan's CAESP Payment Services Act Framework

Japan became the first major economy to require licensing for cryptocurrency exchanges with the 2017 amendment to the Payment Services Act (PSA). After the 2018 Coincheck hack (USD 530M in NEM stolen), the JFSA significantly tightened requirements through additional guidelines, mandatory cold wallet storage, and enhanced cybersecurity standards.

The JFSA maintains a register of approved Crypto Asset Exchange Service Providers (CAESPs). All exchanges operating in Japan or targeting Japanese users must register. The framework covers crypto-to-fiat exchange, crypto-to-crypto exchange, custody, and transfer services. A whitelist approach means each crypto asset must be individually approved for listing — the JFSA reviews legal status, technical characteristics, and ML/TF risk.

Japan's Self-Regulatory Organization (SRO), the JVCEA (Japan Virtual and Crypto assets Exchange Association), plays an important role: JFSA-registered exchanges must also become JVCEA members and comply with JVCEA self-regulatory rules in addition to JFSA requirements.

High bar, high market: Japan's licensing requirements are among the world's most demanding — but the reward is access to 125 million consumers in one of Asia's wealthiest economies, with strong institutional crypto adoption and a history of being FATF-compliant from the start. Budget 12–18 months and JPY 50–100M+ for the licensing process.

How to Get a Japan CAESP License — Step by Step

1
Incorporate Japanese KK and Inject Capital

Incorporate a Japanese Kabushiki Kaisha (KK) via a notary and the Legal Affairs Bureau. Inject minimum JPY 10 million capital. Appoint a Japanese-resident representative director. Establish a physical office in Japan with genuine management presence.

4–8 weeks
2
Develop Compliance Infrastructure

Build the comprehensive compliance infrastructure required by the JFSA: AML/CTF program, KYC/CDD procedures, transaction monitoring system, Travel Rule implementation, cold wallet storage system, cybersecurity framework with penetration testing, and customer asset segregation controls.

3–6 months
3
Prepare and Submit JFSA Application

Submit the full CAESP registration to the Local Finance Bureau (which reviews on behalf of JFSA). Application includes: business plan, governance docs, AML program, cybersecurity policy, system description, and fit & proper documentation for all executives and significant shareholders. Applications are filed in Japanese.

Month 6–8
4
JFSA/Local Finance Bureau Review

The Local Finance Bureau and JFSA review the application. Expect multiple rounds of detailed questions — often requiring site visits, system demonstrations, and amendments to compliance policies. This is the most time-consuming phase.

6–12 months
5
Registration Granted; JVCEA Membership; Operations

JFSA grants CAESP registration. Immediately join the JVCEA as a registered exchange. Commence operations with ongoing JFSA reporting, annual audits by a Japanese CPA firm, JVCEA self-regulatory compliance, and periodic JFSA examinations.

FAQ

Japan Crypto License — Common Questions

To operate a crypto exchange in Japan, you must register as a Crypto Asset Exchange Service Provider (CAESP) with the Japan Financial Services Agency (JFSA) under the Payment Services Act (PSA). Registration is mandatory; operating without registration is a criminal offence under Japanese law. Japan was one of the first countries globally to require comprehensive crypto exchange licensing.
JFSA crypto exchange registration typically takes 6–12 months, and can exceed 18 months for complex applications or where deficiencies are found. Japan is one of the most demanding crypto licensing jurisdictions globally. Deep understanding of Japanese regulatory requirements and local legal representation are essential.
Japan requires a minimum paid-in capital of JPY 10 million (approximately USD 65,000) for CAESP registration. However, the JFSA may require higher net assets based on the scale of business, user fund safeguarding requirements, and risk profile. Exchanges must segregate customer assets from company assets.

Japan maintains a whitelist approach — crypto assets must be reviewed and approved by the JFSA before listing on a Japanese exchange. This approval process can take several months per asset. Major assets (BTC, ETH, XRP, etc.) are already whitelisted; new tokens require JFSA review including a legal opinion on non-securities status.
Initial application fees to the JFSA typically range from 3-5 million yen, with additional costs for legal consultation (1-3 million yen), compliance infrastructure setup (2-5 million yen), and ongoing operational expenses. Total first-year costs generally fall between 6-13 million yen depending on your business scope and whether you operate as a new entity or acquire an existing licensed operator. Budget an additional 2-4 million yen annually for compliance, audits, and regulatory maintenance.
You must secure accounts with Japanese banks or banking partners approved by the JFSA, typically requiring a primary business account and a segregated customer asset account. Most major Japanese banks (MUFG, Mizuho, Sumitomo) work with licensed crypto operators, though approval timelines are 4-8 weeks. Banking relationships must be finalized before your formal JFSA application is submitted, as proof of these arrangements is a mandatory submission document.
Japan's JFSA approval process is significantly more stringent than Singapore's MAS framework, typically requiring 6-12 months versus 3-6 months in Singapore, with higher capital requirements (minimum 100 million yen for Type 1 VASP). Malta's process is generally faster (2-4 months) but Japan offers access to the world's third-largest crypto market with 100+ million potential users. Japan's regulatory clarity and stability, established since the 2017 Payment Services Act, make it more predictable long-term than emerging regulatory frameworks in other jurisdictions.
The JFSA requires detailed AML/KYC policies, transaction monitoring procedures, customer identification documentation standards, and beneficial ownership verification processes that must be submitted in both Japanese and English. You must include your compliance manual, staff training records, incident reporting procedures, and third-party risk assessment frameworks covering vendors and service providers. Additionally, you need to demonstrate integration with Japan's Financial Intelligence Unit (JAFIC) reporting systems and provide sample customer onboarding workflows.
JFSA-licensed crypto exchanges must file corporate income tax returns annually with the National Tax Agency, typically facing a corporate tax rate of approximately 23.2% on profits plus local inhabitant taxes. You must also collect and remit consumption tax (currently 10%) on crypto trading services to end consumers, and file quarterly reports detailing transaction volumes and user numbers. Capital gains taxes for retail customers remain 20.315% for crypto transactions, creating additional compliance and reporting obligations for your customer tax documentation.
Japan crypto licenses under the Payment Services Act do not have fixed renewal periods like traditional licenses; instead, you must maintain continuous compliance and report material changes to the JFSA within 30 days. Annual compliance certifications and audited financial statements are mandatory submissions each fiscal year to demonstrate ongoing adherence to capital, AML, and operational standards. The JFSA can conduct unannounced inspections and may impose stricter requirements or suspension if material breaches are discovered during your operational period.
Japan faces ongoing regulatory evolution, particularly regarding stablecoin definitions and retail investor protections, which could increase compliance costs and operational restrictions without warning. Market concentration risks exist as the JFSA has historically favored larger, well-capitalized operators, making competition with established players (GMO, bitFlyer, Liquid) extremely difficult for new entrants. Additionally, reputational risks from any major exchange hack or customer loss incident can trigger JFSA enforcement actions, license suspension, or operational mandates that significantly impact profitability and viability.
