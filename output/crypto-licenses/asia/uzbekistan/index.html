Section 01

Overview — Uzbekistan as a Crypto Hub

Uzbekistan became one of the first countries in the world to adopt a comprehensive presidential decree-level framework for cryptocurrency regulation when President Shavkat Mirziyoyev signed Decree No. UP-5813 in July 2018. This early-mover positioning, combined with deliberate government policy to attract crypto businesses and a genuinely favorable tax incentive, has made Uzbekistan an increasingly relevant jurisdiction for exchanges, crypto asset service providers, and mining operations.

The country's regulator, the National Agency for Project Management (NAPP) — operating under the direct authority of the President — oversees all crypto licensing. This structure reflects the government's top-level commitment to developing Uzbekistan's digital economy. NAPP (also referred to as the National Agency for Perspective Projects in some contexts) has issued licenses to both domestic and international operators, with Binance receiving a crypto exchange license in 2023 representing the most high-profile validation of the framework.

Notable validation: Binance received a crypto exchange license in Uzbekistan in 2023, making it one of the few jurisdictions where the world's largest exchange holds a government-issued license. This significantly enhances Uzbekistan's credibility as a legitimate licensing destination.

Uzbekistan's positioning is as Central Asia's crypto hub — competing primarily with Kazakhstan (which has the AIFC financial center framework) and, to a lesser extent, Kyrgyzstan. The combination of 0% tax on licensed exchange income, a growing Tashkent tech scene, relatively low operational costs, cheap energy for mining, and 100% foreign ownership makes the value proposition clear for businesses considering the region.

However, Uzbekistan is not without limitations. The USD 1 million minimum capital requirement for exchange licenses is meaningful, the regulatory framework is still maturing compared to established hubs like Singapore or Hong Kong, banking options for crypto businesses are limited, and international credibility — while growing — has not yet reached the level of tier-one jurisdictions. Businesses must weigh these factors against the tax and market-access benefits.

Section 02

Legal Framework — Decrees, Laws & Regulations

Uzbekistan's crypto regulatory framework is built on a foundation of Presidential Decrees, which carry supreme legal authority in the Uzbekistan legal system, supplemented by implementing regulations and, more recently, primary legislation.

Presidential Decree No. UP-5813 (July 2018) — Foundational Framework

This decree, "On measures for the development of the digital economy in the Republic of Uzbekistan," established the foundational framework for crypto regulation. Key provisions included:

  • Recognition of cryptocurrency mining as a legal commercial activity
  • Establishment of the National Agency for Project Management (NAPP) as regulator
  • Authorization for licensed crypto exchanges to operate in Uzbekistan
  • First articulation of the tax incentive framework for licensed operators
  • Foreign currency control exemptions for licensed crypto transactions
  • Prohibition on unlicensed exchange activity

Presidential Decree No. UP-60 (2022) — Updated Framework

The 2022 decree significantly updated and expanded the crypto regulatory framework, including:

  • Expanded definition of crypto assets and covered activities
  • Strengthened AML/KYC requirements aligned with FATF recommendations
  • Updated capital requirements for different license categories
  • Enhanced consumer protection provisions
  • Clarification of tax incentive scope and conditions
  • Provisions for licensed crypto custodians and wallet providers

Law on Crypto-Assets (2022)

Uzbekistan enacted primary legislation on crypto assets in 2022, providing a statutory foundation for the regulatory framework that previously rested solely on presidential decrees. The law:

  • Formally defines crypto assets, tokens, and related terms
  • Establishes the licensing regime in statutory form
  • Sets out investor protection requirements
  • Defines the roles and powers of NAPP
  • Creates a framework for token issuance (ICOs/STOs)

Currency controls: Uzbekistan maintains some currency controls, and the Uzbek som (UZS) is subject to exchange rate management. Licensed crypto exchanges are granted specific exemptions from certain foreign currency regulations, but businesses should review FVEA (Foreign Valuta Exchange Act) implications with local counsel.

AML/CFT Framework

Uzbekistan is a member of EAG (Eurasian Group on combating money laundering and financing of terrorism) and has been aligning its AML/CFT framework with FATF recommendations. Licensed crypto operators are subject to:

  • KYC/CDD obligations for all customers
  • Suspicious transaction reporting to the Financial Monitoring Department
  • Record-keeping requirements (minimum 5 years)
  • Travel Rule compliance for qualifying transfers
  • Regular AML audits

The 0% Tax Incentive — Key Conditions

The 0% tax on exchange income for licensed crypto exchange operators is the headline benefit of Uzbekistan's framework. However, the incentive is conditional:

  • Only applies to entities holding a valid NAPP crypto exchange license
  • Applies specifically to income derived from the licensed exchange activities (trading fees, spread income)
  • The standard 15% corporate tax rate applies to other income streams not covered by the incentive
  • Individual investors trading on licensed Uzbekistan exchanges also benefit from 0% personal income tax on crypto gains
  • The incentive framework may be subject to periodic review and renewal — businesses should confirm current status with NAPP

Mining advantage: Uzbekistan has abundant energy resources and has offered preferential electricity tariffs to licensed crypto miners. The government has designated specific zones for mining operations. This makes Uzbekistan competitive with Kazakhstan for CIS-region mining operations.

Custody & Wallet Licenses

NAPP also provides for licensing of crypto custody and wallet service providers. These licenses are subject to separate requirements and capital thresholds. Businesses offering these services alongside exchange activities may require multiple license types or a combined authorization — NAPP should be consulted on the appropriate structure.

Section 04

License Requirements — What NAPP Requires

The following summarizes the core requirements for obtaining a crypto exchange license from NAPP. Requirements for other license types vary and should be confirmed directly with NAPP or through local legal counsel.

Corporate Requirements

  • Entity type: Limited Liability Company (LLC/OOO) or Joint Stock Company (JSC/AO) incorporated in Uzbekistan
  • Foreign ownership: 100% foreign ownership is permitted — no local shareholder requirement
  • Registered office: Physical registered address in Uzbekistan required
  • Local presence: Some operational presence in Uzbekistan expected (at minimum a local compliance officer)
  • Directors: At least one director; residency requirement varies — confirm with NAPP

Financial Requirements

  • Minimum authorized capital: USD 1,000,000 (equivalent in UZS or approved foreign currency) for crypto exchange license
  • Capital must be deposited and demonstrated prior to or during the application process
  • Proof of funds / source of funds documentation required for shareholders
  • Financial projections and business plan demonstrating viability

Technical & Operational Requirements

  • Exchange platform — operational or at advanced development stage; NAPP may review technical specifications
  • Cybersecurity framework: penetration testing, security audit documentation
  • Cold/hot wallet custody arrangement documentation
  • Business continuity and disaster recovery plan
  • AML/CFT compliance technology: transaction monitoring system
  • KYC system capable of identity verification for Uzbek and international users

AML/CFT & Compliance Requirements

  • Comprehensive AML/CFT policy document
  • Appointed Compliance Officer (MLRO equivalent)
  • KYC/CDD procedures for individual and corporate customers
  • Suspicious activity detection and reporting procedures
  • FATF Travel Rule implementation plan
  • Staff AML training program
  • Internal audit framework

Personnel Requirements

  • Management team CVs and professional background checks
  • No criminal record for directors and significant shareholders
  • Evidence of relevant experience in financial services, technology, or crypto industry
  • Compliance Officer with AML/CFT competency

Section 05

Application Process — NAPP Crypto Exchange License

The NAPP licensing process typically takes 3 to 6 months from initial submission to license issuance. The process involves both document review and, in some cases, meetings with NAPP officials. Local legal representation is strongly recommended.

  1. Feasibility & pre-application engagement: Before formal filing, engage with NAPP (directly or via local legal counsel) to discuss your business model, confirm applicable license type, and understand current requirements. NAPP has been known to engage positively with prospective applicants. This step prevents costly mistakes in the formal application.
  2. Establish Uzbek entity: Incorporate a Limited Liability Company (OOO) or Joint Stock Company (AO) in Uzbekistan through the Ministry of Justice registration portal. Prepare charter documents, appoint directors, and establish a registered office. Foreign shareholders require apostilled corporate documents.
  3. Deposit authorized capital: Open a bank account for the Uzbek entity and deposit the required minimum capital (USD 1,000,000 equivalent for exchange license). Obtain bank confirmation of capital deposit for submission to NAPP.
  4. Prepare compliance framework: Draft and implement the AML/CFT policy, KYC procedures, internal control framework, and staff training program. Appoint a Compliance Officer. This documentation forms a core part of the license application.
  5. Technical documentation: Compile exchange platform technical specifications, architecture documentation, security audit results (from qualified cybersecurity firm), cold/hot wallet custody arrangements, and business continuity plan.
  6. Prepare application package: Compile full application including: entity incorporation documents, shareholder structure and UBO information, director/management CVs and background documentation, business plan and financial projections, AML/CFT policy, technical documentation, and capital confirmation. All documents in Uzbek and/or Russian (professional translation required for foreign-language originals).
  7. Submit application to NAPP: File application package with NAPP. Pay applicable government fees. NAPP acknowledges receipt and assigns a case reference. The formal review period begins.
  8. NAPP review and due diligence: NAPP reviews the application, conducting background checks on shareholders and directors. NAPP may request additional information, supplementary documents, or a meeting with management. Respond promptly to any queries to avoid delays.
  9. License issuance: Upon satisfactory review, NAPP issues the crypto exchange license. The license specifies permitted activities, conditions, and any ongoing reporting obligations. The entity is now authorized to operate and can apply for the 0% tax incentive registration with the tax authority.

Language requirements: Uzbekistan's official languages are Uzbek and Russian. All application documents must be translated into Uzbek (and often Russian). Engaging a local law firm with bilingual capability significantly smooths the process.

Tax Cost Comparison

For a crypto exchange generating USD 5 million annually in trading fee revenue, the tax differential between Uzbekistan and comparable jurisdictions is substantial:

  • Uzbekistan (licensed exchange): 0% = USD 0 in exchange income tax
  • Standard 15% corporate tax: USD 750,000
  • India (foreign company): 40%+ = USD 2,000,000+
  • UK: 25% = USD 1,250,000
  • Singapore: 17% = USD 850,000 (though capital gains generally not taxed)

Banking note: Opening a bank account for a crypto business in Uzbekistan has historically been challenging. Banking options have improved but remain more limited than in Singapore or Dubai. Factor banking setup time and costs into your planning. Some operators use banking in adjacent jurisdictions for non-UZS operations.

Frequently Asked Questions

NAPP stands for the National Agency for Project Management (also referred to as the National Agency for Perspective Projects). It operates directly under the President of Uzbekistan, which gives it a uniquely high-level governmental mandate. NAPP is the primary licensing authority for crypto exchanges, crypto asset exchange operators, and mining operations in Uzbekistan. It reviews and approves license applications, sets regulatory requirements, conducts ongoing supervision, and has the authority to revoke licenses for non-compliance. NAPP's position directly under presidential authority reflects the Uzbek government's strategic commitment to developing its digital economy and signals that crypto regulation is a national priority rather than a delegated technical matter.
The 0% tax on exchange income for licensed crypto exchange operators is a deliberate policy incentive introduced by Presidential Decree and confirmed by the Law on Crypto-Assets (2022). As of 2025, it remains in effect. However, tax incentive frameworks — particularly those based on regulatory policy rather than constitutional provisions — can be modified by subsequent decrees or legislation. Businesses should: (1) confirm current incentive status with NAPP or Uzbek tax counsel before basing projections on it; (2) monitor Uzbek legislative developments annually; and (3) ensure their license agreement or operating permit explicitly documents the applicable tax treatment. The political commitment to the incentive appears strong, with no signals of planned removal as of 2025, but prudent business planning should account for the possibility of future changes.
The USD 1,000,000 minimum authorized capital requirement for a crypto exchange license is intended to ensure that only financially substantial operators receive licenses. It functions as a barrier to entry that filters out undercapitalized or less serious applicants, provides a capital cushion to protect exchange users (as exchanges hold customer funds), and signals the government's intention to attract serious institutional-grade operators rather than low-quality offshore shells. Importantly, this capital is not a fee — it becomes the company's authorized capital and can be used for genuine business operations (technology development, staffing, etc.). It is not consumed in the application process. Businesses with strong balance sheets should view it as deploying working capital to Uzbekistan rather than paying a license fee.
A Uzbekistan NAPP license authorizes you to operate a crypto exchange in Uzbekistan. Whether you can use this license to serve users in other countries depends on the regulatory requirements of those other countries — not Uzbekistan. Most jurisdictions require that exchanges serving their residents obtain local authorization (or at minimum not actively solicit local users). The Uzbekistan license does not provide "passporting" rights to operate in the EU, UK, US, or most other regulated markets. It is, however, a legitimate government-issued crypto license that demonstrates regulatory compliance and may support applications to other regulators. Many international operators use Uzbekistan as one component of a multi-jurisdiction licensing strategy, holding Uzbekistan for CIS/Central Asian users while obtaining Singapore MPI or EU licenses for other markets. Do not assume the Uzbek license provides global operating rights.
Both Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan are competing to be Central Asia's dominant crypto hub, and both offer compelling frameworks. Kazakhstan's AIFC (Astana International Financial Centre) is modeled on English common law and has attracted significant interest due to its common-law framework and English-language proceedings — which many international operators find more familiar. Kazakhstan also has a larger economy and more developed financial infrastructure. However, Uzbekistan's 0% tax incentive on exchange income is a meaningful differentiator — Kazakhstan does not offer the same level of tax exemption for exchange operators. Uzbekistan also has lower operational costs and is more aggressively government-promoted. The choice between the two often comes down to: target market (Kazakhstan is larger; Uzbekistan is growing fast), legal system preference (AIFC's common law vs. Uzbekistan's civil law), banking access (Kazakhstan's banking sector is more developed), and tax sensitivity (Uzbekistan wins on exchange income tax). Some operators license in both jurisdictions for maximum regional coverage.
The primary requirement is USD 1 million in authorized capital, plus application fees of approximately USD 5,000-10,000 paid to NAPP. Additional costs include legal consultation (USD 10,000-20,000), compliance infrastructure setup (USD 20,000-50,000), and annual operational costs estimated at USD 30,000-60,000. Total initial investment typically ranges from USD 1.06 to 1.14 million depending on your chosen service providers and operational scope.
The standard timeline is 30-45 days from submission of a complete application package to NAPP, though this can extend to 60-90 days if additional documentation or clarifications are requested. The review includes background checks on beneficial owners, compliance framework assessment, and banking relationship verification. Once approved, you receive your license certificate and can begin operations within 5-10 business days.
You must establish accounts with at least one bank regulated by the Central Bank of Uzbekistan to handle fiat currency operations. Most applicants work with state-owned banks like O'zbekiston Milliy Banki or Asaka Bank, which have experience with crypto businesses. Banking partners require proof of NAPP licensing and comprehensive AML/KYC procedures compliant with Uzbek law.
Licensed operators must submit quarterly financial reports to NAPP, maintain transaction records for seven years, and implement robust AML/KYC procedures per international standards. You are required to report suspicious transactions to the Financial Intelligence Center (FIU) within 24 hours and conduct annual independent audits by firms approved by the Ministry of Finance. Non-compliance can result in fines up to USD 50,000 or license suspension.
Uzbekistan offers the lowest capital requirement (USD 1 million) compared to Malta (EUR 100,000 minimum plus higher compliance costs) and Singapore (SGD 1 million or approximately USD 740,000, plus operational expenses). However, Uzbekistan has less developed banking infrastructure and smaller market access than Malta or Singapore. The 0% corporate tax incentive in Uzbekistan until 2025 and subsequent favorable treatment remains a significant advantage over competitors.
Licenses must be renewed every three years with NAPP through a formal renewal application that includes updated compliance documentation, financial statements, and beneficial ownership verification. Renewal fees are approximately USD 3,000-5,000, and the process typically takes 20-30 days. Non-renewal results in automatic license termination, requiring a full new application if you wish to resume operations.
Uzbekistan's crypto regulatory framework is still evolving, with NAPP issuing guidance updates that may affect operations without advance notice. International sanctions compliance is critical, as Uzbekistan faces scrutiny for AML standards from FATF and other bodies. Additionally, banking relationships can be terminated if international partner banks become risk-averse, and the government has historically shown preference for state-controlled initiatives over private crypto businesses.
