Overview

Vietnam's Path from Grey Zone to Formal Regulation

Vietnam presents one of the most compelling market entry opportunities in global crypto — not because of an established licensing framework, but precisely because that framework is being built right now. The country has consistently ranked among the top five nations in the Chainalysis Global Crypto Adoption Index, driven by widespread retail participation, an enormous P2P trading ecosystem, and DeFi activity that punches well above its weight relative to GDP.

The legal landscape shifted meaningfully in 2024 when the government issued Decree 52/2024, establishing a controlled pilot sandbox for crypto exchanges. This was the clearest signal yet that Vietnamese authorities — led by the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) and Ministry of Finance (MoF) — intend to regulate, not ban, digital assets. The sandbox allows selected operators to provide crypto exchange services under government supervision while the broader legislative framework is finalised.

Crypto assets are not recognised as legal tender in Vietnam, and no dedicated crypto law exists as of early 2025. However, the government has explicitly committed to developing a comprehensive virtual asset regulatory framework, with a formal VASP licensing regime expected to be introduced between 2025 and 2026. Personal income tax applies to crypto gains, and corporations are subject to the standard 20% rate.

For operators with a long-term view on Southeast Asia, the question is not whether to pursue Vietnam — it is how to position for first-mover advantage before the formal regime opens. Operators who engage now, understand the sandbox process, and build compliance infrastructure will be first in line when the licensing window opens.

Strategic Framing: Vietnam is not a "get licensed today" jurisdiction — it is a "prepare now, win the market tomorrow" jurisdiction. The operators who build compliance frameworks, local presence, and regulatory relationships ahead of the formal licensing regime will hold an insurmountable advantage over late movers.

Regulatory Framework

State Bank of Vietnam, MoF & the Dual Regulator Model

Vietnam's crypto oversight sits across two primary agencies. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) holds authority over payment systems and monetary policy, making it the principal regulator for crypto exchanges and custodians that touch VND flows. The Ministry of Finance (MoF) has oversight responsibility for investment products and securities-adjacent instruments, which may extend to certain token types and derivative products. The Ministry of Science and Technology has been consulted on technology standards and blockchain infrastructure.

This dual-regulator model creates both complexity and opportunity. Complexity because operators must anticipate the requirements of two agencies rather than one; opportunity because engagement with both regulators early in the process builds credibility and speeds approval once the formal framework launches.

Decree 52/2024 is the operative instrument for 2024–2025. It authorises the SBV to run a controlled pilot programme under which a limited number of crypto exchanges may operate legally under close supervisory oversight. Participation in the pilot sandbox requires a formal application and government approval — it is not a self-certification regime.

Sandbox Application Process

How to Enter Vietnam's Pilot Programme

The sandbox established by Decree 52/2024 operates on a selective approval basis. The government evaluates applicant exchanges on their technical capabilities, compliance infrastructure, financial soundness, and the degree to which their operations can be monitored under controlled conditions. Timeline from application to sandbox approval is estimated at 3–6 months, though this may vary given the novelty of the framework.

Market Opportunity

Why Vietnam Is Southeast Asia's Most Important Crypto Market

Vietnam is not merely a large market — it is the world's most crypto-engaged large market by adoption intensity. In every annual Chainalysis Global Crypto Adoption Index since 2020, Vietnam has ranked in the top five globally, frequently at position one or two. This is not driven by institutional capital but by genuine grassroots usage across tens of millions of retail participants.

The adoption drivers are structural, not speculative. Vietnam's remittance economy — one of the largest in Asia as a share of GDP — drives enormous demand for fast, low-cost cross-border value transfer, a need that crypto fulfils better than traditional banking for significant portions of the population. P2P trading platforms have flourished in the absence of formal exchanges, demonstrating real demand that is currently channelled through unregulated channels.

The demographic profile amplifies this: Vietnam's median age is 31, with over 70% of the population under 40. The country has produced a substantial technology workforce — over one million software developers — and Ho Chi Minh City has emerged as one of Southeast Asia's most active fintech startup hubs. Operational costs remain well below Singapore, Thailand, and other regional peers, making Vietnam attractive for building engineering and compliance teams.

DeFi participation is high by regional standards, with Vietnamese users among the most active on decentralised protocols globally. The gaming-adjacent crypto economy — particularly play-to-earn models that gained prominence through projects headquartered in Vietnam — demonstrated the country's capacity to generate and absorb crypto-native products at scale.

Upcoming VASP Framework

What to Expect from Vietnam's 2026 Licensing Regime

The Vietnamese government has publicly committed to introducing a comprehensive virtual asset regulatory framework. The National Assembly's legislative agenda for 2025–2026 includes provisions for crypto regulation, and the Ministry of Finance has been tasked with drafting the primary legislation. While the exact contours of the formal VASP licensing regime are not yet published, observable signals from regulatory consultations and comparable ASEAN frameworks suggest the following likely features.

The regime will almost certainly require a dedicated VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider) licence for entities operating crypto exchanges, custodians, and OTC desks serving Vietnamese residents. Capital adequacy requirements are expected, likely in the range of VND 5–20 billion (approximately $200,000–$800,000). Mandatory local incorporation, Vietnamese directors, and data localisation requirements are consistent with Vietnam's general digital services regulatory posture and highly probable in any crypto law.

AML/CFT obligations will align with FATF Recommendations, which Vietnam has committed to implementing across its financial sector. The Travel Rule — requiring transmission of originator and beneficiary information with virtual asset transfers — is expected to be mandated. Consumer protection provisions, including segregation of client assets and mandatory disclosure requirements, are highly likely given the retail-heavy nature of Vietnam's crypto usage.

Operators who participate in the sandbox prior to formal licensing launch will have significant advantages: established relationships with regulators, compliant infrastructure already in place, and documented operating history that supports a full licence application. The sandbox is, in effect, an extended audition for the formal regime.

Tax Framework

Vietnam Crypto Tax — What Operators & Traders Need to Know

Vietnam's tax treatment of crypto is evolving alongside its regulatory framework. Under current rules, personal income derived from crypto trading and investment is subject to Personal Income Tax (PIT). The applicable rate depends on the characterisation of the income: capital gains-type income from asset transfers is typically taxed at a flat 0.1% on gross transaction value under PIT rules for securities-adjacent income, though this characterisation is not formally confirmed for crypto and individual assessments vary.

Corporate entities generating revenue from crypto-related activities are subject to the standard Corporate Income Tax (CIT) rate of 20%. No capital gains tax exemption applies. VAT treatment of crypto transactions has not been formally clarified but general guidance suggests crypto-to-fiat transactions may be treated as financial services, which are VAT-exempt in Vietnam.

The Ministry of Finance is expected to issue detailed guidance on crypto tax treatment as part of the broader regulatory framework development. Operators should monitor these developments closely, as the formal tax rules will significantly affect the economics of Vietnam operations.
