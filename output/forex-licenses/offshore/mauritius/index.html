Overview

Why Choose Mauritius for Forex Licensing

Mauritius occupies a unique position in the offshore forex licensing landscape. It is more regulated than Seychelles or Vanuatu — with genuine FSC supervision, FATF compliance, and mandatory local substance requirements — but more accessible and cheaper than onshore regulators like CySEC or ASIC.

The key advantage of Mauritius is its double tax treaty network. With 45+ treaties covering India, China, South Africa, and most African and SAARC nations, Mauritius is the premier jurisdiction for brokers focused on these markets. Indian-origin broker groups in particular frequently use Mauritius as their primary holding and operating jurisdiction due to the India-Mauritius treaty framework.

Mauritius banks (MCB, SBM, AfrAsia) are willing to work with FSC-licensed forex brokers and provide multi-currency accounts in USD, EUR, GBP, and regional currencies. This banking access is a major advantage over Seychelles, Vanuatu, or Comoros-licensed brokers who must rely on EMIs and offshore banks.

The Global Business Company (GBC) is the corporate vehicle — the company structure used by international businesses in Mauritius, providing access to tax treaty benefits. The Investment Dealer license is the financial services authorization from the FSC that permits the GBC to offer forex and securities dealing services. Both are needed to operate a fully compliant Mauritius forex brokerage.

Mauritius-licensed brokers have access to domestic banks including MCB (Mauritius Commercial Bank), SBM (State Bank of Mauritius), and AfrAsia Bank — all reputable institutions. International EMIs also accept Mauritius FSC licensees more readily than lower-tier offshore jurisdictions. Multi-currency accounts and client fund segregation are readily available.
The application and initial licensing fee typically ranges from USD 5,000 to USD 15,000, depending on the license category and the FSC's assessment. Additional costs include legal fees (USD 3,000-8,000), compliance setup, and initial capital requirements which vary by license type but generally start at USD 100,000 minimum. Annual renewal fees are approximately USD 2,000-4,000, making Mauritius relatively affordable compared to EU or UK jurisdictions.
The standard processing timeline is 8 to 16 weeks from submission of a complete application to the Financial Services Commission (FSC). This assumes all documentation is provided upfront and no material deficiencies are identified during review. Complex applications or those requiring enhanced due diligence may extend to 20+ weeks.
The FSC requires a minimum paid-up capital of USD 100,000 for standard GBC forex operations, though this may be higher (USD 250,000-500,000) depending on your business model and client base. Capital must be deposited in a Mauritian bank account and verified before license issuance. Leverage and client fund handling requirements may necessitate additional reserve capital.
Yes, the FSC requires that GBC licensees maintain a registered office address in Mauritius with a local representative or compliance officer present. The office does not need to be large, but it must be verifiable and meet FSC standards for document storage and regulatory access. Many firms use co-working spaces or registered agent offices to fulfill this requirement cost-effectively.
Licensed forex brokers must submit quarterly financial reports, maintain client segregated accounts, conduct annual audits, and implement AML/KYC procedures compliant with FATF standards. The FSC also requires annual fitness and propriety assessments of directors and senior management, regular compliance certifications, and participation in the Mauritius Financial Intelligence Unit's reporting regime. Failure to meet these obligations can result in fines ranging from USD 10,000 to USD 100,000 or license suspension.
A Mauritius GBC license has more restrictions on EU client acceptance without additional registrations, whereas CySEC (Cyprus) provides automatic EU passporting rights. However, Mauritius licenses are faster to obtain (8-16 weeks vs 12-24 weeks) and less expensive, while offering competitive tax benefits and lower ongoing compliance costs. For non-EU-focused brokers, Mauritius is preferable; for EU-centric operations, CySEC remains the standard choice.
Required documents include a detailed business plan, audited financial statements (if available), proof of minimum capital, CVs and background checks for all directors and beneficial owners, compliance policies, risk management frameworks, and a director's declaration form. Additionally, you must provide proof of the registered office, banking references, and detailed information on your technology/trading platform. The FSC may request supplementary documentation during the review process, so allowing time for back-and-forth is advisable.
