Overview

Cape Verde: EU-Adjacent African Offshore Jurisdiction

Note: Cape Verde is an emerging jurisdiction. Regulatory framework for international financial services is still developing. Best suited for brokers seeking an EU-adjacent African offshore presence with currency stability.

Cape Verde is a small Atlantic archipelago nation located 600km west of Senegal, with a population of approximately 550,000. A former Portuguese colony, it maintains strong institutional, cultural, and financial ties to Portugal and the EU. Its political stability (ranked highest in mainland Africa by several governance indices), EUR-pegged currency, and Portuguese legal heritage distinguish it from other African offshore centers.

The Banco de Cabo Verde (BCV) serves as both central bank and primary financial regulator. The National Council for Financial System Stability (CNEF) framework governs financial services authorization. Cape Verde is not yet a widely used offshore forex licensing jurisdiction — it is primarily known as an emerging market for Portuguese and West African-facing financial services operations.

The EUR peg (at 110.265 CVE per euro, guaranteed by the Portuguese state) provides exceptional currency stability for a sub-Saharan African jurisdiction, allowing euro-denominated contracts and accounting without currency risk. This makes it particularly attractive for European-origin broker groups seeking an African operating footprint.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Cape Verde has a functioning banking and financial services regulatory framework under the BCV. It is a legitimate sovereign nation with a stable democratic government. However, as an emerging offshore jurisdiction for international forex services, its framework for international brokers is still developing compared to Seychelles or Mauritius. We recommend it primarily for niche use cases targeting West African or Lusophone markets.
The Cape Verdean escudo (CVE) is pegged to the euro at 110.265 CVE = 1 EUR, guaranteed by Portugal under a mutual agreement. This provides complete currency stability for EUR-denominated operations, simplifies financial planning, and signals alignment with EU financial norms. For brokers using EUR as their base operating currency, this eliminates FX risk at the entity level.
Cape Verde-licensed brokers are best positioned for West African client bases, Portuguese-speaking markets including Angola, Mozambique, Guinea-Bissau, and Brazil, and European brokers seeking an EU-adjacent African presence. The Portuguese legal heritage and EUR-pegged currency make it naturally accessible for these markets compared to Anglophone African jurisdictions.
Cape Verde is less established as a forex licensing jurisdiction. Seychelles FSA and Mauritius FSC have wider international recognition among banking partners, liquidity providers, and prime brokers. Cape Verde offers EUR stability and EU/Portugal ties not available in those jurisdictions, but is recommended mainly for specific Lusophone/West African positioning rather than as a general offshore licensing choice.
Cape Verde has several domestic banks including Banco Comercial do Atlântico (BCA), Caixa Económica de Cabo Verde, and Ecobank Cape Verde. Portuguese banking connections and EUR-pegged stability make banking more accessible than in some other African offshore centers. International EMIs with Africa exposure may also accept Cape Verde-licensed entities subject to their own risk assessments.
Initial setup costs for a Cape Verde forex license typically range from USD 15,000 to USD 35,000, including application fees, legal documentation, and regulatory filings with the Bank of Cape Verde. Annual maintenance and compliance fees generally cost USD 5,000 to USD 10,000 depending on your trading volume and client base. These costs are significantly lower than EU or UK licensing, making Cape Verde attractive for startups and small-to-medium brokers.
The complete licensing process from application submission to final approval typically takes 8 to 12 weeks, assuming all documentation is submitted correctly and no additional information requests arise from the Bank of Cape Verde. Initial completeness reviews can take 2-3 weeks, followed by substantive review and background checks on beneficial owners and senior management. Expedited processing is sometimes available for an additional fee, potentially reducing timeline to 6-8 weeks.
Cape Verde requires licensed forex brokers to maintain minimum paid-up capital of CVE 2,000,000 (approximately USD 20,000-25,000 as of 2026), though the Bank of Cape Verde may impose higher requirements based on projected client funds and trading volumes. This capital must be held in a local bank and remain segregated from operational accounts. Additional operational reserves may be required depending on your business plan and risk assessment.
Applicants must provide articles of incorporation, ownership structure documentation, detailed business plans, compliance manuals, AML/KYC policies, CV and background checks for all directors and beneficial owners holding 10% or more equity, and proof of local management or representative presence. Financial statements from the past 2-3 years are required if the parent company is established, and detailed information about your proposed client acquisition and retention strategies. All documents must be certified and, if originally in English, translated into Portuguese by an official translator.
Cape Verde brokers are subject to corporate income tax at 10-15% depending on profit levels, along with a 6% VAT on services and an annual license fee to the financial regulator. The jurisdiction offers attractive tax treatment compared to EU jurisdictions, though you must file annual financial statements with the Bank of Cape Verde and maintain detailed transaction records for regulatory inspection. Companies must also comply with FATCA and CRS reporting requirements for international clients.
The primary risk is reputational contagion from less-regulated competitors operating under similar jurisdictions, which has prompted the Bank of Cape Verde to increase scrutiny of marketing practices and client acquisition methods since 2024. Cape Verde brokers face pressure to implement robust AML/KYC systems exceeding local minimums to satisfy international correspondent banks and payment processors, many of whom have deprioritized African forex jurisdictions. Additionally, regulatory changes are possible given evolving FATF guidance, and the smaller supervisory body compared to larger jurisdictions means fewer resources for brokers seeking regulatory guidance.
Cape Verde forex licenses must be renewed annually, with renewal applications typically submitted 60-90 days before the license expiration date to the Bank of Cape Verde. The renewal process requires submission of updated financial statements, compliance certifications, beneficial ownership confirmations, and payment of the annual regulatory fee (typically USD 1,500-3,000). Renewal is generally granted automatically if the licensee remains in good regulatory standing and has not violated compliance requirements, though the Bank of Cape Verde reserves the right to conduct additional due diligence.
