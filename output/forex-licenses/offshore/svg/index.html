Overview

SVG IBC: Registration vs License

SVG operates under the International Business Companies (IBC) Act, which allows the formation of international companies for conducting business outside of SVG. The SVG Financial Services Authority (FSA) registers these companies but does not regulate their forex trading activities — it is a company registry function, not a financial services supervisory role.

This distinction is important: SVG-registered brokers are often described as "licensed" but technically operate as registered IBCs. There is no SVG regulatory oversight of trading operations, no mandatory capital requirements, no client fund segregation requirements, and no AML supervisory authority. Brokers are governed by their own internal policies and the applicable law of their operational jurisdiction.

Despite this, SVG is globally accepted by hundreds of operating brokers and is widely recognized by traders as the entry-tier offshore registration. Its primary advantages are speed, cost, and simplicity. For many brokers, SVG is the starting point before upgrading to a Seychelles or Vanuatu regulated license.

Frequently Asked Questions

SVG provides IBC registration under the IBC Act — this is a company registration, not a financial services regulatory license. The FSA does not regulate forex trading activities; it only maintains the corporate registry. SVG-registered brokers are legally incorporated entities but operate without dedicated financial services regulatory oversight.
An SVG IBC is a company registration allowing legal incorporation for forex business. A regulated broker (Seychelles FSA, Vanuatu VFSC, CySEC) holds a specific financial services license with regulatory oversight, mandatory AML compliance, capital requirements, and client protection obligations. Regulated brokers have significantly better banking access and international credibility.
No. SVG registration does not authorize active solicitation of EU retail clients. Actively targeting EU traders without MiFID II authorization violates EU law. EU clients who independently discover your platform is technically permitted under reverse solicitation rules, but this is risky without proper legal advice and firm geo-blocking policies.
SVG is one of the hardest jurisdictions for banking. Most EMIs and established banks decline SVG-only broker applications. Options include niche offshore banks in Georgia, Saint Kitts, or some Baltic institutions. Many SVG brokers use informal crypto payment processors initially. Upgrading to Seychelles FSA dramatically improves banking prospects.
The IBC registration itself takes 2–4 weeks from document submission. This is the fastest incorporation in the offshore forex world. However, post-incorporation banking setup can take an additional 1–3 months. The total time from decision to fully operational broker is typically 6–10 weeks for SVG.
Initial setup costs typically range from $3,000 to $8,000, including incorporation fees ($800-1,200), annual license fees ($2,000-4,000), and legal/compliance documentation ($1,500-3,000). Additional costs for ongoing compliance, accounting, and banking requirements can add another $500-1,500 annually. Exact pricing depends on your service provider and the complexity of your business structure.
Required documents include a completed application form, proof of identity (passport), detailed business plan, anti-money laundering (AML) policy, know-your-customer (KYC) procedures, financial projections, beneficial ownership declaration, and proof of registered office in SVG. The Financial Services Authority (FSA) may request additional documentation such as proof of working capital and director background checks.
SVG offers faster approval (4-8 weeks vs. 8-16 weeks for CySEC) and lower initial costs ($3,000-8,000 vs. $10,000-20,000), but CySEC provides stronger regulatory credibility and better EU client access for certain services. CySEC requires higher minimum capital requirements and more stringent operational standards, while SVG is more lenient but carries reputational risks in regulated markets as of 2026.
SVG Forex companies are subject to standard corporate income tax of approximately 0-32% depending on profit levels and applicable tax treaties. However, many operators structure through SVG IBCs (International Business Companies) to minimize tax exposure, though substance requirements are increasing in 2026. You should consult a tax advisor familiar with FATCA, CRS, and your home country's controlled foreign corporation rules.
SVG licenses require annual renewal by December 31st each year, with renewal fees typically between $2,000-4,000. Renewal requires updated compliance documentation, audited financial statements, confirmation of ongoing AML/KYC procedures, and proof of legitimate business operations. Failure to renew on time can result in license suspension or revocation by the FSA.
SVG does not mandate a specific minimum capital requirement as of 2026, but the FSA expects licensees to maintain sufficient working capital to cover operational costs and client segregation obligations, typically $50,000-500,000 depending on trading volume and client base. Capital adequacy is assessed on a case-by-case basis, and you must demonstrate ability to handle client deposits securely.
SVG licensing carries elevated risks of debanking, payment processor rejections, and exclusion from regulated markets due to the jurisdiction's FATF gray list status historically. Major compliance risks include stricter customer verification requirements, potential sanctions exposure, and reputational damage when dealing with institutional clients who require higher regulatory standards. You should maintain comprehensive compliance records and consider supplementary compliance frameworks aligned with FATF recommendations.
