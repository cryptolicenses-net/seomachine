Onshore Regulation — North America

USA Forex Dealer License (NFA/CFTC)

The United States operates the world's most demanding forex regulatory framework. The Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) and National Futures Association (NFA) impose $20 million minimum capital, strict leverage caps, and intensive ongoing compliance requirements on any firm seeking to deal retail forex in the US market.

Framework

CFTC & NFA Regulatory Framework

Retail forex trading in the United States is governed by two overlapping regulatory bodies: the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC), a federal independent agency that sets the rules, and the National Futures Association (NFA), a self-regulatory organisation (SRO) that registers participants and enforces NFA rules. Any firm dealing retail forex in the US must be registered with both.

The legal foundation is the Commodity Exchange Act (CEA) as amended by the Dodd-Frank Act of 2010. The Dodd-Frank Act dramatically tightened retail forex regulation by establishing the RFED registration category and mandating the $20 million net capital floor — a rule explicitly designed to eliminate under-capitalised operators from the US market.

The regulatory framework covers spot forex, forex forwards, forex options, and related OTC derivatives. Exchange-traded forex futures and options are regulated separately under a different framework through CME Group and other exchanges, with lower capital requirements for members.

FIFO and Hedging Rules

US regulation imposes a First-In-First-Out (FIFO) rule that prohibits clients from maintaining multiple positions in the same currency pair simultaneously in the same account. Hedging (holding simultaneous long and short positions in the same pair) is also prohibited. These restrictions are commercially significant and a major reason many experienced forex traders prefer offshore-regulated brokers.

However, IB registration still requires NFA membership, background checks, examinations, and AML programme requirements.

AML Programme & Bank Secrecy Act Compliance

All RFEDs must maintain a written Anti-Money Laundering (AML) programme that complies with the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and related FinCEN regulations. The NFA's Compliance Rule 2-9(c) requires an AML programme that includes: customer identification procedures, ongoing customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting (SARs), and regular staff training.

RFEDs must file Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) for cash transactions exceeding $10,000 and Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) for transactions that may involve money laundering, fraud, or terrorist financing. FinCEN registration is mandatory. The AML programme must be approved by senior management and subject to independent testing at least annually.

OFAC Sanctions Screening

All RFEDs must screen clients and transactions against OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control) sanctions lists, which include specially designated nationals (SDNs) and sanctioned countries. Violations of OFAC sanctions can result in significant civil and criminal penalties. Integration with a real-time sanctions screening provider is standard practice.

NFA Proficiency Requirements: Associated Persons (APs) — anyone who solicits US retail forex clients — must pass the Series 3 National Commodity Futures Examination or obtain a Series 34 Retail Off-Exchange Forex Examination. All APs must be registered with NFA and undergo fingerprinting and background checks.

Why Brokers Avoid the US

Why Most Forex Brokers Exclude US Clients

The overwhelming majority of international retail forex brokers — including firms regulated by FCA, ASIC, CySEC, and offshore regulators — explicitly prohibit US residents from opening accounts. This is not an oversight; it is a deliberate commercial decision driven by the regulatory and financial burden of US compliance.

Warning: Forex brokers without CFTC/NFA registration are legally prohibited from soliciting US retail clients. Violations result in CFTC enforcement actions, injunctions, and substantial civil monetary penalties. The CFTC actively pursues cases against offshore brokers serving US residents.

Key Deterrents

  • $20M capital requirement locked in liquid net capital — extremely costly
  • 50:1 leverage cap vs 500:1+ offshore — uncompetitive product
  • FIFO rule and hedging ban — reduces appeal to experienced traders
  • Monthly NFA financial reporting with full transparency requirements
  • High ongoing compliance costs: CCO salary, annual audit, NFA fees
  • Litigation risk from US client disputes in US courts
  • State money transmitter licensing may also be required in some states

Alternative Structures for Accessing US Market

Some international brokers structure their operations to serve the US market indirectly — for example, by establishing a US-based Introducing Broker (IB) that introduces clients to a CFTC-registered RFED. Others operate institutional desks under separate exemptions for eligible contract participants (ECPs) — sophisticated institutional clients with $10M+ in assets. Retail access without full RFED registration is not legally possible.

The NFA requires annual renewal of RFED registration with an annual membership fee of approximately USD 3,500-5,000 as of 2026. Renewal involves submitting updated financial statements, compliance certifications, and demonstration of continued adherence to NFA Rule 4-43 regarding capital maintenance and operational standards. Failure to renew by the deadline results in loss of registration and inability to service US clients.
Licensed RFEDs must file Form 8-R financial statements annually with the NFA, undergo independent compliance audits at least annually, maintain daily monitoring of hedge effectiveness, and submit quarterly Reports of Financial Condition. The NFA conducts routine examinations every 2-3 years, with heightened frequency if compliance issues are identified. You must also report any material changes to business operations, ownership, or financial condition within 10 business days.
As a Swiss-based RFED applicant, you'll face potential double-taxation but can leverage US-Swiss tax treaties to claim foreign tax credits. The US taxes RFED entities on worldwide income from US clients, so you must register for an EIN and file US tax returns regardless of Swiss residency. We recommend consulting with a US tax specialist experienced in regulated financial services, as improper structuring can create significant tax liability in both jurisdictions.
If denied, the NFA provides written reasons for rejection; common causes include insufficient capital, weak compliance infrastructure, or problematic principals. You may reapply after addressing deficiencies, though reapplication involves similar fees (USD 1,500-3,000) and timelines of 6-12 months. Repeated denials over consecutive years may prompt the NFA to require additional documentation or evidence of material operational improvements before considering future applications.
US RFED licensing is typically more expensive than FCA regulation in the UK (USD 50,000-200,000 total cost) or CySEC in Cyprus (USD 30,000-100,000), but offers superior market access to US retail clients worth the premium. Regulatory ongoing costs are also higher in the US, with annual NFA and compliance expenses around USD 50,000-150,000 versus USD 20,000-50,000 in EU jurisdictions. The choice depends on whether your target market justifies the additional regulatory burden and cost.
