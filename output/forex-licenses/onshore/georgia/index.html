Overview

Georgia's NBG: Credible, Fast, and Tax-Efficient

Georgia has emerged as one of the most attractive onshore forex licensing jurisdictions for brokers seeking a credible regulatory framework without the complexity and cost of full EU licensing. The National Bank of Georgia (NBG), established under the Organic Law of Georgia on the National Bank of Georgia, serves as the primary financial regulator, overseeing banks, non-banking depository institutions, and financial enterprises including forex dealers.

The Georgian forex licensing framework is governed primarily by the Law of Georgia on Activities of Commercial Banks, associated NBG regulations on Forex Dealers, and the country's AML/CFT legislation. Georgia's financial regulation modernization accelerated significantly following the EU-Georgia Association Agreement (in force since 2016), which mandated alignment with EU financial services standards as part of the Deep and Comprehensive Free Trade Area (DCFTA).

Unlike offshore jurisdictions, Georgia has a functioning domestic banking system, a stable currency (Georgian Lari, GEL), robust digital infrastructure (Tbilisi ranks in the top 50 global startup ecosystems), and a young, English-speaking professional workforce. These operational advantages, combined with the Virtual Zone tax regime, create a genuinely compelling case for brokers targeting non-EU international markets.

Regulatory Authority: The National Bank of Georgia

The NBG operates independently from the Georgian government, maintains its own supervisory framework, and publishes annual supervisory reviews. The NBG's Supervisory Department reviews forex dealer applications, conducts on-site inspections, and enforces ongoing compliance. The NBG is not an EU institution but is aligned with Basel III capital standards and FATF AML recommendations.

Virtual Zone Tax Benefits

0% Corporate Tax — The Virtual Zone Advantage

Georgia's Virtual Zone (officially "IT Company" status under Georgian Tax Code Article 99) provides a 0% corporate income tax on profits earned from the provision of information technology services to persons outside Georgia. The Georgian Revenue Service broadly interprets "IT services" to include technology-mediated financial services — including online forex brokerage platforms — when the primary client base is outside Georgia.

For a forex broker incorporated in Georgia with Virtual Zone status, the tax treatment is compelling: profits from non-Georgian clients are exempt from Georgia's standard 15% corporate income tax. Dividends paid from Virtual Zone profits are also exempt from Georgian withholding tax.

The standard Georgian corporate tax is 15%, applied on a distributed profit basis (similar to Estonia's system — tax is paid when profits are distributed rather than when earned). Even without Virtual Zone status, Georgia's tax regime is competitive. With Virtual Zone status, it becomes one of the most efficient onshore jurisdictions globally.

Virtual Zone Eligibility: To qualify for Virtual Zone status, the forex broker must obtain formal certification from the Georgian Ministry of Finance confirming IT company status. Applications require a description of the IT infrastructure, software platforms used, and evidence that the primary revenue source is technology-mediated services to non-Georgian clients. Legal advice from a Georgia-qualified tax attorney is strongly recommended.

AML/CFT Framework

Georgia AML: FATF-Aligned Standards

Georgia joined the FATF-Style Regional Body (MONEYVAL) and has undergone multiple mutual evaluation rounds, consistently improving its AML/CFT compliance ratings. The Law of Georgia on Facilitating the Prevention of Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (the "AML Law") imposes comprehensive obligations on forex dealers.

  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD) for all clients — verification of identity, beneficial ownership, and source of funds
  • Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), high-value clients, and clients from high-risk jurisdictions
  • Ongoing transaction monitoring using risk-based approach
  • Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) filed with Georgia's Financial Monitoring Service (GFMS/Safinamo)
  • Annual AML risk assessments and staff training programs
  • Record-keeping for minimum 5 years from end of business relationship
  • Sanctions screening against UN, EU, OFAC, and Georgian National Sanctions lists

For forex brokers with Virtual Zone status, earnings from non-Georgian clients are completely tax-free, and dividends from those profits carry no Georgian withholding tax — making it one of the most efficient onshore structures globally.

The NBG forex dealer licensing process typically takes 3–6 months from submission. Company incorporation in Georgia takes only 1–3 days. The NBG review itself typically concludes within 2–4 months for a complete application. Virtual Zone status can be obtained in parallel, usually within 4–8 weeks.

Georgia is FATF-aligned and requires full AML/CFT programs including CDD, EDD for high-risk clients, transaction monitoring, STR filing with Georgia's Financial Monitoring Service (GFMS), annual risk assessments, staff training, and 5-year record-keeping. Sanctions screening against UN, EU, and OFAC lists is mandatory.
The National Bank of Georgia charges an application fee of approximately 5,000-10,000 GEL (roughly 1,500-3,000 EUR) plus ongoing annual supervision fees ranging from 2,000-5,000 GEL depending on your trading volume and risk profile. Additional costs include legal and compliance setup, which typically runs 5,000-15,000 EUR through local advisors. Total initial investment usually falls between 20,000-40,000 EUR when accounting for all professional fees and regulatory expenses.
Georgia forex licensees can open accounts with major Georgian banks such as TBC Bank and Bank of Georgia, which offer forex trading settlement and custody services. However, correspondent banking relationships with Western institutions (US, EU) remain limited due to Georgia's regulatory positioning, making international client settlements more complex than traditional EU-regulated brokers. Many operators establish additional banking relationships in EU jurisdictions to improve client access to international payment rails.
The NBG requires notarized articles of incorporation, beneficial ownership documentation, detailed business plans covering trading operations and risk management, financial projections for 3 years, CVs and background checks for all directors and key compliance officers, and proof of registered office in Georgia. All non-Georgian documents must be officially apostilled and translated into Georgian by certified translators. The complete documentation package typically comprises 50-100 pages and takes 4-8 weeks to properly assemble.
Georgia's NBG license costs 50-70% less than CySEC (approximately 20,000-40,000 EUR vs 50,000-100,000 EUR) and has faster initial approval timelines of 60-90 days versus 120-180 days for CySEC. However, CySEC provides direct EU market access and stronger international banking relationships, while NBG licenses face restrictions with Western correspondent banks and no EU passporting rights. Georgia's lower costs suit operators targeting emerging markets or those with limited capital, whereas CySEC suits firms needing EU client acquisition.
The NBG requires licensees to maintain minimum capital of 100,000 GEL (approximately 30,000 EUR) at all times, calculated quarterly based on audited financial statements. Capital adequacy ratios must typically exceed 20% for forex operations, with higher buffers required if leverage exceeds 30:1. The NBG conducts annual stress testing to ensure firms can absorb potential client losses, and capital breaches result in mandatory operational restrictions within 10 business days.
Georgia NBG licenses must be renewed annually, with renewal applications submitted 60 days before expiration to avoid operational lapses. The renewal process requires updated financial statements, compliance certifications, beneficial ownership declarations, and payment of the annual supervision fee of 2,000-5,000 GEL. The NBG typically approves renewals within 30-45 days if no compliance issues exist; however, any unresolved customer complaints or regulatory violations can delay renewal by 60-90 days or result in license suspension.
The primary risks include increasingly strict FATCA and CRS reporting requirements for clients with US or other jurisdictional connections, mandatory implementation of transaction monitoring systems compliant with FATF guidelines, and potential de-risking by correspondent banks affecting client fund transfers. The NBG has intensified focus on suspicious transaction reporting and market manipulation detection, with penalties for violations reaching 50,000-500,000 GEL. Firms must also navigate geopolitical uncertainties affecting Georgia's banking sector and maintain updated cyber-security protocols to meet international standards.
