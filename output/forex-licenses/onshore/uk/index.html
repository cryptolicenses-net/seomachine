Overview

FCA Authorization for Forex Brokers

The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) is the UK's primary conduct regulator for financial services firms. For forex brokers, FCA authorization is granted under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA) and various statutory instruments covering investment services, client money, and conduct of business. FCA-authorized forex brokers are listed on the FCA Register and subject to continuous supervision.

FCA regulation is considered the global benchmark for broker credibility. Major prime brokers (UBS, Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas), liquidity providers, and institutional counterparties routinely require FCA or equivalent authorization from their broker clients. For any broker seeking institutional-grade prime brokerage, FCA authorization is virtually mandatory.

Since Brexit (January 31, 2020), UK FCA authorization no longer carries MiFID II passporting rights into the EU. Brokers wishing to serve retail EU clients need a separate EU license (typically CySEC or MFSA). Many large forex groups maintain both FCA (for UK/global institutional) and CySEC (for EU retail) entities.

Post-Brexit reality: FCA authorization covers the UK market and is internationally recognized, but does not automatically permit EU retail client solicitation. Brokers targeting both UK and EU retail must budget for dual licensing.

Compliance

COBS, SYSC & SM&CR Requirements

FCA-authorized forex brokers must comply with multiple components of the FCA Handbook. The most relevant are COBS (Conduct of Business Sourcebook), SYSC (Senior Management Arrangements, Systems and Controls), CASS (Client Assets Sourcebook), and the SM&CR (Senior Managers and Certification Regime).

  • COBS 2: Client communications, fair, clear and not misleading
  • COBS 4: Risk warnings (CFD/leverage products — FCA mandates 74% loss disclosure)
  • COBS 9A: Appropriateness assessments for complex products
  • COBS 11: Best execution obligation and annual best execution report
  • COBS 16: Trade confirmations and periodic statements
  • SYSC 4: Organizational requirements — governance, risk management
  • SYSC 6: Compliance, internal audit, AML systems and controls
  • CASS 7: Client money rules — segregation, daily reconciliation, CASS audit
  • CASS 6: Custody asset rules (if holding client assets)
  • SM&CR: Senior Manager pre-approval, Certification Regime, Conduct Rules
  • MLRO appointment and annual MLRO report to the Board
  • Annual ICARA (Internal Capital Adequacy and Risk Assessment) process
Application Process

Step-by-Step FCA Application

1
UK Company Incorporation & Pre-Application Preparation
Incorporate a UK limited company. Appoint UK-resident Senior Managers (CEO, Compliance Oversight, MLRO). Engage FCA-specialist counsel. Conduct pre-application meetings with FCA if needed for novel/complex business models. Prepare initial capital deposit evidence.
Months 1–3
2
Connect Application Submission
Submit the complete application via FCA Connect portal. Includes Regulatory Business Plan, financial projections, systems and controls description, SM&CR Senior Manager applications (Form A) for all designated Senior Managers, and due diligence on all beneficial owners (Controllers Questionnaire).
Month 3–4
3
FCA Case Officer Review
FCA assigns a case officer who reviews the application. Expect 3–6 rounds of detailed queries covering business model viability, financial crime risk, client onboarding processes, IT security, and systems resilience. Each query round may take 4–8 weeks to address comprehensively.
Months 4–14
4
Onsite Visit / Interview (if required)
FCA may conduct an onsite visit to verify office substance and interview Senior Managers on their knowledge of regulatory requirements. Senior Managers must demonstrate genuine understanding of their specific SMCR responsibilities and the firm's compliance framework.
Month 10–15
5
Authorization Decision
FCA issues a Decision Notice granting or refusing authorization. If granted, the firm is added to the FCA Register. Authorized firms may commence regulated activity immediately but must complete any outstanding pre-trading conditions specified by the FCA.
Month 12–18

Firms transmitting and executing orders without dealing on own account may qualify for the lower £125,000 threshold under certain conditions. Most retail forex brokers are classified as dealing on own account and therefore require £730,000 or must demonstrate equivalent capital adequacy under ICARA.

The FCA targets a 12-month statutory processing period from receipt of a complete application. In practice, complex applications from first-time applicants can take 15–18 months. The FCA's Authorisation Division is known for detailed due diligence and extensive queries on business models, financial crime frameworks, and governance arrangements.
The Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SM&CR) applies to all FCA-authorized firms including forex brokers. SM&CR requires firms to identify Senior Managers (Directors, CEO, CFO, Compliance Oversight, MLRO) who are individually accountable to the FCA, obtain FCA pre-approval for all Senior Manager appointments, and certify annually that Certified Functions staff are fit and proper.
FCA annual fees for forex dealers range from approximately £5,000 for very small firms to £100,000+ for large brokers, calculated based on annual income (tariff data). Additional ongoing costs include FSCS levies, Financial Ombudsman Service levies, and Periodic Fees. Total regulatory levy for a mid-sized forex broker: £20,000–£50,000 per year, plus internal compliance costs of £200,000–£500,000+.
The FCA application fee ranges from GBP 5,000 to GBP 10,000 depending on your firm type, plus legal and compliance setup costs typically between GBP 30,000 and GBP 80,000. You should also budget for initial capital requirements (CASS rules require segregated client funds) and professional advisors, bringing total first-year costs to approximately GBP 100,000 to GBP 200,000. This excludes ongoing operational expenses and annual fees.
FCA-regulated forex brokers must hold client funds in segregated bank accounts separate from operational funds under the Client Assets sourcebook (CASS rules). Your bank must be an FCA-approved institution or equivalent in another jurisdiction, and you must maintain detailed reconciliation records audited annually by an external auditor approved by the FCA. Many UK banks have tightened policies on forex clients since 2024, so securing banking arrangements before applying is advisable.
The FCA license provides greater regulatory prestige and access to UK institutional clients but involves higher compliance costs and stricter leverage rules (maximum 30:1 retail, 500:1 professional). CySEC licenses offer lower operating costs and higher leverage allowances but less market credibility post-2026 and limited access to UK clients without additional FCA permissions. Your choice depends on target client base, capital availability, and operational complexity tolerance.
Required documents include a detailed business plan, organizational charts, compliance policy manual, financial crime risk assessments, shareholder/beneficial ownership declarations (with background checks), proof of adequate capital, banking arrangements, and CVs of key senior management. The FCA also requires evidence of conflict-of-interest policies, complaints procedures, and fit-and-proper assessments for all approved persons under SM&CR rules. Expect to prepare 200+ pages of documentation before submission.
A UK FCA-licensed firm pays UK corporation tax on UK-sourced income (currently 25% as of 2026) unless structured as a Swiss subsidiary, which may qualify for favorable Swiss cantonal rates in Zug (approximately 12-14% combined). You must report to both UK tax authorities and Swiss cantonal authorities depending on your entity structure, and comply with international tax reporting standards like CRS and FATCA. Professional tax structuring through a Swiss advisor is essential to minimize double taxation.
If rejected, the FCA provides written reasons for refusal, typically citing inadequate capital, poor compliance frameworks, or unsuitable senior management. You can reapply after addressing specific deficiencies, but the reapplication process takes 6-12 months and incurs additional fees of GBP 2,000 to GBP 5,000. Repeated rejections may flag your firm to the FCA, making future applications more difficult, so addressing core issues before resubmission is critical.
Annual obligations include submitting financial returns (XBRL format) quarterly, conducting annual audits, maintaining fit-and-proper assessments for approved persons, publishing annual compliance reports, and conducting regular stress testing and capital adequacy reviews. You must also undergo periodic FCA supervision visits, respond to regulatory questionnaires, and maintain updated policies on pricing, conflicts of interest, and operational resilience. Non-compliance can result in fines up to GBP 7 million or license suspension.
