Overview

Serbia's Regulatory Landscape: EU-Aligned Investment Firm Supervision

Serbia is an official EU accession candidate country, having submitted its application in 2009 and opened accession negotiations in 2014. This EU candidate status is not merely diplomatic — it has driven substantial regulatory convergence with EU financial services law. Serbia's Capital Market Law (Zakon o tržištu kapitala) was substantially revised in 2011 and further updated to align with MiFID II principles, making Serbia's investment firm regulatory framework one of the most EU-compatible among non-EU Balkan states.

Investment firm regulation in Serbia involves two authorities: the National Bank of Serbia (NBS — Narodna banka Srbije) oversees banking, payment services, and certain financial entities, while the Serbian Securities Commission (Komisija za hartije od vrednosti) is the primary licensing authority for investment firms. The Commission operates under the Capital Market Law and issues licenses for investment services including forex dealing, portfolio management, and investment advice.

Belgrade has emerged as a significant financial and technology hub in the Western Balkans. Major international banks (Erste, UniCredit, Intesa Sanpaolo, Raiffeisen) operate in Serbia, providing the banking infrastructure necessary for licensed investment firm operations. Serbia's IT and fintech sectors are robust, with Novi Sad recognized as a significant European tech hub.

EU Accession Progress

Serbia has opened 22 of 35 EU accession negotiation chapters. Chapter 9 (Financial Services) is one of the key harmonization areas, requiring Serbia to implement EU financial services legislation as a condition of accession. This means that firms licensed in Serbia today are operating in an environment that is actively converging toward full EU regulatory status — providing strategic optionality as accession progresses.

Why Serbia

The Case for Serbia: Cost, Talent, and Balkan Gateway

Serbia presents a compelling value proposition for brokers whose primary target markets are in the Western Balkans, Southeast Europe, or the broader non-EU region. The country's combination of EU-aligned regulation, low operating costs, strong talent pool, and 15% corporate tax creates a jurisdiction that punches above its weight.

  • Operating costs significantly lower than any EU member state — Belgrade office costs, professional salaries, and compliance costs run at 40–60% of Cyprus equivalents
  • Serbian universities produce strong graduates in finance, economics, and technology — Belgrade and Novi Sad universities have established finance faculties
  • Extensive double tax treaty network with over 60 countries, including most EU member states and key non-EU markets
  • Stable banking infrastructure with major international bank presence providing multicurrency clearing and settlement
  • Proximity to EU markets provides practical market intelligence and talent connections
  • EU accession trajectory means regulatory standards will only improve over time — licenses issued under current framework will convert to EU-standard licenses upon accession
  • Western Balkan regional market of ~18 million people, growing retail investor base, limited domestic broker competition

Step-by-Step: Serbia Securities Commission License

1

Pre-Application Consultation

Engage a Serbia-qualified lawyer to review the proposed business model against current Capital Market Law requirements. Submit informal inquiries to the Securities Commission regarding documentation expectations and processing timelines.

2–4 weeks
2

Incorporate Serbian Company

Register a DOO (d.o.o. — limited liability company) or AD (joint-stock company) with the Serbian Business Registers Agency (APR). Deposit minimum capital in a Serbian bank. Appoint local director(s) meeting Securities Commission fit-and-proper requirements.

1–2 weeks
3

Prepare License Application Package

Compile documentation: business plan, financial projections, internal controls, AML/CFT program, IT system descriptions, risk management framework, officer CVs and background certifications (apostilled from home jurisdictions), shareholder documentation, and UBO declarations.

4–8 weeks
4

Submit to Securities Commission

File the complete license application with the Serbian Securities Commission. Pay application processing fees. The Commission acknowledges receipt and begins formal review. All documentation must be in Serbian or with certified Serbian translations.

Submission event
5

Commission Review and Due Diligence

The Commission conducts a detailed review, submitting information requests. Officers may be invited for interviews. The Commission coordinates with the NBS on banking-related aspects of the application. Decision typically within 3–5 months of a complete application.

3–5 months
6

License Granted — Commence Operations

Investment firm license issued and published in the Securities Commission's official register. Establish banking relationships, connect liquidity providers, implement AML platform, and onboard first clients. Begin quarterly regulatory reporting obligations.
