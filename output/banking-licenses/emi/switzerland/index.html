Overview

Swiss FinTech Licence & Banking Act Article 1b

Switzerland introduced the FinTech licence category through an amendment to the Banking Act (Bankengesetz, BA) that came into effect on 1 January 2019. Article 1b of the BA creates a distinct licence tier below the full banking licence, specifically designed for innovative financial technology firms that accept public money but do not operate as lenders.

The core principle is simple: a FinTech licence holder can accept deposits from the public up to a maximum of CHF 100 million in aggregate, but these deposits must not be invested or used for lending. The funds must remain in liquid, segregated form. This makes the licence appropriate for payment intermediaries, crypto custodians (for fiat), and digital wallet providers that hold customer balances — but not for firms seeking to generate yield on deposited funds.

The FINMA FinTech licence has attracted significant interest from crypto and payment firms because: (1) the minimum capital requirement is only CHF 300,000 — far below the CHF 10 million required for a full Swiss banking licence; (2) FINMA is a respected, internationally recognised regulator; (3) Switzerland's DLT Act (Distributed Ledger Technology Act), in force since 2021, provides comprehensive crypto-asset legal infrastructure; and (4) the Zug/Zurich corridor has the world's densest concentration of blockchain expertise, legal counsel, and institutional infrastructure.

Switzerland is not an EU member, so the FINMA FinTech licence does not provide EU passporting rights. Firms requiring EU access need a separate EU licence (e.g., Lithuanian or Estonian EMI). The Swiss FinTech licence is most powerful when paired with EU or UK authorisation in a dual-entity structure, or when the firm's clients are primarily non-EU (crypto-native firms serving institutional or international clients).

FINMA also operates a regulatory sandbox: firms can accept up to CHF 1,000,000 from the public without any FINMA authorisation, provided funds are not invested. This allows early-stage fintech firms to test concepts without formal licensing — a valuable MVP pathway before committing to the full FinTech licence process.

Crypto Valley

Zug, Zurich & the Swiss Crypto Ecosystem

The canton of Zug (30 minutes from Zurich) is home to the Ethereum Foundation, Cardano's IOHK, Polkadot's Web3 Foundation, and hundreds of other blockchain companies. This concentration of talent, legal expertise, and institutional infrastructure means that crypto payment firms based in Switzerland have unparalleled access to counterparts, banks with crypto capability, and specialised counsel.

Swiss banks with meaningful crypto capabilities include SEBA Bank, Sygnum, and several cantonal banks that accept crypto-related clients. This is a critical operational factor — many international EMIs struggle to find banking partners. In Switzerland, the banking ecosystem has adapted to the reality of crypto business.

The Swiss DLT Act (effective February 2021) introduced a new category of DLT trading facility, updated insolvency law to protect crypto asset segregation, and created a legal framework for tokenised securities. This legal clarity further strengthens Switzerland's position as the jurisdiction of choice for crypto-native financial institutions.

Important: For crypto asset handling (exchange, custody, transfer of crypto), the FinTech licence alone is typically insufficient. Most crypto firms also register with a FINMA-approved Self-Regulatory Organisation (SRO) under the AMLA. The combination of FinTech licence + SRO membership covers both fiat and crypto payment activities.

Process

Step-by-Step FINMA FinTech Application

1
Incorporate Swiss Entity
Establish a Swiss AG (Aktiengesellschaft) or GmbH in a suitable canton (Zug or Zurich preferred for ecosystem access). Articles of association must specify financial activities. Minimum share capital: CHF 100,000 for AG; CHF 20,000 for GmbH (but FinTech licence requires at least CHF 300,000 paid-up capital).
2–4 weeks
2
Engage SRO (AMLA Compliance)
For crypto activities, engage a FINMA-approved Self-Regulatory Organisation (SRO). Major SROs include VQF, PolyReg, and OAD FCT. SRO membership satisfies AMLA obligations as a financial intermediary and is required before FINMA will grant certain licences.
4–8 weeks
3
Prepare FINMA Application
Prepare comprehensive application: business model description, financial projections, organisational regulations (internal rules), AML/KYC concept, technology/IT security concept, draft governance documents, and personal questionnaires for qualifying shareholders and managers.
8–16 weeks
4
Pre-filing Meeting with FINMA
FINMA generally accepts pre-filing meetings for innovative business models. This is strongly recommended to validate that the proposed structure falls within the FinTech licence scope and to identify potential issues before formal filing.
Month 2–3
5
Submit to FINMA & Pay Fee
File formal application with FINMA. Application fees are based on FINMA's tariff regulation (typically CHF 5,000–30,000 depending on complexity). FINMA will review completeness and proceed to substantive assessment.
Month 4–5
6
FINMA Grant & Operational Launch
Upon FINMA approval, receive FinTech licence. Commence accepting public deposits up to CHF 100M aggregate. Maintain quarterly reporting to FINMA. Engage licensed audit firm for annual FinTech licence audit.

The total cost typically ranges from CHF 80,000 to CHF 150,000, including application fees to FINMA (approximately CHF 5,000–CHF 10,000), legal and compliance consulting (CHF 40,000–CHF 80,000), and operational setup costs. Ongoing compliance and audit costs add CHF 20,000–CHF 40,000 annually. Costs vary significantly based on business complexity and the consulting firm engaged.
The standard approval timeline is 4–8 months from submission, though complex applications or those requiring additional FINMA clarification can extend to 12 months. The timeline begins after your application is formally accepted as complete; pre-application preparation typically takes 2–4 months. Responsiveness to FINMA requests significantly impacts the total duration.
FINMA requires at least one member of the board of directors to be a Swiss resident with professional financial experience and clean regulatory history. The board must have documented risk management policies, internal audit functions, and compliance oversight. Beneficial owners must be identified, and fit-and-proper assessments are conducted on all board members and senior management.
While the license is issued under Swiss jurisdiction, you do not need to physically reside in Switzerland. However, you must maintain a registered office address in Switzerland and have at least one board member or designated compliance officer based there. Remote management is permitted provided FINMA requirements for governance, oversight, and regulatory communication are met.
You must establish a banking relationship with a Swiss-regulated bank or other FINMA-approved financial institution to hold customer deposits and operate settlement accounts. As of 2026, this remains challenging for crypto-adjacent businesses, though banks like Sygnum, Maerki Baumann, and others specializing in fintech partnerships are available. Banking due diligence typically takes 2–4 months and requires detailed AML/CFT documentation.
Licensed EMIs must comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) requirements under the Swiss Money Laundering Act, maintain minimum capital reserves, conduct annual external audits by FINMA-approved auditors, file quarterly regulatory reports, and implement documented governance and risk management systems. FINMA conducts periodic on-site inspections, typically every 2–3 years, and can impose sanctions for non-compliance.
A Swiss EMI is required to operate legally in Switzerland and serves the Swiss domestic market directly; however, it does not grant EU passporting rights. A Lithuania or Estonia EMI gives EU-wide access under PSD2 but does not authorize operations in Switzerland. Many fintech firms obtain both licenses to serve both markets, or choose Switzerland exclusively if focused on the Swiss/APAC markets.
A FinTech licence covers fiat deposit acceptance. Crypto asset handling may require separate FINMA authorisation. Many Swiss crypto payment firms combine a FinTech licence for fiat deposits with SRO membership for crypto activities. Legal counsel is essential to structure the correct authorisation.
